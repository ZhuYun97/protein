","page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"","page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"Citrate synthase (CS, EC 2.3.3.1) is a rate-limiting enzyme of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle and is present in most organisms, from bacteria to animals, and catalyzes the first step in the TCA cycle that produces citrate and CoA from acetyl-CoA and oxaloacetate. Type-I CS found in eukaryotes and archaea, and Gram-positive bacteria differ from type-II CS found in Gram-negative bacteria in their biochemical properties (Nguyen et al. 2001). Type-I CS is dimeric and type-II CS is hexameric, and type-II CS usually has additional residues at the N-terminus compared to type-I CS, with monomers of both types exhibiting $35\\mathrm{-}40\\%$ sequence similarity (Park et al. 2021). Phylogenetic analysis using amino acid sequences of CS from various organisms suggests that the eukaryotic ancestor possessed CS gene before the endosymbiosis and CS family genes are transferred from mitochondrial ancestors to ancient eukaryotic cells (Cavalcanti et al. 2014). The phylogenetic analysis comparing CS genes also suggests that the CSs of the Grampositive bacteria Corynebacterium glutamicum and Bacillus coagulans diverged earlier than that of Gram-negative bacteria such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Escherichia coli (Roux et al. 1997). CSs of some Gram-negative bacteria are strongly inhibited by NADH by an allosteric mechanism and activated by KCl (Chen et al. 2011; Park et al. 2021). Allosteric inhibition of type-II CS by NADH is thought to be acquired based on a type-I CS during evolution (Nguyen et al. 2001; Stokell et al. 2003). NADH, ATP, and citrate have been reported as the major inhibitors of CS (Ge et al. 2015; Lee and Kim 2019). Genetic manipulation of CS in plants and bacteria increases stress tolerance for aluminium and citrate production (Anoop et al. 2003; Buch et al. 2009). Amphibian sperm-specific CS may function both as an enzyme in mitochondria and as a $\\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ inducer in the egg cytoplasm (Iwao 2012). In C. merolae, the expression of genes related to the TCA cycle is selectively activated compared to genes involved in the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway and the Calvin-Benson cycle (Moriyama et al. 2015). The genome of $C_{\\cdot}$ . merolae encodes four citrate synthase genes, but only CS4 (CMQ191C) has been found to localize to the mitochondria (Moriyama et al. 2014). It is also known that the mRNA encoding CS4 is downregulated at night in $C.$ . merolae (Miyagishima et al. 2019). CS-1, CS-2, and CS-3 of $C.$ . merolae localize to peroxisomes (Moriyama et al. 2014). These three CSs may convert acetyl-CoA produced by $\\upbeta$ -oxidation of fatty acids to citrate, and the resultant citrate may be transported to the mitochondria (Moriyama et al. 2014). Thus, in C. merolae, studies on the localization and gene expression of TCA cycle enzymes, including CS, have been conducted; however, biochemical analysis of the TCA cycle enzymes is less performed. In C. merolae, carbon fluxes have not been studied, whereas concerning the carbon flux of Synechocystis, the carbon flux of the TCA cycle has always been found to be low under photoautotrophic, photomixotrophic, and heterotrophic conditions (You et al. 2014, 2015; Nakajima et al. 2014; Wan et al. 2017).
