Introduction ","text_level":1,"page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"Structural topology optimization has attracted much attention from both scientific and engineering communities due to its important applications in multidisciplinary design [1]. In the past decades, several optimization techniques have been developed, including the solid isotropic material with penalization (SIMP) [2-4], the bi-directional evolutionary structural optimization (BESO) [5,6], the level set [7-9], and the moving morphable components (MMC) [10,11], which have been widely used in the design of, for examples, vehicles, aircrafts, buildings, and engineering structures [12- 15]. Using these techniques, designers are able to find the optimized material layouts that can maximize the structural performance and simultaneously satisfy complex constraints on, e.g., stress, displacement, natural frequency, material microstructures, and characteristic sizes [16-18]. ","page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"","page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"In recent years, the rapid development of computer hardware has enabled large-scale structural analysis. Discretizing a structure using finer meshes allows us to describe its geometric details more precisely. However, high time cost, low computational efficiency, and huge CPU/memory usage of conventional optimization techniques still hinder their applications in high-resolution structural design. Therefore, increasing effort has been directed toward improving the efficiency of the optimization process [19-21]. For instance, the widely used method of moving asymptotes (MMA) was parallelized, and implemented in $\\mathrm{C}{+}{+}$ [22] and PETSc [23], respectively. Based on such a parallel framework, Aage et al. [24] proposed a powerful computational morphogenesis tool, which realized an optimized design of airplane wing with more than one-billion voxels. In addition to the CPU-based ones, several GPU-based parallel strategies were proposed to accelerate the optimization process [25-27].
