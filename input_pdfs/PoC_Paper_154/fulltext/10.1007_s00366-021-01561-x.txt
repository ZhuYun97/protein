The pioneer, John Nash [22] presented the core concept, hereafter called Nash equilibrium, of non-cooperative game. In the following decades, game theory has been broadly introduced in economics [23], supply chain [24], mechanical system control [25, 26], and engineering [27, 28]. In the Nash game, each player exactly grasps the game framework, including cost function and strategy. Players seek to maximize their benefit through optimizing their cost function and estimating the rivals’ strategies. Therefore, the premise of the Nash game is that players can accurately evaluate the cost functions of themselves and their rivals. Nevertheless, uncertainty, such as material, external load, and boundary condition, is widespread and inevitable in the real world. Small parameter uncertainties may cause considerable uncertainty of the entire system [29]. The most challenging step of the robust Nash problem is to determine the worstcase scenario of the cost function under uncertainty. To attack this, affine arithmetic is first introduced into the robust Nash equilibrium problem. By rewriting the uncertain cost function into affine form, the worst-case scenario can be directly obtained without requiring expensive computational time, whereby the most time-consuming double-loop optimization is avoided. However, the affine arithmetic can only deal with the cost function with explicit expressions. To overcome this drawback, the Chebyshev polynomial expansion is adopted to convert the original cost function into a group of explicit polynomials. By combining the affine arithmetic and Chebyshev polynomial expansion, the worst-case scenario of the cost function for EMB can be directly obtained. Afterward, the robust Nash equilibrium of EMB can be solved conveniently.
