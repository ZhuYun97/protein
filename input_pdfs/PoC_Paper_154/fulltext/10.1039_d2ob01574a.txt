","page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"The disulfide bond is known to be cleaved by nucleophiles such as hydrides,1 thiolates,2 and organometals.3 Concurrently, it indicates that lone pairs of the disulfide bond are relatively low in energy to be nucleophilic. ","page_idx":0},{"type":"equation","text":"$$\n\\Delta E={\\frac{2(c_{\\mathrm{m}})^{2}(c_{\\mathrm{n}})^{2}\\beta_{\\mathrm{mn}}}{E_{\\mathrm{m}}-E_{n}}}\n$$","text_format":"latex","page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"where $c_{\\mathrm{m}}$ and $c_{\\mathrm{n}}$ are coefficients of atomic orbitals in the molecular orbital $m$ and $n$ , respectively, $\\beta_{\\mathrm{mn}}$ is the resonance integral, and $E_{\\mathrm{m}}$ and $E_{\\mathrm{n}}$ are energies of the molecular orbital m and n, respectively. ","page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"In this study, we performed the synthesis of thiocarbamates directly from disulfide bonds and isocyanates. A variety of synthetic methods9 of thiocarbamates have been extensively studied because they have attracted much attention as biologically active molecules.10 However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no synthetic method of thiocarbamates directly from disulfide bonds. Thus, insights into the nucleophilicity of disulfide bonds would provide a new synthetic route toward sulfur-containing compounds. Herein, we report neighboring group participation by disulfide bonds to produce cyclic thiocarbamates and the computational study.
