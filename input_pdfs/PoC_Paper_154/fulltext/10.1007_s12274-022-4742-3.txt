In traditional data acquisition methods, experimental signals are usually recorded in the time domain. The data quality of experimental signals depends on their cumulative counts. ","page_idx":2},{"type":"text","text":"When the counting time cannot be further increased due to the requirement of time resolution, how to keep the raw experimental data with a high signal-to-noise ratio and a good time resolution is an unavoidable problem. In this study, a new method of data measurement is adopted, in which the experimental data will be collected in the frequency domain instead of the time domain. To better understand this new sampling scheme in frequency domain, the experimental signal could be treated as a constant if the sampling frequency is high enough. For a variable that is relatively constant in a certain time scale, it is possible to increase its signal-to-noise ratio to a certain extent by increasing the number of repeated measurements and the number of signal readouts to reach a high statistic. In this way, the counting time for each data point and the time resolution could be shortened as compared with the conventional sampling scheme. This unconventional data collection method here is named as highfrequency sampling scheme. In this SAXS/XRD/XAFS combined technique, a high-speed counter card (NI 9223) purchased from National Instruments (NI) was used in the high-frequency sampling scheme. Three sampling frequencies of 1, 10, and $100\\mathrm{kHz}$ can be chosen, which are corresponding to the ratios of signal-to-noise $(N/\\Delta N=\\sqrt{N})$ )of about 30,100, and 300.Evidently, the higher of the sampling frequency, the better of the data statistic. It has to be said that the variable under measurement is not really a constant, but approximately constant for a short period of time. If the fluctuation frequency of experimental signal is higher than or close to the sampling frequency, the high frequency sampling scheme in frequency domain has no obvious advantage as compared with the conventional sampling scheme in time domain.
