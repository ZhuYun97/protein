","page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"INTRODUCTION ","text_level":1,"page_idx":0},{"type":"text","text":"Taxonomists, systematists and evolutionary biologists are interested in understanding which morphological traits differ among taxa, what drives this variation and, ultimately, how traits change over time. In cryptic species, morphological traits can evolve via slow divergence or convergence between distantly related taxa (Struck et al., 2018). Although several definitions have been proposed for adaptive radiation (reviewed by Rundell & Price, 2009; Glor, 2010), they typically share the notion of rapid changes in traits as a response to environmental gradients or key innovations in traits that enable organisms to cope with new environmental conditions. Nevertheless, the identification of such modes of trait evolution is hindered by some traits evolving in a coordinated way, whereas others change independently. The former process relates to trait integration whereby genetic, functional, environmental and developmental constraints cause a high degree of covariation among all traits (Klingenberg, 2014). Conversely, the latter applies when covariance concentrates within a module of traits (e.g. an organ) and is low between modules, implying that distinct subsets of traits can respond differently to the same selective regime (Klingenberg, 2014; Evans et al., 2021). Studies focusing on a wide range of traits are thus necessary to assess correlation among traits in morphological evolution. Otherwise, the conclusion regarding the biological question of interest can be affected as one group of traits might evolve slowly, while another group could show adaptive trait changes. Moreover, shell shape is highly correlated with environmental factors (McClain et al., 2004; Schreiber et al., 2012; Stelbrink et al., 2020). This last point has received considerable attention in the context of evolutionary radiations. Rapid cladogenesis within clades presenting strikingly distinct shell and radulamorphologieshasservedasevidenceforadaptive radiation of lacustrine gastropods (Glaubrecht & von Rintelen, 2008; von Rintelen et al., 2010b). In contrast, gastropod taxa living in flowing water are considered iconic examples of non-adaptive radiations, also showing a burst of cladogenesis but without adaptive divergence in morphology (Davis, 1993; Wilke et al., 2002, 2010; Clark et al., 2003). Non-adaptive trait divergence does not necessarily imply low variation among species. Under a drift-like (i.e. non-adaptive) mode of trait evolution, variance accumulates over time, and trait divergence is thus the product of the time that has passed since a speciation event and the pace of morphological evolution (quantified here as the Brownian motion rate, $\\mathrm{{\\bf{U}}^{2}}$ ; Felsenstein, 1985). Therefore, a low rate can result in little trait variance over time and among species (although they speciate rapidly), an evolutionary pattern referred to as morphostatic radiation (Davis, 1993). However, this low rate is not the only way to generate a morphostatic trait pattern. Alternatively, evolution constrained toward an optimal trait value, which is shared by all lineages and constant over time, causes stasis in trait variation over time, along with scarce deviation from the single optimum across species (Butler & King, 2004). Although the term morphostatic radiation is sometimes used synonymously with non-adaptive radiation (e.g. Wilke et al., 2010), especially given that they share a pattern of rapid speciation, they differ in either the rate (low vs. high) or mode (constrained vs. non-adaptive) of trait evolution. The ‘rubber band parameter’, $\\mathrm{\\bf{a}}$ , of the OU model determines that traits deviating greatly from the morphological optimum, θ, are pulled more strongly back toward the optimum than the less deviated traits. With an increase in α, species differ less in their traits over time, and phylogenetic relatedness becomes less informative of trait similarity, generating a morphostatic pattern. Radular traits showed the least trait integration (Fig. 3B), and we can therefore rule out the possibility that their evolution is influenced by the other traits examined here. This would limit the potential variation in radular traits and, together with the identified evolution toward a single optimum, could (but does not) result in a morphostatic pattern (as revealed for the shell). Likewise, the evolutionary changes detected in the ctenidium and osphradium were not correlated with changes in shell dimensions (Supporting Information, Fig. S10A), which contradicts the assumption that a reduction in the ctenidium is associated with body size (Hershler & Holsinger, 1990). However, this relationship requires further investigation given the similar body sizes of Radomaniola species attributable to morphostatic evolution.
