3D‑QSAR, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics analysis of dihydrodiazaindolone derivatives as PARP‑1 inhibitors

Abstract
Context  PARP-1 plays an important role in DNA repair and apoptosis, and PARP-1 inhibitors have shown to be effective in the treatment of several malignancies. To evaluate the function of new PARP-1 inhibitors as anticancer adjuvant medicines, 3D-QSAR, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics (MD) simulations of a sequence of dihydrodiazepinoindolone derivatives PARP-1 inhibitors were undertaken in this study.
Methods  In this paper, 43 PARP-1 inhibitors were studied in a three-dimensional quantitative structure-activity relationship (3D-QSAR) using comparative molecular field analysis (CoMFA) and comparative molecular similarity index analysis (CoMSIA). CoMFA with q2 of 0.675 and r2 of 0.981 was achieved, as was CoMSIA with q2 of 0.755 and r2 of 0.992. The changed areas of these compounds are shown by steric, electrostatic, hydrophobic, and hydrogen-bonded acceptor field contour maps. Subsequently, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics simulations further confirmed that key residues Gly863 and $\mathsf{S e r904}$ of PARP-1 are vital residues for protein interactions and their binding affinity. The effects of 3D-QSAR, molecular docking and molecular dynamics simulations supply a new route for the search of new PARP-1 inhibitors. Finally, we designed eight new compounds with exact activity and ADME/T properties.

Introduction
Polyadenylate diphosphate ribosyltransferase (PARP) is an enzyme extensively worried in the process of DNA injury repair. PARP inhibitors can promote apoptosis of tumor cells by inhibiting PARP function in tumor cells and blocking the repair of DNA damage in the cells themselves to exert antitumor effects. PARP inhibitors alone use synthetic lethal effects to selectively kill tumor cells with defects in homologous recombination repair (particularly BRCA1 1/2 mutations) [1]. In addition, in combination with other antitumor therapies, PARP inhibitors can enhance the efficacy of anticancer drugs that damage tumor cell DNA (such as alkylating agents, topoisomerase inhibitors, and platinum-based chemotherapeutic agents) as well as radiotherapy while maintaining safety. In recent years, it has been combined with VEGF inhibitors, programmed death receptor 1 (PD1)/programmed death receptor ligand 1 (PD-L1) inhibitors, and other novel targeted anti-cancer drugs, expanding the clinical scope of application [2–5].
PARP inhibitors mainly act on PARP-1 and prevent the repair of cellular DNA damage by two main mechanisms: first, PARP inhibitors directly inhibit the enzymatic activity of PARP-1. When DNA damage occurs, PARP1/2 acts as a DNA damage sensor, and its zinc finger structure in the DNA-binding region binds to the damaged DNA, resulting in a conformational change that inhibits the binding of the cofactor nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide $(\mathrm{NAD+})$ to ADP-ribosyl transferase (ART) and activates the enzymatic activity of PARP, cleaving ${\mathrm{NAD}}+$ to nicotinamide and ADPribose. PARP inhibitors interact with the ${\mathrm{NAD}}+$ binding site in the PARP1/2 catalytic domain, leading to the failure of DNA damage repair by inhibiting enzymatic activity [6–8]. At the same time, PARP inhibitors prevent PARP-1 from shedding from the DNA binding site and PARP itself, “trapping” PARP at the site of damage [9]. DNA repair proteins are unable to bind at the DNA nick site to complete the repair process, and PARP-mediated SSB restoration cannot be accomplished and is transformed into a double-strand wreck (DSB) after the subsequent replication cycle. The repair of DNA DSBs is mainly accomplished through the
HRR pathway, and the DNA duplexes repaired by HRR have a high degree of reduction. If mutations occur in key factors in the HRR pathway, such as BRCA1/2, and cells cannot effectively initiate HRR repair, they select techniques such as non-homologous to give up becoming a member of (NHEJ) for repair. DNA restoration in these methods is now not homologous, and DNA sequences are regularly incorrectly repaired, leading to genomic instability and eventual mobile phone death [10–13]. As can be seen above, the absence of cellular HRR repair results in the inability to complete cellular DNA damage properly. The combined effect of PARP inhibition and HRR deficiency leads to tumor cell death, which is known as the “synthetic lethal” effect.
Currently, 4 PARP inhibitors have been clinically accredited for advertising and marketing by means of the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and/or the European Medicines Agency (EMA), specifically olaparib [14], rucaparib [15], niraparib [16], and talazoparib [17], but very few PARP inhibitors with brain penetration capability. Wang and his colleagues identified a series of thickened tetra- or pentacyclic dihydrodiazepine indolone derivatives with inhibitory effects. While having good antitumor effects, they also have good brain penetrating ability [18]. Therefore, primarily based on the reported structures and activities of dihydrodiazepine indolone derivatives, we have systematically investigated their conformational relationships using 3D-QSAR, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics simulations and designed several novel PARP-1 inhibitors with better inhibitory activities.

Materials and methods

Data sources
In this paper, data were collected from the literature for a series of PARP-1 inhibitors, and the activity data for these compounds were used as dependent variables for the conformational relationship study [18]. In the collected data, the activity values of the compounds are expressed as $\mathrm{IC}_{50}$ .
Here, we need to convert the $\mathrm{IC}_{50}$ values into $\mathrm{pIC}_{50}$ values as the dependent variable in the QSAR study by using the equation $\mathrm{pIC}_{50}{=}-\mathrm{lg}\mathrm{IC}_{50}$ . $\mathrm{pIC}_{50}$ values obtained from the conversion are listed in Table S1.

Structural optimization and superposition
In the process of model construction, molecular superposition is the most critical step, which is generally divided into superposition based on a common backbone, superposition based on pharmacophore, and superposition based on molecular docking [19]. The structural formula of compound 33 is shown in Fig. 1, where the black bolded part is the common backbone of the stacking. Then, all molecules were optimized in SYBYL-X2.0 software using GasteigerHuckel charge, Tripos force field, and Power II energy gradient methods with default values for all parameters except for the maximum optimization limit and convergence criterion. They were set to 10,000 times and $0.005\mathrm{kcal/mol}$ , respectively [20, 21]. Then, the 43 compounds were randomly divided into 33 training sets and 10 test sets according to the variability of their structures and activity ranges, where the training sets were used to construct stable and reliable 3D quantitative conformational relationship models and the test sets were used to verify the reliability of the constructed models. Finally, the models were generated, validated, and analyzed using CoMFA and CoMSIA, respectively.

3D‑QSAR modeling and validation
CoMFA and CoMSIA techniques were used to create 3D-QSAR models. Within the CoMFA concept. The Coulomb function and the Lennard–Jones function are used to compute the contributions of the electrostatic and steric fields, respectively [22]. Additional hydrophobic fields, hydrogen bonding interaction fields, and hydrogen bonding body fields were added to create the CoMSIA model. The leave-one-out (LOO) cross-validation method was used to determine the ideal number of correlation model components $(n)$ and the maximum cross-validated correlation coefficient $(q^{2})$ . Non-cross-validation analysis was used to derive the traditional multiple correlation coefficients $(r^{2})$ , standard errors of estimation (SEE), and Fisher test $(F)$ values [23]. Both $q^{2}$ and $r^{2}$ are used to evaluate QSAR models. $q^{2}$ reflects the internal predictive power of the model, and $r^{2}$ is used to measure the stability of the model. A credible 3D-QSAR model must satisfy the following criteria: $q^{2}>0.5,r^{2}>0.9_{\L}$ , the $F$ -value should be as large as possible, and the See value should be as small as possible to pass internal validation [24]. When designing novel and more potent compounds, the influence of molecular structure on biological activity is investigated using isopotential maps of 3D-QSAR models. These maps serve as a guide for changing compound structures to increase activity.

Molecular docking
Molecular simulations are widely used to elucidate potential interaction patterns between active inhibitors and protein targets [25]. In this work, the Surflex-DOCK module of SYBYL X-2.0 was used to run molecular docking simulations (Tripos, LP). The RCSB Protein Data Bank was used to locate the PARP-1 complex’s X-ray eutectic structure (PDB code: 7CMW). To make proteins into receptors for the docking model, we repaired the deletion and termination residues of the polypeptide chain, took out the water, and inserted hydrogen atoms [26]. The optimal coordination of the crystal ligands was used to screen the compound docking conformations, and their binding mechanisms were investigated. Figure S1, which uses PYMOL software to visualize binding, demonstrates this.

ADME/T property prediction and synthetic availability prediction
The in vivo processes (absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion) and toxicity (ADME/T) of a chemical are crucial indicators of its drug-forming properties in the development of novel medications [27, 28]. In this work, the ADME/T residences of the developed PARP-1 inhibitors could be predicted using the pkCSM online server (http://biosig...Unimelb.edu.au/pkcsm/forecast) and Swissadme online server (SwissADME). A compound’s rate of medication absorption in the human gut can be predicted by web servers, and an absorbance (HIA) of less than $30\%$ is typically regarded as being poorly absorbed. Low VDss is defined as less than $0.71\mathrm{L}/\mathrm{kg}$ , and high VDss is defined as more than $2.81~\mathrm{L/kg}$ . Use the online tool to see if the CYP450 3A4 and 2D6 isoforms or CYP450 inhibitors can also be used to metabolize these compounds. Total clearance was used to calculate the administration rate to reach steady state (logmL $\mathbf{\delta}(\operatorname*{min}\mathbf{\delta}\mathbf{k}\mathbf{g})-1,$ ). The molecule’s potential for toxicity was evaluated using the Ames toxicity/hepatotoxicity/skin sensitization prediction.
We used the online tool SwissADME (http://​www.​swiss adme.​ch/​index.​php) to evaluate the synthetic availability of new compounds [29]. The synthetic accessibility difficulty scale was 1–10, and the smaller the score, the simpler the synthetic route of the compound.

Molecular dynamics (MD) simulation
The molecular dynamics of PARP-1 complexed with ligands were simulated using a simulation model. The ligand and receptor were loaded with the general Amber force field (GAFF), the TIP3PBOX (buffer $\overset{\cdot}{\geq}10.0\overset{\circ}{\mathrm{A}}_{.}^{\cdot}$ ) was used to solventize the entire complex system, and then sodium and chloride ions were added to make the system electrically neutral.
We use the PMEMD.mpi and PMEMD.cuda modules from the AMBER16 bundle for dynamics simulations [30–32]. First, various minimization steps have been carried out on the structures to keep away from feasible spatial collapse. Then, throughout the heating phase, each system was gradually heated from 0 to $300\mathrm{K}$ and maintained at $300\mathrm{K}$ at some stage in the subsequent equilibration and manufacturing phases. The entire manufacturing segment, the heating phase, and the equilibration phase all had 2 fs time increments. The pressure was adjusted to 1 atm and controlled using an anisotropic $(x-,y-$ , and $z\mathrm{-}$ ) strain legislation scheme with a strain rest time of 1 ps. Periodic boundary conditions were utilized to preserve temperature constancy and pressure system synthesis [33]. Using Langevin dynamics and a collision frequency of 2 ps1, the temperature was changed. Longrange electrostatics were handled using the particle mesh Ewald (PME) approach, while real-space interactions were handled using a $\overset{\mathcal{-}}{10}\overset{\mathcal{\circ}}{\mathrm{A}}$ cut-off value. The shake method was used to restrict all covalent connections involving hydrogen atoms. Every system underwent a 100-ns MD simulation, and every 100 ps, the trajectory of the simulated systems was stored [34, 35].
The binding energies of crystal complexes with different ligands were calculated by processing the saved molecular dynamics simulation traces using the MM/GBSA algorithm [36–38]. Results and discussion

3D‑QSAR model analysis
In this study, the CoMFA and CoMSIA approaches were applied to the training set to model 3D-QSAR, and the matching precise models were achieved. By adding together these force fields’ contributions to the models, we may determine the major contributions that they have to the activity. When creating novel chemicals, these contributions should be taken into account.
As shown in Table 1, every parameter of the COMFA model indicates that the model is very stable and predictable. The values of $r^{2}$ , $q^{2}$ , and SEE are 0.675, 0.050, and 0.981, respectively. In space, steric and electrostatic fields makeup, respectively, 69.3 and $30.8\%$ of the volume. This shows that both the steric and electrostatic fields are required. We found many models with high levels of stability and predictability among the 31 combined fields in our investigation of the five CoMSIA regions $(q^{2}>0.50$ , $r^{2}{>}0.90$ , and $F_{\l}$ -value $>100$ ). However, in addition to the main steric and electrostatic fields of the model, the hydrogen bond donor field, hydrogen bond acceptor field, and hydrophobic field also provide important information on the activity. We finally choose the COMSIASEHA model. The values of $r^{2},q^{2}$ , and SEE are 0.992, 0.035, and 0.755, respectively. There are steric and electrostatic fields present in amounts of 15.3 and $37.0\%$ , respectively, as well as hydrogen bond acceptor fields and hydrophobic fields in amounts of 29.4 and $18.3\%$ . Finally, based on the results of residual and linear regression analysis of the experimental (Pki) and predicted (Pki) values in Table S2 and Fig. 2, we found that almost all compounds were close to or located on the trend line with a good linear relationship.

Results and analysis of molecular docking
We used the Surflex-Dock module in SYBYL X2.0 software to re-dock the ligand (BGB-290) in the initial crystal structure to the active center and then calculated the RMSD to be $0.73\mathring{\mathrm{~A~}}$ . The RMSD value is less than $2\mathring{\mathrm{A}}$ , which suggests that the generated docking procedure is reliable. Compound
33 can be utilized as a template to research molecular interactions since, as shown in Table S1, it has the strongest activity. Following that, by examining 3D-QSAR isopotential maps, novel compounds were created.
Figure 4 displays the outcomes of the Surflex docking of compound 33 with 7CMW. Numerous amino acid residues, including Gly863, Ser904, Phe897, Ala898, His909, Tyr907, and His862, surround compound 33. According to earlier research, Gly863, Ser904, and compound 33, all establish tridentate hydrogen bonds with the ligand in the original crystal structure of Fig. 5. Key residues will therefore be crucial assessment criteria for assessing how newly developed chemicals interact with target proteins.

ADME/T and synthetic availability
Table  4 displays the ADME/T characteristics of the newly created compounds. The solubility and lipophilicity of the molecules were evaluated using LogP and LogS, and all eight compounds were within a reasonable range
$(\mathrm{Log}P;-0.7$ to $\mathrm{;Log}S\mathrm{;-}10$ to 6). This shows that all eight compounds have effective absorption characteristics. None of the compounds showed BBB permeability. Some of the new compounds had better log VDSS performance, and they mostly ranged from 0.71 to $2.81\mathrm{L}/\mathrm{kg}$ . In terms of pharmacokinetics, the 3A4 subtype and 2D6 subtype could metabolize all compounds. In addition, all compounds did not inhibit cytochrome $\mathrm{P4502D6}$ , while all compounds could inhibit cytochrome P450 1A2. Some compounds inhibited cytochrome P450 2C19/2C9/3A4 isoforms. Toxicity prediction showed that all new compound molecules had some damaging effect on the human liver but no sensitization to the skin.

Conclusion
In this research, theoretical prediction models were put together, and the binding patterns of a number of newly created dihydrodiazepine indolone derivatives with PARP-1 were explored. This was done using 3D-QSAR modeling, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics simulations. The CoMFA and CoMSIA models had strong internal and external validation as well as a statistically significant connection, demonstrating the models’ potent predictive power. By using molecular docking and molecular dynamics simulations to investigate the binding mode of inhibitors to 7CMW, it was discovered that several important residues significantly contribute to the activity of PARP-1. For instance, Gly863, Ser904, and Tyr896 played a crucial role in recognizing small molecules on 7CMW. Eight new, absolutely designed compounds were further validated by molecular docking and molecular dynamics simulations and may bind to PARP-1 based on theoretical models and molecular docking results. Compound 33a could have a stronger inhibitory impact, according to the free energy analysis and the density flooding hypothesis. These findings establish an accurate prediction model for the rational design of new PARP-1 inhibitors against cancers. It serves as a helpful resource for additional structural alterations of PARP-1 inhibitors.
