Mechanistic insight into the destabilization of p53TD tetramer by cancer-related R337H mutation: a molecular dynamics study†
Yawei Yu, Xuewei Dong, Yiming Tang, Le Li and Guanghong Wei*
The p53 protein is a tumor suppressor crucial for cell cycle and genome integrity. In a very large proportion of human cancers, p53 is frequently inactivated by mutations located in its DNA-binding domain (DBD). Some experimental studies reported that the inherited R337H mutation located in the p53 tetramerization domain (p53TD) can also result in destabilization of the p53 protein, and consequently lead to an organism prone to cancer setup. However, the underlying R337H mutation-induced structural destabilization mechanism is not well understood. Herein, we investigate the structural stability and dynamic property of the wild type p53TD tetramer and its cancer-related R337H mutant by performing multiple microsecond molecular dynamics simulations. It is found that R337H mutation destroys the R337–D352 hydrogen bonds, weakens the F341–F341 \(\pi{-}\pi\) stacking interaction and the hydrophobic interaction between aliphatic hydrocarbons of R337 and M340, leading to more solvent exposure of all the hydrophobic cores, and thus disrupting the structural integrity of the tetramer. Importantly, our simulations show for the first time that R337H mutation results in unfolding of the \(\alpha\)-helix starting from the N-terminal region (residues \(^{335}\mathrm{RER}(\mathrm{H})\mathrm{FEM}^{340}\)). Consistently, community network analyses reveal that R337H mutation reduces dynamical correlation and global connectivity of p53TD tetramer, which destabilizes the structure of the p53TD tetramer. This study provides the atomistic mechanism of R337H mutation-induced destabilization of p53TD tetramer, which might be helpful for in-depth understanding of the p53 loss-of-function mechanism.

Introduction
The human tumor suppressor p53 protein, dubbed the ''guardian of the genome'' and ''policeman of the oncogenes'',1–4 is a key transcription factor. In response to cell stress, p53 is involved in regulating a variety of cellular functions such as DNA damage repair, cell cycle arrest, autophagy and apoptosis.5,6 Full-length p53 is a 393-residue protein comprising four functional domains: N-terminal transactivation domain (p53NTD) (residues 1–93), DNA-binding domain (p53DBD) (residues 94–297), tetramerization domain (p53TD) (residues 326–356) and intrinsically disordered C-terminal domain (p53CTD) (residues 357–393). Many mutants of the p53 protein lose its normal function and cause humans to be susceptible to cancer.7 According to the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IRAC) TP53 Database, more than half of human cancers result from loss-of-function mutations in the
p53 protein,8–11 and around \(95\%\) of these mutations occur in p53DBD.12 Mutations in p53DBD are mostly associated with the Li-Fraumeni familial cancer syndrome which shows an increased risk of breast cancer, soft-tissue sarcomas (STSs) and adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC).13–18 The p53 protein is active, when it is tetrameric, and binds with DNA or interacts with some target proteins.19 Importantly, p53TD is an independent folding domain responsible for p53's tetramerization, and it forms a symmetrical tetramer in the functional form of p53.20,21 Extensive experimental studies showed that the p53TD tetramer is essential for p53–DNA binding, p53 posttranslational modifications, protein–protein interactions and p53 degradation.20–24 Moreover, the p53TD tetramer mediates the shifts between the active and inactivate p53 DNA-binding conformations and ensures that p53 is endowed with its correct conformation.19,21,25,26
There are many cancer-associated mutations in the p53 tetramerization domain, such as F328S, F338I, L344R, L348S, L330P, R337P, R342P, L344P, G334V, G334W, and E346A.26–30 These mutations can lead to a decrease in the structural stability of the p53TD tetramer27,28 or the prevention of tetramer formation.29,30 Among them, R337H is the most frequent germline mutation found inside the p53TD and it is linked to
an increased risk of ACC in humans.\(^{31-36}\) This mutation results in loss-of-function of the p53TD tetramer and consequently leads to an organism prone to ACC setup.\(^{16,37}\) Some studies have suggested that Brazilian patients typically carry the unique R337H mutation, and the incidence of pediatric ACC in southern Brazil is 10–15 times higher than that worldwide.\(^{32,38,39}\)
In vitro experiments revealed that under certain conditions \(\mathrm{pH} \leq 4\) and elevated temperature), isolated p53TD forms a tetramer and its R337H mutant has a higher propensity than the wild type (WT) p53TD tetramer to convert from a soluble native structure to amyloid-like fibrils, and the results provided a possible mechanism for the observed nuclear accumulation of p53 in ACC cells.\(^{40-43}\) Spectroscopic experiments by DiGiammarino et al.\(^{44}\) showed that the formation of a p53TD tetramer is hampered by R337H mutation and the R337H p53TD tetramer adopts a native-like fold but is less stable than the WT p53TD tetramer, leading to the failure of p53 in tumor suppression. Moreover, their circular dichroism experiments showed that the R337H p53TD tetramer unfolds at significantly lower temperatures than the wild type p53TD tetramer at all tested pH values \(\mathrm{pH} 5-9\), indicative of its reduced structural stability. By performing circular dichroism, differential scanning calorimetry and \({}^{1}\mathrm{H}\) saturation transfer difference NMR experiments, Gordo et al.\(^{37}\) found that a synthesized small molecule can strongly enhance the stability of the R337H p53TD tetramer and recover its structural integrity to that of the WT p53TD tetramer. It was reported that the nuclear export signal (NES) of p53 located within p53TD is shielded in the p53TD tetramer in the nucleus,\(^{45}\) and after R337H mutation, the NES domain in non-tetrameric forms (monomer and possibly dimer) of p53TD is exposed, and thus facilitates exportation of p53 to the cytoplasm for proteolysis.\(^{46}\)
Complementary to experiments, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations can provide atomic-level details of p53's conformational dynamics,\(^{47-51}\) p53–MDM2 interactions,\(^{50,52,53}\) p53–DNA interactions,\(^{54-56}\) conformational ensemble of p53 TAD\(^{57,58}\) and the aggregation of p53 segments.\(^{59-62}\) For example, on the basis of 15 ns implicit-solvent MD simulations of the p53TD tetramer, an early study\(^{47}\) reported that four residues R333, E349, R337 and D352 could form a fluid salt-bridging network, which may help stabilize the p53TD tetramer. Our recent 600 ns explicit-solvent MD study\(^{48}\) revealed that two common cancer mutants (R175H and R273H, located in p53DBD) exhibit a larger hydrophobic surface area and higher loop flexibility than wild type p53DBD, and drive the p53 DNA-binding domain towards aggregation-prone conformations. Utilizing a combination of experimental and simulation methods, Zhao et al.\(^{50}\) investigated the interactions between EGCG (a green tea extract) and p53, and found that EGCG binds with the intrinsically disordered N-terminal domain of p53 and disrupts p53–MDM2 interaction, providing insights into the mechanisms for EGCG's anticancer activity. Very recently, we explored the effect of four cancer-related mutations (L145Q, P151S, Y220C, and G266R) located in the \(\beta\)-sandwich region on p53DBD–DNA interactions, and it was found that these mutations allosterically destabilize the structural stability of the p53
DNA-binding groove and disrupt the p53–DNA interactions.\(^{54}\) By using atomistic simulations, Das and Makarov\(^{60}\) characterized the aggregation process of the aggregation-prone p53 fragment \(\left({}^{252}\mathrm{L}\mathrm{T}\mathrm{TITLE}^{258}\right)\). They found that the wild type fragment displays a high propensity to self-assemble into a stable \(\beta\)-sheet-rich structure in the parallely arranged dimeric form, but the I254R mutation destabilizes the dimer, and reduces the aggregation propensity of the p53 fragment. Based on atomistic and coarse-grained MD simulations, Lima et al.\(^{62}\) suggested that a p53DBD peptide \(\left({}^{245}\mathrm{GMNRP}\mathrm{P}\mathrm{L}\mathrm{T}\mathrm{TITLE}\mathrm{ESSGN}^{263}\right)\) can form both antiparallel and parallel \(\beta\)-sheet dimers. These studies provide valuable mechanistic insights into the physiological and pathological function of p53 protein. However, these simulation studies are mainly focused on the p53DBD, TAD, and intrinsically disordered N-terminal domains, and very little computational research on the TD domain has been reported, especially on the molecular mechanism of structural destabilization of the p53TD tetramer induced by the cancer-related R337H mutation.
Herein, we performed all-atom MD simulations on WT p53TD tetramer and its R337H mutant with the aim of understanding how the cancer-related R337H mutation reduces the structural stability of the p53TD tetramer. Our multiple microsecond simulations show that R337H mutation significantly weakens the hydrophobic interaction between aliphatic hydrocarbons of R337 and M340, the \(\pi{-}\pi\) stacking interaction between F341 and F341 and the cation–\(\pi\) interaction between F338 and R342, destabilizes the HP cores of the monomer subunit, dimer subunit and the tetramer, results in the unfolding of the \(\alpha\)-helix (residues \({}^{335}\mathrm{RER}(\mathrm{H})\mathrm{FEM}^{340}\)), and finally disrupts the structural intactness of the p53TD tetramer. Consistently, community network analyses reveal that R337H mutation attenuates the global correlation and destructs native connectivity between communities in the p53TD tetramer. These results provide molecular insights into the R337H mutation-induced destabilization mechanism of the p53TD tetramer, which might be helpful for the prevention and treatment of ACC.

Materials and methods

WT p53TD tetramer and R337H p53TD tetramer systems
Two tetramer systems were simulated and they are the wild type of the p53TD tetramer (referred to as WT-p53Tet hereafter, residues 326–356) and its R337H mutant (referred to as R337H–p53Tet hereafter). The amino acid sequence of p53TD is shown in Fig. 1a. In the functional form of p53, the p53TD forms a symmetrical tetramer (Fig. 1b). The tetramer consists of two identical dimer subunits and each subunit is stabilized by an antiparallel \(\beta\)-sheet and two antiparallel helices (Fig. 1c). The two identical monomers in the dimer subunit are denoted as the monomer subunit (Fig. 1d), which comprises a \(\beta\)-strand, a tight turn and an \(\alpha\)-helix.\(^{63,64}\) There are three hydrophobic (HP) cores in the p53TD tetramer. The initial coordinate of WT-p53Tet was taken from the solution NMR structure (PDB ID: 2J0Z)25 and the R337H mutant was generated by replacing Arg (R) at position 337 with His (H) using Pymol.67 The N- and C-termini of each protein were respectively capped by acetyl \(\left(\mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{CO}\right)\) and amino \(\left(\mathrm{NH}_{2}\right)\) groups, in order to provide comparable systems with those two terminal residues uncharged in full-length p53. To mimic the neutral experimental \(\mathrm{pH}\) condition,25,26,37 the side chains of residues Asp/Glu/His were deprotonated \(\left(\mathrm{Asp}^{-}/\mathrm{Glu}^{-}/\mathrm{His}\right)\), and the side chains of residues Arg and Lys were protonated \(\left(\mathrm{Arg}^{+}\right.\)and \(\left.\mathrm{Lys}^{+}\right)\).

MD simulation details
Five independent all-atom explicit-solvent MD simulations were performed both for WT-p53Tet and R337H–p53Tet systems in the isothermal-isobaric \((N P T)\) ensemble by using the GROMACS-2018.3 software package68 in combination with Charmm36m69 force field. Each MD simulation was run for \(2\,\upmu\mathrm{s}\), resulting in an accumulated simulation time of \(20\,\upmu\mathrm{s}\). Each p53TD tetramer was initially placed in the center of a cubic box filled with TIP3P water,70 and the size of the simulation box is \(7.94\times7.94\times7.94\,\mathrm{nm}^{3}\). Eight and twelve \(\mathrm{Na}^{+}\)ions were used to neutralize respectively WT-p53Tet and R337H–p53Tet systems, and additional NaCl with a physiological concentration of \(0.15\,\mathrm{M}\) was added to each simulation box. Periodic boundary conditions were applied in all simulations. Before MD runs, the energy minimization was first carried out to remove steric clashes using the steepest descent method for 50 000 steps. Subsequently, simulations were performed under an NVT ensemble followed by an NPT (1 bar) ensemble, both for 0.1 ns, to equilibrate the systems. The pressure was kept at 1 bar using the Parrinello–Rahman method,71 and the
temperature was maintained at \(310\,\mathrm{~K~}\) by separately coupling the protein and non-protein groups to an external heat bath using a velocity-rescaling coupling method.72 The neighbor list is updated every 10 steps with a cutoff distance of \(1.4\;\mathrm{nm}\) using a Verlet buffer.73 Constraints were applied to all-bond lengths using the LINCS method74 for proteins and the SETTLE algorithm75 for water molecules, allowing an integration time step of 2 fs. The van der Waals interactions were treated using a cutoff of \(1.4~\mathrm{nm}\), and the electrostatic interactions were calculated using the particle mesh Ewald (PME) method with a real space cutoff of \(1.4~\mathrm{nm}\).76

Analysis methods
Trajectory analyses were carried out using the tools implemented in the GROMACS-2018.3 software package68 and our in-house codes. Several parameters, including all-atom root mean square deviation (RMSD), residue-based root mean square fluctuation (RMSF), radius of gyration \(\left(\mathrm{Rg}\right)\), solvent accessible surface area (SASA) and hydrogen bond (H-bond) number were used to characterize the structural features and conformational properties of WT-p53Tet and R337H–p53Tet. The secondary structure of the proteins was calculated using the Define Secondary Structure of Protein (DSSP) package.77 A H-bond was considered to be formed if the distance between the H-bonding donor D and the acceptor A is \(\leq\,0.35\ \mathrm{nm}\) and the D–H···A angle is \(\geq150^{\circ}\).78 A salt bridge was taken as formed if the minimum distance between the side chain \(\mathrm{COO}^{-}\)group of a negatively charged residue and the \(_\mathrm{N}\) atom of the side chain \(\mathrm{NH}_{2}^{+}/\mathrm{NH}_{3}^{+}\)group of a positively charged residue Arg/Lys is smaller than \(0.4\,\mathrm{nm}\). An atomic contact was defined if the distance between two carbon atoms of nonsequential residues lies within \(0.54\,\mathrm{nm}\) or the distance between any other two atoms of nonsequential residues lies within \(0.46\,\mathrm{nm}\). Two aromatic rings were considered to form \(\pi{-}\pi\) stacking when their centroid distance is within \(0.6\,\mathrm{nm}\).79 The stacking patterns are roughly classified into three categories with a cutoff of \(0.65\,\mathrm{nm}\): parallel \((0{-}30^{\circ})\), herringbone \((30{-}60^{\circ})\), and perpendicular or T-shaped \((60{-}90^{\circ})\).80 Conformations used for principal components analysis for each system were binned into a histogram over the first two principal components (PCs). The histogram was converted into a free energy landscape constructed using the relation \(-RT\ln P(\mathrm{PC}1\), PC2), where \(P\) is the histogram of two selected reaction coordinates PC1 and PC2. Dynamical networks were obtained using codes developed by Fargle and Sethi.81 Each \(\mathrm{C}\alpha\) atom was selected as a representative node for the corresponding residue. Edges were added between two nodes if the corresponding residues are in contact during a majority of the simulation time \((>70\%)\). The decomposition of dynamical networks into communities was conducted using the Girvan–Newman algorithm82 by iteratively removing the edge with the highest betweenness and recalculating the betweenness of all remaining edges until the modularity of the community network was maximized. The last 500 ns simulation trajectories were used for analysis. Unless specified, all the results presented below were obtained using the data from five independent MD runs. The graphical
analyses and structural visualization were performed using the Pymol package.\(^{67}\)

Results and discussion

R337H mutation disrupts the structural integrity of the p53TD tetramer
Firstly, we explored the effects of R337H mutation on the structural stability of the p53TD tetramer by calculating the all-atom RMSD over the five MD runs for each system (WT-p53Tet and R337H–p53Tet) relative to their initial conformations (Fig. 2a). The RMSD value of R337H–p53Tet is larger \((\sim\!0.34\,\mathrm{\nm})\) than that of WT-p53Tet \((\sim\!0.26\,\mathrm{\nm})\), indicating that R337H mutation reduces the structural stability of the p53TD tetramer. In order to identify the regions that lead to large RMSD values in R337H–p53Tet, we calculated the RMSD values of the \(\alpha\)-helix (residues R335–A355) and \(\beta\)-sheet (residues Y327–R333) regions (including four \(\alpha\)-helices and two \(\beta\)-sheets) of the p53TD tetramer (Fig. 2b, c). The RMSD values of the \(\alpha\)-helix region \((\sim\!0.33\,\mathrm{\nm})\) and \(\beta\)-sheet region \((\sim\!0.32\,\mathrm{\nm})\) in the R337H–p53Tet variant are larger than those \((\sim\!0.24\,\mathrm{\nm})\) in WT-p53Tet. These data suggest that both the \(\alpha\)-helix and \(\beta\)-sheet regions contribute to the structural destabilization of R337H–p53Tet. Qualitatively similar results are observed for each individual MD run of the two systems (Fig. S1, ESI†). To further demonstrate the structural destabilization induced by R337H mutation, we compared the total contact number, H-bond number and RMSF of the two tetramers. As shown in Fig. 2d, the total contact number of R337H–p53Tet is smaller than that of WT-p53Tet, consistent with the increase in RMSD
values of R337H–p53Tet in Fig. 2a. It can be seen from the probability density function (PDF) of the H-bond number of the p53TD tetramer that the curves of the total and main chain H-bond number of WT-p53Tet exhibit peaks around 115 and 85, respectively, while those of the R337H mutant have peaks at reduced values (100 and 77) (Fig. S2, ESI†). In addition, we investigated the influence of R337H mutation on the conformational flexibility of p53TD by calculating the residue-based RMSF values of the two tetramers (Fig. S3, ESI†). Compared with WT-p53Tet, two regions of the R337H mutant have relatively larger RMSF values, indicative of increased structural flexibility. Interestingly, those two regions correspond to the \(\alpha\)-helix region (residues R335–M346 and L350–G356) (Fig. S3, ESI†). The gradual disruption of the p53TD tetramer can be manifested from representative snapshots of WT-p53Tet and R337H–p53Tet at four different time points (Fig. 2e). Our data here demonstrate that R337H mutation causes a notable conformational change and disrupts the structural integrity of p53TD tetramer, which can be clearly seen from the snapshots of the wild type and R337H mutation p53TD tetramer at \(t=2.0~\upmu\mathrm{s}\) (Fig. 2e).

In order to elucidate the molecular mechanism of \(\alpha\)-helix unfolding, we calculated the differences in the intrachain pairwise residue contact number between R337H–p53Tet and WT-p53Tet in the \(\alpha\)-helix region (residues 335–355) (Fig. 3c). The residue–residue contact number includes the side chain–side chain (SC–SC) and main chain–main chain (MC–MC) contact number. Upon mutation, three residue pairs show a reduced SC–SC contact number (marked by red rectangles). Specifically, the residue pair R(H)337–M340 located in the mutation site exhibits slightly decreased atomic contacts (rectangle 1), and the other two residue pairs nearby the mutation site, namely F338–R342 (rectangle 2) and R335–E339 (rectangle 3) display significantly diminished atomic contacts. By calculating the contact number between different SC groups \(\left(\mathrm{NH}_{2}^{+}\right.\), \(\left.\mathrm{COO}^{-}\right.\), aliphatic hydrocarbons) of residue pairs R(H)337–M340 and R335–E339 for each MD run, we found that SC aliphatic hydrocarbons are responsible for the decrease in SC–SC contact number (Fig. S7, ESI†), revealing that R337H mutation weakens the hydrophobic interaction of R(H)337–M340 and R335–E339. We then calculated the PDF of the distance between the centroid of the aromatic ring of F338 and the \(\mathrm{NH}_{2}^{+}\)group of R342. There are two peaks in the distance curve of the F338–R342 cation–\(\pi\) residue pair. The dominant peak in WT-
p53Tet is located at \(0.5\,\mathrm{nm}\), while it is centered at \(1.0\,\mathrm{nm}\) in R337H–p53Tet (Fig. 3d), indicating that R337H mutation reduces the F338–R342 cation–\(\pi\) interaction. In the MC–MC contact map, one large region (marked by a yellow rectangle) displays a notable decrease in contact number. To explore whether the decrease in contact number corresponds to the disruption of the MC H-bond, we calculated the PDF of the MC H-bond number and H-bond maps in the \(\alpha\)-helix region. It can be seen that the H-bond number is decreased (Fig. S8a, ESI†) and the H-bonds in the \(\alpha\)-helix region spanning residues \({}^{335}\mathrm{RER}(\mathrm{H})\mathrm{FEMFRE}^{343}\) are broken (Fig. S8b, ESI†). The loss of intrachain MC H-bonds indicates the unfolding of the \(\alpha\)-helix. The unfolding process of the \(\alpha\)-helix can be seen from the snapshots in Fig. 2e (lower panel). In addition, R337H mutation leads to more solvent exposure of the HP core-3, as seen from its increased SASA (Fig. 3e) and \(\mathrm{Rg}\) (Fig. 3f). The snapshots in Fig. 3(g, h) illustrate the alteration in the F338–R342 cation–\(\pi\) interaction and HP core-3 induced by R337H mutation. Taken together, these results demonstrate that R337H mutation disrupts the intrachain R337–M340/R335–E339 hydrophobic interactions, F338–R342 cation–\(\pi\) interaction and N-terminal MC H-bonding interactions, thus leading to more solvent exposure of the HP core-3 and the unfolding of the \(\alpha\)-helix.

R337H mutation significantly weakens the interchain
H-bonding and \(\pi{-}\pi\) stacking interactions, and results in more solvent exposure of the hydrophobic core in the dimer subunit
To explore the effect of R337H mutation on the structures of the p53TD dimer subunit, we calculated the PDF of the centroid
distance between \(\alpha\)-helices in the two identical dimer subunits, labelled A–B and C–D. In the R337H mutant, the inter-helix centroid distance is increased compared to that in the wild type dimer subunit (from \(1.2\,\mathrm{nm}\) to \(1.4\,\mathrm{nm}\)) (Fig. 4a), indicating that R337H mutation disrupts the intactness of the dimer subunit. By analyzing the PDF of the SASA (Fig. 4b) of the hydrophobic core in the dimer subunit (HP core-2, Fig. 4c), it is found that a sharp peak centered at a SASA value of \(16.0\,\mathrm{nm}^{2}\) is observed for the WT system, while it changes to a broad peak around \(18.0\,\mathrm{nm}^{2}\) for the R337H mutant. In addition, the \(\mathrm{Rg}\) of the HP core-2 in the wild type dimer subunit remains almost constant, but it increases continuously with simulation time in the R337H mutant (Fig. S9, ESI†). These data suggest that R337H mutation causes more solvent exposure of the hydrophobic core in the dimer subunits. To identify the residue–residue interactions that play a major role in the disruption of dimer subunits, we calculated the differences in the interchain pairwise residue contact number between the R337H mutant and wild type dimer subunits (Fig. 4d). In the R337H mutant, the overall contact number is decreased apparently, and relatively weakened interactions are observed for F341–F341 and R(H)337–D352 residue pairs (respectively marked by yellow and red rectangles). Previous studies reported that inter-molecular R337–D352 salt bridges play an important role in stabilization of the WT p53TD tetramer.83,84 An early implicit-solvent MD study reported that four residues R333, E349, R337 and D352 could form a fluid salt-bridging network in which partners are dynamically exchanged, which may help stabilize the p53TD tetramer.47 We also calculated the time evolution of salt bridges among the four residues, whereas the fluid salt-bridging network was not observed in our simulations (data not shown). In the p53TD R337H mutant, it is obvious that the R-to-H mutation at residue site 337 completely destroys the salt bridge (Fig. 4h). The disruption of the R337–D352 salt bridge is supported by the increase in the distance between the \(\mathrm{NH}_{2}{}^{+}\) group of R337 (or NH group of H337) and the \(\mathrm{COO^{-}}\) group of D352 (Fig. S10a, \(\mathrm{ESI\dagger}\) ). The destruction of the R337–D352 salt bridge does not exclude the existence of other interactions between the two charged groups, such as H-bonding interactions. We thus calculated the H-bond number between the two SC groups of residue R(H)337 and residue D352, and found that the SC H-bond number in the R337H mutant is smaller than that in the WT (Fig. 4i, Fig. S10b, \(\mathrm{ESI\dagger}\) ), indicating that R337H mutation destroys the H-bonding interactions between residue 337 and D352. To sum up, our data reveal that R337H mutation not only destroys the inter-helix R337–D352 salt bridge, but also disrupts F341–F341 \(\pi{-}\pi\) stacking and R337–D352 side chain H-bond interactions, thus resulting in a loosely-packed structure of the dimer subunit.
The substitution of Arg to His also exists in p53DBD. Our recent MD study48 showed that R175H mutation in p53DBD destroyed the interactions between residue 175 and the residues in the middle part of the C-terminal region of loop2, and resulted in an increased flexibility of loop2. Furthermore, R273H mutation allosterically increases the flexibility of the distant N-terminal region of loop2 by enhancing the correlation of the \(\upbeta\) -sheet and disrupting Y163–E171 H-bonds. Both mutations lead to the increase in loop flexibility and of the hydrophobic surface. These results, together with the results in this work, suggest that the substitution of Arg by His in different
domains of p53 can lead to more solvent exposure of the hydrophobic core, but through different mechanisms.

R337H mutation disrupts the hydrophobic core, alters the free-energy landscape, and weakens the dynamical correlation of the p53TD tetramer
The hydrophobic core (i.e. HP core-1) between two dimer subunits in the p53TD tetramer is crucial for stabilizing the structure of the p53TD tetramer.27 The \(\mathrm{Rg}\) distribution of the HP core-1 in Fig. 5a shows that the R337H mutant has a larger HP core than the WT. Fig. 5b presents the contact number differences of each residue in one dimer with all residues in the other dimer between the R337H mutant and the WT (i.e. R337H–p53Tet minus WT-p53Tet), with the negative value indicating that the two dimer subunits in the R337H mutant have a smaller contact number than those in the WT. It can be clearly seen that the overall contact number is reduced in the R337H mutant. Particularly, hydrophobic residues L344 and L350 exhibit significantly decreased contact numbers. The destabilization of HP core-1 can be visualized from the snapshots in Fig. 5(c, d). We also characterized the interactions between water molecules and the hydrophobic (HP) core of the p53TD tetramer by calculating the number of water molecules in contact with the HP core. The PDF of the number of water molecules in the R337H mutant is larger than that in WT-p53Tet (Fig. S11, \(\mathrm{ESI\dagger}\) ), indicative of the increased interactions between water molecules and the hydrophobic core in the R337H mutant. These data indicate that the interaction of the HP core with water molecules also contributes to the destabilization of the R337H mutant tetramer. Taken together, these results reveal that R337H mutation causes the disruption of the HP core-1 in the p53TD tetramer, which facilitates the dissociation of the p53TD tetramer. It is conceivable that with the extension of MD simulation time, the R337H mutant tetramer might become more unstable.

We further investigated the effect of R337H mutation on the dynamical correlation of the p53TD tetramer by calculating the correlation values between each two residues. Fig. 7(a, b) and 7(c, d) respectively present the correlation maps of WT-p53Tet and R337H–p53Tet from two representative independent MD runs (MD1 and MD2), which provides an overall picture of the correlated motions between protein residues. As shown in Fig. 7(a, b) the correlation maps of WT-p53Tet in both MD runs contain two strongly negatively-correlated regions (green circles), corresponding to the inter-residue interactions between chain A and C and between chain B and D. And they also contain two positively-correlated (green rectangles) regions corresponding to the inter-residue interactions between chain A and D and between chain B and C. The R337H–p53Tet correlation maps have a similar pattern to that of WT-p53Tet (Fig. 7c, d), but the correlation values in the four marked regions in R337H–p53Tet are remarkably smaller than those in WT-p53Tet. Similar results are observed in the correlation maps from the other three MD runs (Fig. S13, ESI†). These results demonstrate that R337H mutation significantly decreases the residue–residue dynamical correlations in the p53TD tetramer.

In addition, we found that connectivity between community-3 and community-5 is strengthened in the R337H mutant. By calculating the centroid distance and contact number between \(\alpha\)-helical residues L344–G356 in community-3 and \(\alpha\)-helical residues R335–R342 in community-5 and between \(\alpha\)-helical residues R335–N345 in community-1 and \(\alpha\)-helical residues E343–G356 in community-2, we found that for communities 3–5, the centroid distance is decreased and the contact number is increased in the R337H mutant (Fig. S15a, b, ESI†). These results indicate that R337H mutation weakens both the dynamical correlation and global connectivity of the p53TD tetramer, and thus causes the p53TD tetramer to go towards dissociated states.

Conclusions
In summary, we have investigated the molecular mechanism of R337H mutation-induced structural destabilization of the p53TD tetramer by performing a total of ten independent
2 μs all-atom explicit-solvent MD simulations. Our simulations show that R337H mutation causes a notable conformational change, and disrupts the monomer subunit, dimer subunit, as well as the structural integrity of the p53TD tetramer. In the monomer subunit, R337H mutation weakens the intrachain R337-M340/R335-E339 hydrophobic interactions, F338–R342 cation–π interaction and N-terminal main chain H-bonding interactions, thus leading to more solvent exposure of the hydrophobic core-3 and the unfolding of N-terminal residues \(^{335}\mathrm{RER}(\mathrm{H})\mathrm{FEM}^{340}\) of the \(\alpha\)-helix. In the dimer subunit, R337H mutation completely destroys the inter-helix R337–D352 salt bridge, and destructs F341–F341 \(\pi{-}\pi\) stacking and R337–D352 side chain H-bonding interactions, thus results in a loosely-packed HP core-2. In the p53TD tetramer, R337H mutation causes the disruption of HP core-1, alters the free-energy landscape and shifts the native conformations of the p53TD tetramer towards disordered states. R337H mutation also significantly weakens both the dynamical correlation and global connectivity, and thus destabilizes the structure of the p53TD tetramer. This study sheds light on the molecular mechanism of cancer-associated R337H mutation-induced destabilization of the p53TD tetramer and provides a theoretical basis for the design of potent anti-cancer therapies of the p53 protein.
