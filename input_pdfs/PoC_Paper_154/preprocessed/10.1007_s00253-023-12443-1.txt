Thermostability enhancement and insight of L‑asparaginase from Mycobacterium sp. via consensus‑guided engineering

Abstract
Acrylamide alleviation in food has represented as a critical issue due to its neurotoxic effect on human health. L-Asparaginase (ASNase, EC 3.5.1.1) is considered a potential additive for acrylamide alleviation in food. However, low thermal stability hinders the application of ASNase in thermal food processing. To obtain highly thermal stable ASNase for its industrial application, a consensus-guided approach combined with site-directed saturation mutation (SSM) was firstly reported to engineer the thermostability of Mycobacterium gordonae L-asparaginase (GmASNase). The key residues Gly97, Asn159, and $\mathrm{Glu}249$ were identified for improving thermostability. The combinatorial triple mutant G97T/N159Y/E249Q (TYQ) displayed significantly superior thermostability with half-life values of $61.65\pm8.69$ min at $50~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and $5.12\pm1.66\mathrm{\min}$ at $55^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , whereas the wild-type was completely inactive at these conditions. Moreover, its $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ value increased by $8.59^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ from parent wild-type. Interestingly, TYQ still maintained excellent catalytic efficiency and specific activity. Further molecular dynamics and structure analysis revealed that the additional hydrogen bonds, increased hydrophobic interactions, and favorable electrostatic potential were essential for TYQ being in a more rigid state for thermostability enhancement. These results suggested that our strategy was an efficient engineering approach for improving fundamental properties of GmASNase and offering GmASNase as a potential agent for efficient acrylamide mitigation in food industry.

Key points
• The thermostability of GmASNase was firstly improved by consensus-guided engineering.   
• The half-life and $T_{m}$ value of triple mutant TYQ were significantly increased.   
• Insight on improved thermostability of TYQ was revealed by MD and structure analysis.

Introduction
Acrylamide (AA) is an organic compound classified as Group 2A carcinogen that can be found principally in processed carbohydrate-rich foods such as potato chips, French fries, coffees, and cookies (Khaleel et al., 2021; Lineback et al. 2012). AA was mainly formed through the Maillard reaction between the carbonyl group of reducing sugars (e.g., glucose) and the amino group of certain amino acids (e.g., L-asparagine, L-ASN) (Fernández et al. 2022; Nematollahi et al. 2020). To be noted, AA displays highly toxic effects on both nervous and reproductive systems in humans and animals (Bin-Jumah et al. 2021). As a consequence, the hunt for methods to reduce AA can address the significant food safety issue. To date, a variety of strategies have been applied to inhibit AA formation including controlling the conditions of processing (temperatures and times), adding inhibitors (amino acids, organic acids, salts, and glutathione), or plant extracts (rich in phenolics and antioxidants) (Kumari et al. 2022). Besides these strategies, there is another possible approach concerning AA alleviation in food by using L-asparaginase (ASNase, EC 3.5.1.1) in the pretreatment procedure. ASNase can hydrolyze L-ASN (the precursor substance for AA formation) to L-aspartic acid (L-ASP) and ammonia (Chakraborty et al., 2021), resulting into reduced acrylamide content. As an additive for acrylamide reduction in food, ASNase has been proven to have no negative effects in the sensory (color, texture, and flavor) and nutritional (main active substances) properties of products (Correa et al. 2021; Sarion et al. 2021; Xu et al. 2016). More importantly, ASNase has been granted “Generally Recognized as Safe” (GRAS) status by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) due to its inactivation for ensuring safe use during thermal processing (Correa et al. 2021; Xu et al. 2016). Therefore, ASNase could be proposed as a potential additive for addressing the food safety problem caused by acrylamide.
To date, only two ASNases are commercially available for AA mitigation in the food industry, including PreventASe™- ASNase from Aspergillus niger by DSM (Heerlen, The Netherlands) and Acrylaway $\textsuperscript{\textregistered}$ -ASNase from Aspergillus oryzae by Novozymes (Bagsvaerd, Denmark) (Sharma et al., 2017; Tundisi et al. 2022). In the meanwhile, several other sources of ASNases have been mined to reduce acrylamide levels in food, such as Bacillus subtilis (Chohan et al., 2018; Niu et al. 2022), Aspergillus spp. (Vala et al. 2018; Yassin et al. 2022), Corynebacterium glutamicum (Chi et al. 2022a, b), and Bacillus licheniformis (Alrumman et al. 2019; Chen et al. 2019). However, the poor thermal stability of ASNase from these sources possesses negative effect on the removal rate of AA. Besides, the process of AA mitigation is under harsh thermal conditions. Therefore, ASNase of high specificity, high catalytic activity, and high thermal stability is essentially required for efficient acrylamide reduction (Chi et al. 2021; Jiao et al. 2020). Unfortunately, improving the thermal stability of enzymes and enzymatic activity in the meantime is challengeable for the trade-off between stability and activity (Gong et al. 2018; Jiao et al. 2022). Accordingly, developing efficient protein engineering strategy for ASNase is of great significance for the improvements of thermal stability and enzymatic activity to meet the demands of food processing.
Many studies have pointed out that rational design, semi-rational design, and directed evolution are important protein engineering strategies for enzymatic property improvement (Mrudula Vasudevan et al. 2019). Although directed evolution is possessed as the most efficient and widespread approach, time-consuming and expensive cost limit its operation (Liu et al. 2022; Schmitt et al. 2022; Yu et al., 2014). Thus, rational design is considered a good method for saving time. Due to the requirement of the clear understanding of protein structure and function in advance (Zhang et al. 2019), rational design is a structure-dependent strategy with significant challenge. Consequently, semi-rational design has gained popularity among researchers (Li et al. 2021a, b; Xu et al. 2020). Particularly, consensus design is recognized as one of the easiest and most reliable engineering strategies. Generally, consensus residues occur most frequently at a single position in a multiple sequence comparison of existing homologs (Forrer et al. 2004), resulting highly conserved consensus residues hold the key for protein thermodynamic stability and catalytic activity of protein according to over millions of years of evolution (Li, Xie, et al., 2021). Substituting consensus residues in a protein could introduce consensus information for protein design (Jones et al. 2020). Moreover, consensus design has been applied to improve the thermal stability of various proteins with beneficial mutations (Bai et al. 2020; Shakeel et al. 2018; Sternke et al. 2020). However, this method has not yet been introduced for the improvements of thermal stability and enzymatic activity of ASNase.
In the previous study, we reported a novel ASNase from Mycobacterium gordonae (GmASNase) with high catalytic activity, high specificity, and wide pH range (Chi et al. 2021). However, an important limitation for application was the low acrylamide reduction caused by its poor thermal stability. Herein, consensus-guided approach combined with site-directed saturation and combinatorial mutagenesis was successfully applied to improve the thermal stability of GmASNase. Engineered GmASNase achieved significantly enhanced thermostability without losing enzymatic activity. Moreover, molecular dynamics simulation and structural analysis were used to elucidate the mechanism of enhanced thermal stability of GmASNase, which could provide a theoretical basis of thermal stability engineering for other aminohydrolases.

Materials and methods

Chemicals and strains
The high-fidelity enzyme ( $2\times$ Phanta® Flash Master Mix) and reagent Kits (ClonExpress $\textsuperscript{\textregistered}$ II One Step Cloning Kit, FastPure $\textsuperscript{\textregistered}$ Gel DNA Extraction Mini Kit, and FastPure $\textsuperscript{\textregistered}$ Plasmid Mini Kit) were achieved from Vazyme Biotech Co., Ltd. The enzyme of QuickCut™ Dpn I was obtained from Takara Bio Co., Ltd. The substrate L-ASN, Nessler’s reagent (potassium mercury iodide), and TCA (trichloroacetic acid) were obtained from Aladdin Reagent Co., Ltd. Commercially available analytical grade was used for all other chemicals and reagents in the study. The E. coli BL21 (DE3) competent cell (EMD Millipore) was stored in our laboratory and was used as a host for protein expression. The plasmid pET30a-GmASNase (GmASNase gene accession number WP_069434439) from our previous study (Chi et al. 2021) was used as the template for the construction of the mutants.

Selection of the eligible residues for improving thermostability based on the consensus sequence
The amino acid sequence of the GmASNase was submitted to the Consensus Finder online server for prediction (Jones et al. 2020). Similar sequences were firstly looked up and aligned from NCBI database. Then, redundant and highly similar sequences were removed. After analyzing the consensus, a list of possible stable consensus mutations was outputted in a table.

Construction of site‑directed mutagenesis
Random mutagenesis libraries were prepared by the whole plasmid PCR method and $2\times\mathrm{Phanta@}$ Master Mix using plasmid pET 30a-GmASNase as the template, and mutagenesis primer sequences (Supplementary Table S1). The PCR products were digested with $2~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ DpnI on methylated or hemimethylated parental templates for $2\mathrm{~h~}$ , and recovered using FastPure $\textsuperscript{\textregistered}$ Gel DNA Extraction Mini Kit. The resulted products were then reconstituted using the ClonExpress $\textsuperscript{\textregistered}$ II One Step Cloning Kit and transformed into $E$ . coli BL21 for constructing the high-throughput screening library.

High‑throughput screening for mutants with improved thermal stability
Protein expression and ASNase activity assay were based on the method of Chi et al. (Chi et al. 2021). The high-throughput screening was conducted according to the method of Lu et al. with slight modifications (Lu et al. 2019). Firstly, crude enzyme solutions were added to plates A and B. Then, plate A was placed on ice and plate B was treated at $45/50/55^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $30~\mathrm{min}$ , followed by a 30-min bath on ice. Furtherly, the enzyme activities of plates A and B were measured (in the $200~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ reactions). Compared to plate A, the remaining mutants with higher residual activity than the control in plate B were finally selected for further studies.

Purification, protein determination, SDS‑PAGE analysis, and ASNase activity assay of wild‑type and mutants
The crude enzyme solutions were purified using a nickel affinity chromatography column. The protein concentration was determined at $595\mathrm{nm}$ using the Bradford method (Bradford 1976). SDS-PAGE (sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis) was conducted with separating gel ( $12\%$ , w/v) and polyacrylamide stacking gel $(5\%,\mathrm{w}/\mathrm{v})$ , which was used to analyze the purity, integrity, and molecular mass weight of ASNase. The activity of ASNase was measured using the Nessler method with Nessler’s reagent according to Chi et al. (2022a, b). The assay was performed as follows. Firstly, ${900\upmu\mathrm{L}}$ of PBS buffer $50~\mathrm{mM}$ , $\mathrm{pH}8.0$ ) containing appropriately diluted enzyme and $20~\mathrm{mM}$ substrate L-ASN reacted at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $10\mathrm{min}$ . Then, $100{\upmu\mathrm{L}}$ of $25\%$ TCA was used for reaction termination. Besides, the control group was the reaction with TCA at the beginning. Finally, $40\upmu\mathrm{L}$ of the resulted mixture was mixed with $960~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ of Nessler’s reagent dilution buffer, followed by measuring the absorbance at $\mathrm{OD}_{436}$ after $5\mathrm{min}$ . One International Unit (IU) of ASNase activity was defined as the amount of enzyme required to liberate $1\upmu\mathrm{mol}$ of ammonia per mL per min at $\mathrm{pH}8.0$ at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ .

Thermal properties of the wild‑type and its mutants
The thermal stabilities of the wild-type and mutants were assessed including three main factors: half-life $(t_{1/2})$ , melting point temperature $(T_{\mathrm{m}})$ , and the residual activity remaining at different temperatures. For $t_{1/2}$ , the enzymes were incubated with $50~\mathrm{mM}$ PBS buffer $\mathrm{(pH8.0)}$ at 40, 45, 50, and $55~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . The initial enzyme activity was defined as $100\%$ . The activities of samples extracted at different incubation times were calculated as the residual activity of the initial enzyme activity. The $t_{1/2}$ value was calculated from the equation $t_{1/2}=\ln2/$ $\mathrm{k_{d}}$ where the inactivation rate constant $(k_{\mathrm{d}})$ was determined by first-order linear regression with the equation: ln $A{=}k_{\mathrm{d}}{\times}t$ (A, residual activity; $t$ , incubation time). $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ values of wildtype and its mutants were determined using a Protein Thermal Shift™ Dye Kit (Thermo-Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). The mutants with significantly increased halflives and the wild-type were firstly introduced in the buffers of $50~\mathrm{mM}$ PBS $\mathrm{(pH~8.0)}$ with final protein amount of $1.0\upmu\mathrm{g}$ , setting the control without protein. Fluorescence was monitored using a StepOne real-time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, Waltham, MA, USA). $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ was determined as the midpoint of increased fluorescence. The enzymes were incubated at different temperatures for a period of time and residual activities were measured at intervals to analyze their thermal stability at different temperatures.

Enzymatic properties of the wild‑type and its mutants
The reaction temperatures were set at 35 to $55~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ with a temperature interval of $5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , and enzyme activities at different temperatures were measured to determine the optimum enzyme reaction temperature. To determine the optimum reaction $\mathrm{\pH}$ , enzyme activities were measured in the buffers of $\mathrm{pH}6.0\substack{-12.0}$ . The highest enzyme activity was set to $100\%$ , and enzyme activities at other temperatures or pHs were calculated as relative activities $(\%)$ . The kinetic parameters $K_{\mathrm{m}},k_{c a t},$ and $k_{c a t}/K_{\mathrm{m}}$ of wild-type and its mutants were analyzed in the presence of $1{-}10\mathrm{mM}$ L-ASN for $2\mathrm{min}$ under standard assay conditions $(\mathrm{pH}8.0,37^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ . Kinetic parameters were estimated by fitting the Michaelis–Menten plots by non-linear regression.

Molecular modeling and structural analysis
The crystal structure of ASNase from Helicobacter pylori (PDB: 2wlt) was used as the template to build the 3D structure model of the mutants on the Swiss-Model online website. The interaction of mutant residues with other residues in mutants and wild-type was analyzed using PyMOL software.

Molecular dynamics simulation analysis of wild‑type and its mutant
All-atom molecular dynamics simulations based on dimeric complexes were carried out using AMBER 18 software (Salomon-Ferrer et al. 2013). Before the simulations, the protein system was processed using the LEaP module and the protein was described using the ff14SB protein force field (Maier et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2004). Then, hydrogen atoms were added to the system, a truncated octahedral TIP3P solvent box4 was added at a distance of $10\mathring{\mathrm{A}}$ (Mark et al., 2001), and $\mathrm{{Na^{+}}}$ along with $\mathrm{Cl}^{-}$ was added to the system for balancing the system charge, and finally, the topology and parameter files for the simulations were outputted.
Meanwhile, the system was subjected to energy optimization, and then, the system was slowly ramped up from 0 to $330\mathrm{K}$ using a 200-ps ramp at a fixed volume and constant ramp rate. Five hundred picoseconds of NVT (isothermal isotropic) simulations was carried out to further homogenize the solvent molecules in the solvent box while maintaining the system at $330\mathrm{K}$ . Finally, a 50-ns equilibrium simulation of the whole system was performed for the NPT (isothermal isobaric) simulation. Tracks were saved every 5 ps for subsequent analysis. The analysis included radius of gyration $(\mathrm{Rg})$ , solvent accessible surface area (SASA), root mean square deviation (RMSD), and root mean square fluctuation (RMSF) were performed using the CPPTRAJ module in AMBER 18.

Results

Consensus sequence generation for improving thermostability of GmASNase
Based on the concept that conserved amino acids are more likely to contribute higher stability of protein folding than non-conserved amino acids (Jones et al. 2020), the amino acid sequence of GmASNase was submitted to the Consensus Finder online server for the prediction of conserved residues. The consensus sequence alignment and potential amino acids responsible for possible stable consensus with suggested mutations are shown in Supplementary Fig. S1 and Table S2, respectively. According to the references, mesoprotein and thermoprotein usually lead to biased results, as well as different correlations between residues, especially the less conserved residues (Bai et al. 2020; Jäckel et al. 2010). Thus, simple substitutions of residues that occur less frequently on the original sequence with consensus residues might not result in more stable proteins. To achieve more effective stable substitutions, site saturation mutagenesis (SSM) has been introduced into consensus sequences for mutations, where mutated residues by random mutagenesis screening can have more possibility for improved thermal stability of protein (Siloto et al., 2012). Considering the success rate, residues with consensus threshold above $30\%$ were selected in this study. In addition, substitution sites within 6-Å distance from the active site (Thr12, Thr84) of GmASNase were chosen for avoiding catalytic interruption. Finally, 17 residues in accordance with these criteria were selected for further study, including Ala172, Ala203, Ala286, Val56, Arg134, Phe44, Thr34, Ile71, Val163, Met178, Val185, Val230, Gly97, Asn159, Lys135, Glu249, and Gly164.

Residues characterization for thermostability and specific activity of GmASNase
To investigate the contribution of the consensus sequence combined with SSM for the thermal stability of GmASNase, random mutations were applied for all the selected 17 residues using degenerate primers (Supplementary Table S1) through high-throughput screening method. The specific activities and half-lives of mutants with improved thermostability were determined (Table 1). Among these mutants, the specific activities of mutants N159H, N159D, and V230M were 1.62, 1.40, and 0.75 times higher than that of wild-type, respectively, while the specific activities of other mutants were not significantly different from that of wild-type, indicating residues Asn159 and Val230 had desirable impact on enzyme activity. The half-lives of mutants V230M, N159W, N159H, N159D, A172G, G164E, R134E, E249Q, and V163C were 4.75-, 3.81-,
0.70-, 0.90-, 1.84-, 0.78-, 1.59-, 1.56-, and 1.69-fold higher than that of wild-type, respectively. To be noted, the thermal stability of mutant G97T was greatly improved with half-life of $990.21\pm5.99\mathrm{{min}}$ , which was 29.57 times than that of wild-type.
Although the above strategy provided some better thermostability mutants than the wild-type, an attempt was made to combine these residues to obtain more stable mutants. Therefore, random mutations of residues Asn159, Val230, Ala172, Arg134, Glu249, and Val163 were applied in template mutant G97T. As shown in Fig. 1A, the thermal stabilities of mutants G97T/V230A, G97T/ V230P, G97T/V230Q, and G97T/V163C were obviously improved compared with parent G97T, while their specific activities were significantly reduced. In contrast, the thermostabilities of mutants G97T/R134P, G97T/R134D, G97T/N159H, G97T/N159F, G97T/N159C, G97T/ N159W, and G97T/N159Y were significantly enhanced with slightly improved specific activities. Particularly, the half-life at $45~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ of mutant G97T/N159Y was 16.43 and 135.03-fold higher than that of parent G97T and wild-type GmASNase, respectively.
In order to achieve better thermal stability and activity of mutant G97T/N159Y (TY), continuing random mutageneses of residues Val230, Ala172, Arg134, Glu249, and Val163 were introduced in parent TY. As expected, the specific activities of mutants TY/V230T, TY/V230L, TY/V230M, TY/E249Q, and TY/R134S were 1.28, 1.37, 1.49, 1.28, and 1.13 times higher than that of parent TY, respectively (Fig. 1B). Moreover, the half-lives at $50^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ of these mutants were 1.81-, 1.78-, 1.90-, 5.46-, and 2.30-fold higher than that of parent TY, respectively. To be noted, mutation of $\mathrm{Glu}249$ contributed highest thermostability along with high specific activity among these mutations. Therefore, Gly97, Asn159, and Glu249 were identified as key residues for the thermostability and activity of GmASNase. In conclusion, consensus design and SSM technology along with gradually screening mutation were positively correlated for the thermal stability and catalytic activity of GmASNase.

Thermostability analysis of mutant GmASNases
To determine the detailed thermal stability of wild-type and mutant GmASNases, these enzymes were firstly exposed to phosphate buffer solutions $\left(\mathrm{pH8.0}\right)$ at different temperatures $(40-55~^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ . Then, their half-life $(t_{1/2})$ values were primarily determined after the pretreatment. As shown in Table 2, the $t_{1/2}$ value of wild-type was $33.49\pm3.19\mathrm{~t~}$ min at $40^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , while the $t_{1/2}$ value of mutants G97T increased to $990.21\pm5.99\mathrm{{\min}}$ . After gradual mutations of G97T, the $t_{1/2}$ values of double mutant TY and triple mutant TYQ were significantly over $990~\mathrm{{min}}$ , and more mutations contributed greatly higher $t_{1/2}$ values. At $45^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , the $t_{1/2}$ values of mutants G97T, TY, and TYQ were still much higher than that of wild-type. Besides, TY and TYQ had high $t_{1/2}$ values of $594.13\pm8.02$ min and over $990\mathrm{min}$ , respectively. Surprisingly, TYQ maintained more than 206.25 times higher $t_{1/2}$ than wild-type. As reported in the literatures, mutant enzymes A26N/G29F and S180N/D289T/ E260F/E292S of $B$ . subtilis ASNase can only achieve increased $t_{1/2}$ value of 7.76 and 8.10 times in comparison with the wild-type (Feng et al. 2018, 2019). When temperature rose to $50~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , wild-type GmASNase became inactive, while mutants G97T, TY, and TYQ still maintained active. Notably, TYQ had highest $t_{1/2}$ value of $61.65{\pm}8.69\mathrm{\min}$ . As temperature kept rising to $55^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , both wild-type and mutant G97T were inactive, but TY and TYQ were still active and had $t_{1/2}$ values of $1.04{\pm}0.16$ and $5.12\pm1.66$ min. Compared to Erwinia chrysanthemi ASNase (ErA) and Escherichia coli ASNase (EcA) with half-lives of 0.65 days and 1.28 days at $37~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Beckett et al., 2019), the super thermal stability indicated that mutant TYQ could be expected as great additive for acrylamide removal in food industrial application.
Furthermore, the melting temperature $(T_{\mathrm{m}})$ values of wild-type and mutant GmASNases were subsequently measured (Table  2). Wild-type displayed $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ value of $58.83\pm0.21~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , while mutants G97T, TY, and TYQ had gradually increased $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ values. To be noted, the $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ value of mutant TYQ significantly increased by $8.59\ ^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ from that of wild-type, displaying the similar increasing trend of $t_{1/2}$ value. The $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ value of TYQ $(67.42^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ was much higher than those of ASNases from A. soli (mutant enzyme C8Y/ C283Q with $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ value of $62.30^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ (Jiao et al. 2022), $B$ . subtilis (G107D, $64.30^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ (Long et al. 2016), and $E$ . chrysanthemi (D133V, $55.8~^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ (Kotzia et al., 2009), which was consistent with $B.$ . licheniformis ASNase (D172W/E207A, $67.43^{\circ}\mathrm{C}_{\iota}$ (Chi et al. 2022a, b). Taken together, the improved thermal stability of mutants was correlated to its structural stability at high temperatures.

Based on the above results, the thermal stabilities of wildtype and mutant GmASNases were continuingly investigated at different temperatures (Fig. 2). At $40^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , mutants G97T, TY, and TYQ had residual activities of $67.01\%$ , $83.23\%$ , and $95.23\%$ after heat treatment for $11\mathrm{~h~}$ , respectively, whereas wild-type had only residual activity of $35.41\%$ after heat treatment for $^{\textrm{1h}}$ (Fig. 2A). When temperature rose to $45^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ mutants G97T, TY, and TYQ had over $60\%$ residual activities after $150\mathrm{-min}$ heat treatment for $40\mathrm{min}$ , while wild-type lost activity at this condition (Fig. 2B). Notably, TY and TYQ even maintained $82.46\%$ and $95.33\%$ residual activities after heat treatment for $150\mathrm{min}$ . When temperature rose to $50~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , wild-type GmASNase was completely inactive, and residual activity of mutant G97T dropped to $35.75\%$ after heat treatment for $5\mathrm{min}$ . Residual activity of TY reduced to $22.06\%$ after heat treatment for $20\mathrm{min}$ . However, TYQ could still have $68.46\%$ residual activity after heat treatment for $35\mathrm{min}$ (Fig. 2C). When temperature reached $55^{\circ}\mathrm{C}.$ , both wild-type and mutant G97T completely lost their activity while mutant TY could maintain residual activity of $28.52\%$ after heat treatment for $3\mathrm{min}$ and mutant TYQ maintained residual activity of $55.45\%$ after heat treatment for $5\mathrm{min}$ (Fig. 2D). These results were consistent with the results of the $t_{1/2}$ and $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ values, which conformed that GmASNase mutants especially TYQ achieved significantly improved thermostability.

Enzymatic properties of mutant GmASNases
Considering the industrial applications of GmASNase, the enzymatic properties are essential characteristic for GmASNase mutants. The optimum reaction temperature was firstly determined for GmASNase mutants (Fig. 3A). Mutants G97T, TY, and TYQ displayed the same optimum reaction temperature of $50^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ as wild-type. When the temperature is below $50^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , three mutants all displayed obviously higher relative activities than wild-type. At the elevated temperature of $55^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , the relative activities of three mutants all exceeded $78\%$ and slightly higher than that of wild-type of $72\%$ . In general, elevated reaction temperatures are required in industrial applications for increasing reaction rate and reducing microbial contamination (Zhu et al. 2021). Therefore, three mutants gained better reaction temperatures for their applications.
To investigate the optimum reaction pHs of GmASNase mutants, their relative activities were measured over the pH range of 6.0 to 12.0 (Fig. 3B). Similar to the wild-type, mutants G97T, TY, and TYQ all showed maximum activities at alkaline pH $\mathrm{(pH9.0)}$ . Surprisingly, the $\mathrm{\pH}$ range of triple mutant TYQ was significantly wider than those of wild-type and other mutants, indicating its expanded application for various $\mathrm{\pH}$ requirements in industry.
Fig. 3   The optimum reaction temperatures (A) and pHs $\mathbf{\tau}(\mathbf{B})$ of wild-type and its mutants. The highest enzyme activity was set to $100\%$ , and the activities at the other temperatures or pHs were calculated as relative values to it $(\%)$
In addition, the kinetic constants of wild-type and GmASNase mutants were determined using L-ASN as the substrate (Table 3). The $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ and $k_{c a t}$ values of mutant TYQ were slightly higher than those of wild-type, while mutants G97T and TY had lower $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ and $k_{c a t}$ values than those of wild-type. Among these $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ values, mutant G97T had lowest value, indicating that the G97T substitution promoted better substrate binding. In the meanwhile, the catalytic efficiency $(k_{c a t}/K_{\mathrm{m}})$ of mutant G97T was the highest among wild-type and other mutants, while its specific activity was the lowest. Besides, mutant TYQ displayed slightly higher catalytic efficiency than wild-type and highest specific activity among wild-type and other mutants. Considering enzymes that can maintain high catalytic efficiency, high specific activity, and improved thermostability are required for industrial processes (Vimal et al., 2017), the favorable enzymatic properties made mutant TYQ potentially promising for industrial applications.

Molecular dynamic analysis of wild‑type and mutant GmASNase
Molecular dynamic (MD) simulations were performed to investigate the global and local flexibilities of wild-type and mutant TYQ. Since the root mean square deviation (RMSD) is an important basis for measuring protein stability and is a key parameter in MD simulations to reflect the internal motion properties and flexibilities of molecules (Yang et al. 2022), RMSD was firstly analyzed for wild-type and mutant TYQ. As shown in Fig. 4A, RMSD values of wild-type and mutant TYQ could gradually converge to equilibrium around 5 ns. After

50-ns simulation, wild-type had an average RMSD of $1.60\mathring{\mathrm{A}}$ and TYQ had an average RMSD of $1.50\mathring{\mathrm{A}}$ . In general, a lower RMSD value presents better preservation of the initial conformation of enzyme structure and thus higher thermal stability (Donga et al. 2018; Wang et al. 2020). Therefore, the lower RMSD value could contribute better thermostability of mutant TYQ. Root mean square fluctuations (RMSF) are generally used to determine the variation of each residue to its mean and also an indicator of the flexibility of molecular structure (Yang et al. 2022). To gain more insight into flexibility, the RMSF values of wild-type and mutant TYQ were compared (Fig. 4C and D). The overall fluctuations were very similar in both enzymes, while mutant TYQ had significantly smaller fluctuations in the regions of loops 157–166 in chain A (Fig. 4C) and loops 26–34, 185–196, and 240–250 in chain B (Fig. 4D) than those of wild-type, indicating the lower flexibilities of these loops for improved thermal stability. According to the references, the radius of gyration $(\mathrm{Rg})$ is related to the structural stability of the protein and smaller $\mathrm{Rg}$ value represents more stable structure (Cheng et al. 2020; Wang et al. 2020). The $\mathrm{Rg}$ values of wild-type and mutant TYQ were determined (Fig. 4B). The average $\mathrm{Rg}$ value for wild-type was $25.42\mathring\mathrm{A}$ , while the average $\mathrm{Rg}$ value for mutant TYQ decreased to $25.29\mathring{\mathrm{A}}$ , supporting TYQ of tighter structure. Since the solvent accessible surface area (SASA) value indicates the aggregation-related properties of protein and smaller SASA value implies tighter stacking structure (Wang et al. 2020), the SASA values of wild-type and mutant TYQ were analyzed as well (Fig. 4E). The average SASA value of mutant TYQ was $149.91\mathring{\mathrm{A}}^{\bar{2}}$ , which was lower than that of wild-type, indicating a tighter structure of TYQ. Based on the above results, mutant TYQ has lower flexible and more stable structure for thermostability than wild-type.

Structural properties of mutant GmASNase
In order to investigate the effects of these mutations in mutant TYQ on GmASNase structure, the interactions and surface charge distributions around the mutation sites were primarily analyzed. For the site Gly97, a hydrogen bond was formed with residue Leu94 $(3.2\mathring\mathrm{\textrm{A}})$ . After the replacement of Gly to Tyr, more hydrogen bonds were formed with residues Leu94 and Trp93, respectively $3.3\mathring\mathrm{A}$ and $3.2\mathring\mathrm{A}$ ) (Fig. 5A and B). Asn159 in wild-type was located in the middle of the hydrophobic cavity and formed hydrophobic interactions with the surrounding residues Lys157, Glu162, Val163, Val259’, and $s\mathrm{er}261^{\prime}$ , in addition to hydrogen bonds with residues Thr160 and Gln161 (Fig. 5C). However, Tyr159 in mutant TYQ formed hydrophobic interactions with residues Lys157, Thr160, Gln161, Glu162, Val163, Val259’, Ser261’, Gly262’, and $\mathrm{Arg}263^{\circ}$ (Fig. 5D). $\mathrm{Glu}249$ in wild-type was located in the negative potential region of GmASNase surface, which was inconsistent with the surrounding potential distribution and made a rather negative contribution to the overall stability (Fig. 5E). In contrast, the $\mathrm{Gln}249$ in mutant TYQ remained consistent with the surrounding potential distribution and had a positive potential distribution (Fig. 5F). The symmetry of the electrostatic potential on the protein surface is also a major factor in thermal stability, and a greater symmetrical positive potential likely leads to higher thermal stability (Zhou et al. 2019).

Discussion
AA is a class 2A carcinogen with carcinogenic, neurotoxic, immunotoxic, and reproductive toxicity. The hydrolysis ability of ASNase can specifically hydrolyze L-Asn (the prerequisite substance of acrylamide), for solving the problems of acrylamide production from the root. Currently, only two sources of ASNase have been commercially used in food processing. However, these ASNases have some disadvantages, such as few kinds, low yield, high price, and poor thermal stability. Therefore, on the basis of known properties of ASNases, it is very important to identify and design ASNases with higher catalytic activity and higher thermostable for the wide application.

Direction evolution allows libraries of enzymes to be evolved by screening random mutations (Lansing et al. 2022). Several enzymes have already been developed by this way, demonstrating the application of direction evolution (Buchholz et al. 2020; Hauber 2008; Lu et al. 2019). However, the generation of a new mutant enzyme still requires substantial time and resource investments. The evolution of some of these enzymes took over 150 cycles, and an equivalent of 6–12 months of work (Schmitt et al. 2022). Therefore, although direction evolution is effective, it remains expansive in terms of time and labor (Liu et al. 2022). At the start point of a new project, it is unclear which of the modifications have the target properties. However, due to the complex nature of enzyme, it has so far not been possible to rationally design mutant enzyme with desirable properties at a new target sequence (Schmitt et al. 2022). Hence, a method predicting amino acid changes has the potential for accelerating the generation of novel designer enzymes.
Compared with directed evolution and rational design, semi-rational design is a simple, efficient, and successful method for protein engineering. B-factor, multiple sequence alignment, and consensus analysis have been all used for engineering the thermal stability of proteins. In particular, consensus analysis is an effective method for improving the thermal stability of proteins. In this study, based on consensus guided, site saturation mutagenesis (SSM) and combination mutation technique were used to design the mutants with greatly improved thermal stability. Finally, the combined approach of consensus design and SSM technology displayed a positive success rate of $47\%$ for enhancing the thermal stability of GmASNase, which was significantly higher than that of $18\%$ by simple substitution of consensus residues (Supplementary Table S2), suggesting our combined strategy of great advantage for effective stable substitutions. Tripe mutant TYQ showed better thermal stability than double mutant TY and single mutant G97T. Studies have shown that beneficial amino acid mutations usually exhibit cumulative or synergistic effects on the thermal stability of proteins (Zhu et al. 2018). Herein, the beneficial residues Gly97, Asn159, and $\mathrm{\Glu{249}}$ predicted by the combinatorial approach had a synergistic effect on the thermal stability and activity of GmASNase, confirming our strategy of effectiveness on stable substitution furtherly. Therefore, the strategy designed in this study by consensus-based combinatorial strategy could pave an effective way to improve the thermostability of other enzymes.
Enzyme preparations are now widely used in the chemical, food, and pharmaceutical industries. Due to differences in pretreatment conditions, the complexity of the processing environment, and the stability of storage process, enzymes are required to maintain high catalytic activity and stability over a wide range of $\mathrm{pH}$ and temperature (Zhang et al. 2021). The conditions of commercial application vary between enzyme preparations, while ASNase for industrial application has to meet the good catalytic activity and stability at $40–50\ ^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and $\mathrm{pH}6.0{-9.0}$ (Zhang et al. 2021). The properties of ASNases from different sources vary considerably, and only those enzymes that meet these criteria can be considered potential new food agents (Beckett et al., 2019). Although thermophilic sources of ASNases have high catalytic activity $(550{-}5278\mathrm{IU/mg)}$ and thermal stability (good thermal stability at above $85^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ ), their activities decrease significantly or even lose below $60^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Table S3), which is a great challenge in meeting industrial applications. In addition, commercial ASNase PreventASe™-ASNase from $A$ . niger has an optimum reaction temperature of $50^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , while the optimum reaction $\mathrm{pH}$ is 4.0–5.0; Acrylaway $\textsuperscript{\textregistered}$ -ASNase from A. oryzae has an optimum reaction $\mathrm{\pH}$ of 7.0, whereas the optimum reaction temperature is $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Yim et al., 2019). They have certain limitations in terms of temperature or $\mathrm{\pH}$ preference. In the present study, the mutant ASNase TYQ obtained through consensus-based combinatorial strategy has excellent properties at $40–50\ ^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and $\mathrm{pH}6.0\substack{-12.0}$ , which makes it as a good candidate for industrial applications.
It is widely known that proteins should be considered dynamic systems rather than static objects (Chen et  al. 2020). It has been shown that reducing the flexibility of surface and cavity residues can achieve enzyme thermostability, revealing that flexibility is an important indicator of improved enzyme thermostability (Joo et al. 2010). Besides, many structural factors influence the thermal stability of proteins, such as electrostatic interaction, salt bridge, disulfide bond, hydrogen bond, hydrophobic interaction, secondary structure, and amino acid composition (Guo et al. 2017; Kumar et al. 2000). According to the reference, the formation of hydrogen bonds network helps to stabilize the surrounding structure for the thermal stability of the whole protein (Mrudula Vasudevan et al. 2019). The formation of a single hydrogen bond provides a net stability energy of approximately $0.6\mathrm{kcal/mol}$ (Bi et al. 2020), and the additional hydrogen bond could potentially contribute stability energy for G97T mutation for its structural stability. Although the hydrogen bonding interactions disappeared in the mutant TYQ, the hydrophobic interactions obviously increased, forming a large hydrophobic cluster. Pace et al.
(2011) found that hydrophobic interactions contributed approximately $64\%$ to protein stability, while hydrogen bonding contributed $44\%$ to protein stability. Therefore, the increased hydrophobic interactions were more favorable for N159Y mutation for its structural stability. Tyr as a strongly hydrophobic residue contains a benzene ring side chain, which tends to fill the hydrophobic cavity more efficiently for stabilizing protein structure (Yang et al. 2015). Furthermore, Glu is deprotonated and charged residue under physiological conditions due to a $\mathrm{\pKa}$ of 4.1 for the $\scriptstyle\gamma-\mathrm{COOH}$ . Charged residues are generally considered to be detrimental to the internal stability of protein. In contrast, the Gln does not have a dissociative behavior and therefore reduces damage to the local structural densities (Zhu et al. 2016). Besides, studies have reported that the enhanced interaction network around Gln facilitates the stabilization of the $\upbeta$ -hairpin structure for contributing substrate binding and improving catalytic activity (Menach et al. 2012). Thus, E249Q could gain structural stability by greater electrostatic potential on the protein surface.
Based on our results, the additional hydrogen bonds, increased hydrophobic interactions, and favorable electrostatic potential changes on the protein surface generated by the mutations could help mutant TYQ to maintain a highly stable conformation for improving its thermal stability and catalytic activity. Jiao et al. (2022) constructed two non-conserved cysteine residues library, and the formation of new hydrogen bonds of $\mathrm{Gln}283$ and the aromatic interaction of Tyr8 formed with adjacent residues resulted in enhanced thermostability. Chi et al. (2022a, b) enhanced the rigidity of the three-dimensional structure by substituting Phe172 with Asp172 and Ala 207 with Glu 207, which improved the thermal stability of B. licheniformis ASNase. The mutations result in small conformation changes and surface electrostatic potential redistribution, which is more conducive to maintaining the stability of the protein structure (Long et al. 2016). In addition, the flexibility of the N-terminal region of ASNase could also affect the enzyme thermostability (Feng et al. 2019).
In conclusion, engineered GmASNase with high thermal stability and enzymatic activity was successfully generated. To our knowledge, this is the first thermostability design of ASNase with a $47\%$ positive mutation rate by consensusbased combinatorial strategy. Triple mutant enzyme TYQ was obtained with a half-life of more than $990\mathrm{min}$ at $45^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , which was over 206.25-fold higher than that of wild-type, and its $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ reached $67.42^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . The thermal stability of TYQ was significantly enhanced compared to those of the single and double mutants of GmASNase. Further molecular dynamics and 3D structural analysis revealed that mutant TYQ was introduced with increased hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and favorable electrostatic potential for its improved thermal stability. Our research explored a semi-rational and effective approach along with insight into thermal stability enhancement of
GmASNase, which provided a potential orientation for engineering L-asparaginase to address acrylamide-caused food safety issue in food industry.
