Hierarchical contrastive representation for zero shot learning
Ziqian Lu\(^{1}\) · Zheming Lu\(^{1}\) \(\circledcirc\) · Zewei He\(^{1}\) · Xuecheng Sun\(^{1}\) · Hao Luo\(^{1}\) · Yangming Zheng\(^{1}\)
Accepted: 17 May 2024 / Published online: 12 July 2024 \(\circledcirc\) The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Science+Business Media, LLC, part of Springer Nature 2024

Abstract
Zero-shot learning aims to identify unseen (novel) objects, using only labeled samples from seen (base) classes. Existing methods usually learn visual-semantic interactions or generate absent visual features of unseen classes to compensate for the data imbalance problem. However, existing methods ignore the representation quality of visual-semantic pairs, resulting in unsatisfactory alignment and prediction bias. To tackle these issues, we propose a Hierarchical Contrastive Representation learning paradigm, termed HCR, which fully exploits model representation capability and discriminative information. Specifically, we first propose a contrastive embedding, which preserves not only high quality representations but also discriminative enough information from class-level and instance-level supervision. Then, we introduce a regressor by valuable prior knowledge for conducting more desirable visual-semantic alignment for unseen classes. A pluggable calibrator is also aggregated to further alleviate prediction bias in contrastive embedding. Extensive experiments show that the proposed HCR can significantly outperform the state-of-the-arts on popular benchmarks under ZSL and challenging GZSL settings.
Keywords Generalized zero-shot learning \(\cdot\) Visual-semantic alignment \(\cdot\) Contrastive embedding \(\cdot\) Calibration model

1 Introduction
A powerful nervous system ensures that human beings can recognize a variety of objects based on numerous prior knowledge even without seeing them in advance. For example, given the knowledge that zebras appear like horses with similar striped pattern of the tiger and black and white color like penguins, one can quickly recognize a zebra if he or she has previously seen tigers and penguins. However, conventional supervised machine learning does not excel at recognizing objects without seeing them during the
training phase. Motivated by this challenge, zero-shot learning (ZSL) [1, 2] was proposed to recognize novel classes by a subtle designed knowledge transfer model using side information.
Existing ZSL approaches typically address this task by leveraging the available seen classes samples to learn interaction models to connect class semantic prototypes and visual instances, i.e. embedding-based methods [3], or by generating features for unseen classes, i.e., generative-based methods. Xian et al. [4], Li et al. [5], Xu et al. [6] While gratifying progress has been achieved, the existing approaches still suffer from three aspects: (1) Overly generic and simplistic model representation. As shown in Fig. 1, both embedding-based and generative-based approaches focus on aligning non-trivial yet informative visual and semantic features via designed models. However, the mapping between various modalities is often performed through shallow MLP structures, as the representation quality is not adequately concerned, which severely limits model performance. (2) Neglecting valuable semantic information of unseen classes. Existing methods pay limited attention to discriminative semantic knowledge in unseen classes given the highly entangled inter-class semantic information. Therefore, the learned interactions between visual and semantic space in these models are suboptimal, leading to undesirable bias problem. (3)

Prediction bias for domain. For conventional ZSL, it is not necessary to worry about the bias issue as the seen classes are totally excluded from the test phase. But in GZSL [7], the bias towards the seen classes destroys the transfer of knowledge from the seen classes to unseen ones [8]. In summary, the overall goal of the GZSL is to reduce the prediction bias towards seen classes and core point is how to learn an ideal representation for both unseen and seen classes.
To tackle these challenges, we propose a Hierarchical Contrastive Representation learning paradigm, termed HCR, which fully exploits model representation capability and valuable semantic information. By constraining the feature representations for classes-level and instances-level individually, it greatly improves our model's discriminative. To further make full use of valuable semantic information from unseen classes, we design an effective regressor to conduct desirable visual-semantic interaction, especially for unseen ones. Thus, contrastive representation optimization can also be applied collaboratively to unseen classes. Additionally, a pluggable yet empirical regularizer for prediction calibration is aggregated for alleviating domain bias. In this way, we could build a more discriminative visual-semantic relationship to reduce the bias issue in GZSL.
Specifically, inspired by the phenomenon in [10–12] that contrastive representation learning leads to better down
stream task performance, we first propose a contrastive embedding, which simultaneously preserves alignment and uniformity properties for feature representations on both unseen classes and seen ones. First, alignment ensures the tightness of features from positive pairs and features are thus invariant to unneeded noises. Second, uniformity encourages to preserve as much inherent information of the features as possible, which improves the possibility that unseen features stand alongside seen ones, alleviating bias issue implicitly. The empirical analysis of how the quality of alignment and uniformity affect the ZSL model is also given. However, just learning to adjust the feature distribution by contrastive representations is overly ambiguous for challenging ZSL/GZSL tasks. Therefore, we leverage not only class-level supervision but also instance-level supervision to further constrain feature representations. By decoupling the discriminative information into a class-wise and a instance-wise aspect, the quality of both visual and semantic representations is reasonably improved.
In addition, although the basic performance of the model is improved after the contrastive representation learning, we cannot conduct desirable visual-semantic alignment of unseen classes due to the lack of prior knowledge of unseen classes, i.e., class labels. To get the best of both worlds, we introduce an effective regressor to compensate for the lack
of unseen supervisions. Intuitively, we establish a regression process between seen and unseen semantic information, and take the regression coefficients as the transferable interactions. It essentially learns the mapping relationship from the seen classes prototype to the unseen classes prototype. Therefore, the regression coefficients can be regarded as extra supervision to replace one-hot labels of seen classes, and it can be explicitly applied in collaborative visual-semantic alignment of the unseen classes.
Last but not least, to further alleviate prediction bias for domain, we calibrate the predictions by a pluggable novelty detector. Different from the previous method, we calibrate scores from the output of contrastive embedding for both seen and unseen classes instead of single domain(e.g.,increase the prediction probability of unseen classes), which explicitly and simply reduces the prediction bias. Furthermore, this calibration benefits from the proposed contrastive embedding, which is more discriminative for domain classification. Detailed analysis of calibration is also given to explore the impact of different calibration ways on performance.
To summarize, the major contributions are as follows:
- We propose a contrastive embedding which simultaneously preserves alignment and uniformity properties for feature representations. Effectively class-level and instance-level constraints are then introduced to further improve the discriminative for representations.- We introduce a simple regression mechanism to compensate for the lack of unseen supervision, encouraging model conduct unbiased visual-semantic alignment for both seen and unseen classes.- We also introduce a novel pluggable calibrator, which directly calibrates the output scores of contrastive embedding for seen and unseen classes rather than single domain. Explicit calibration strategy significantly reduces the problems of prediction bias.- Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed method achieves excellent and competitive performance on four popular ZSL/GZSL benchmarks. Empirical yet detailed analysis of the regression mechanism and the calibrator proves the effectiveness.

2 Related work
In this section, we review the related work in terms of zeroshot Learning, contrastive learning and novelty detection.

2.1 Zero-shot learning
Different from Few-shot learning (FSL) [13, 14], ZSL aims to recognize objects which is never seen before. As visual
Different from Few-shot learning (FSL) [13, 14], ZSL aims to recognize objects which is never seen before. As visual information is not available during training stage, ZSL models [15] usually need some side information to transfer discriminative knowledge to unseen classes. Generalized zero-shot learning (GZSL) [16–21] extends the test classes into the combination of seen and unseen classes, which is more challenging. Early ZSL methods aimed to learn interactions between visual and semantic spaces to transfer the class-specific information, called embedding-based methods [22, 23]. Some approaches directly map visual features to semantic domain, and thus the model can train a classifier for unseen classes based on semantic information (e.g., attribute, wor2vec). Other methods explored visual space as the classification space to alleviate hubness problem [9]. Additionally, there are some popular methods conduct the visual-semantic alignment in latent space. By mapping the original feature to latent space, the more intrinsic information has been preserved. As one of the methods based on latent-embedding, RelationNet [24] proposes a learnable metric, which is the most related to our methods. However, due to the lack of unseen class information, it can only align visual-semantic pairs of seen classes ignoring the unseen ones. Differently, we compensate for the lack of unseen class information by using regression mechanism to conduct unbiased visual-semantic alignment. Another method related to our work is EDE [25], which optimizes visual-semantic relationships on latent space. But EDE focuses on transductive setting that require prior test data, which is different from our method.
Although promising progress has been achieved, these approaches still suffer from prediction bias problems for the lack of visual prior knowledge of unseen classes. Therefore the generative based ZSL methods (e.g., GANs, VAE) [4–6, 26] are introduced to address this challenge by synthesizing numbers of visual features or class prototypes for unseen classes. With the help of generative models, challenging ZSL can thus be converted to supervised learning. GAZSL [26] learns to generate samples from noisy text via GAN. FCiswgan [4] further expands the training paradigm into WGAN fashion. CADA-VAE [27] explores the generation using more stable VAEs. However, the generative methods described above focus on capturing a similar distribution to the source visual features, neglecting the representations ability for the generator. In contrast, we explore feature representations by contrastive learning to improve the quality of representation for final classification.

2.2 Contrastive representation learning
Representation learning aims to learn more useful intrinsic information from training data so that this information can be better applied to downstream tasks. SimCLR [10] uses data augmentation to generate a large number of positive pairs and negative pairs to mine inherent information. MoCo [28]
cache a large number of negative instances by momentum update. Li et al. [29] extends the contrastive loss into 3D fashion through a multi-channel strategy. Motivated by SimCLR, CE-ZSL [30] extends the traditional self-supervised contrastive loss to a supervised manner. However, CE-ZSL only conducts visual-semantic constraints while neglecting the alignment and uniformity properties of representations. Considering the quality of the representations, we build connections between self-supervised and supervised losses. CC-ZSL [31] also involves contrastive learning, but it still classifies in visual space while we use latent space as classification space.

2.3 Novelty detection in ZSL
Novelty detection [32] aims to recognize outliers from positive data points by learning distribution. Noteworthily, the training set does not include abnormal points. In fact, traditional novelty detection (also called OOD) methods can be grouped into three lines, i.e., Probability-based, Similarity-based and Energy-based methods. MSP [33] filter out novelty data by softmax comparison. MODE [34] introduces a global-local attention mechanism to improve the discriminative of the representation. SEM [35] propose to separate out-of-distribution points by an energy model. Inspired by this, the unseen classes in GZSL can be regarded as outliers. There are some works explore the novelty detection, e.g., CMT [36] performs novelty detection in semantic space by Gaussian estimation, and then calibrates the probability. ESZSL [37] extends the calibration to stacking ways. Nonetheless, ESZSL aims at calibrating outputs by a plain threshold for single domain (i.e., unseen domain), while we classify and calibrate various objects directly by contrastive values for both seen and unseen domain. In addition, COSMO [38] propose to calibrate the output by soft way. OOD [39] and DUS [40] adopt similar spherical VAEs to learn a reasonable boundary for in-distribution features. However, these VAE methods require more computing resource to build a large number of class-wise prototypes. In this work, we focus more on feature representations, while reducing prediction bias by novelty detection. Detailed analysis of calibration is given in Section 3.4.

3 Proposed method

in Section 3.3. Finally, we introduce the pluggable calibrator followed by the proposed model training and inference strategy. The overall architecture of proposed method is shown in Fig. 2.

3.1 Preliminary & definition
In traditional zero-shot learning, we are given \(N\) seen classes denoted by \(\mathcal{V}^{S}\) and \(L\) unseen classes denoted by \(\mathcal{V}^{U}\) , where the seen and unseen classes are disjoint, i.e., \(\mathcal{V}^{S}\cap\mathcal{V}^{U}\,=\) \(\varnothing\) . Formally, the set of visual samples and labels can be represented as \(D_{t r}\ =\ \{({\bf x}_{i},{\bf y}_{i})|{\bf x}_{i}\ \in\ \mathcal{X}^{S},\mathbf{y}_{i}\ \in\ \mathcal{V}^{S}\}_{i=1}^{N}\) , where \(\mathbf{x}_{i}~\in~\mathcal{X}^{S}\) denotes the visual feature and \(\mathbf{y}_{i}\) represents the corresponding label. Similarly, we have test set \(D_{t e}=\{({\bf x}_{i},{\bf y}_{i})|{\bf x}_{i}\in\mathcal{X}^{U},{\bf y}_{i}\in\mathcal{V}^{U}\}_{i=N+1}^{N+L}\) . Meanwhile, the semantic descriptors (e.g., attributes) of both seen and unseen classes are also provide \(\mathcal{A}\,=\,\{\mathbf{s}_{1}\,,\,...,\,\mathbf{s}_{N},\,\mathbf{s}_{N+1},\,...,\,\mathbf{s}_{N+L}\}\) , where the first \(N\) semantic features correspond to seen classes in \(\mathcal{V}^{S}\) , while the remainder correspond to unseen ones. The goal of traditional ZSL is to learn \(f_{Z S L}:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{V}^{U}\) . In GZSL, the test samples include both seen and unseen classes, thus the goal of GZSL is to learn \(f_{G Z S L}:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{V}^{S}\cup\mathcal{V}^{U}\) .

4 Experiments and analysis
In this section, we evaluate the proposed method in detail. Extensive experiments on four popular benchmarks are given to prove the effectiveness of the proposed HCR. Additionally, comprehensive ablation studies also demonstrate the influence of each part.

4.1 Settings
Datasets We conduct experiments on four popular benchmarks, i.e., 2 coarse-grained datasets including Animals with Attributes2 (AwA2) [44] and attribute-Pascal and Yahoo (APY) [44], and 2 fine-grained Oxford Flowers (FLO) [45] and Caltech-UCSD Birds (CUB) [46]. AwA2 contains 37,322 images of 50 classes animals and 85 attributes for each class. APY has 15,339 image collected from PASCAL VOC dataset and Yahoo images, 20 classes for training and 12 classes as test set, and 102 attributes. CUB contains 11,788 birds images of 200 classes and FLO has 8,189 flowers images of 102 classes. For these fine-grained datasets, we follow [47] to use CNN-RNN 1.024-dimensional learned features as class semantic prototypes. For the visual aspect, 2,048-dimensional extracted features by ResNet-101 are regarded as visual features, and positive pairs are obtained by augmentations. The detailed information of four benchmarks are given in Table 1.
ZSL&GZSL Metrics We analyze the performance of the proposed HCR under both the popular ZSL and GZSL setting. ZSL setting focuses on the classification ability of the model for unseen classes, while GZSL focuses more on the comprehensive performance of the model for all the test classes. For traditional ZSL we compare the average per-class accuracy: \(\mathbf{T}\) of the test (unseen) classes of the methods. For GZSL, the average per-class accuracy of unseen classes: U and seen classes: S are also given to evaluate the model performance. The harmonic mean between S and U denoted by \(\mathbf{H}=(2\times\mathbf{S}\times\mathbf{U})/(\mathbf{S}+\mathbf{U})\) is also given to evaluate the comprehensive performance of the model.
Model variants & implementation details Thanks to the flexibility of the proposed model, we can comprehensively analyze the performance of different variants. Specifically, HCR w/o R denotes the variant of HCR without \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{unseen}}^{\text{class}}\), i.e., setting \(\omega=0\) in (13), HCR w/ N is the variant with neural networks regression, and HCR w/ R is the variant with Ridge regression. Similarly, HCR w/o C denotes the variant without calibrations. Finally, HCR w/o RC denotes base model without regressor and calibrations.
Encoder is performed by MLP with 2048 dimensional hidden layers, where the dimension of latent features \(\mathbf{z}\) is 512. MLP maps attribute features to visual space by 4096 dimensional hidden layers equipped with ReLU. Similarly, RNet includes 4096 dimensional hidden layers and sigmoid function, and the output dimension is 1. Regressor is performed by Ridge regression, or alternatively training a single hidden layer(512 dimension) neural network by semantic features. Calibrator is performed by a softmax classifier with 2048 dimensional hidden layers equipped with CrossEntropy Loss. The learning rate of the calibrator and RNet is 0.001 and 0.0001, respectively. Other hyperparameters of the proposed method are given in the following sections, respectively.
Data splits For traditional ZSL setting, the test set \(\mathcal{D}_{u}^{te}\) only contains unseen classes samples. In other words, the calibrations for prediction biases is not necessary. In contrast, the
proposed calibration strategy can effectively alleviate this problem in GZSL. It is worth noting that, reorganization is only for implementation convenience and does not destroy the fairness of the test scope.

For Hierarchical Constraints, we can observe that there are about \(1.3\%\) performance gains for \(\mathbf{H}\) when instance-level constraint is adopted. As detailed instance information can be preserved as much as possible, the model's discrimination is also improved. For Contrastive Representation, it can be seen that the performance of the model will be significantly improved through the combination of \(\mathcal{L}_{Alignment}\) and \(\mathcal{L}_{Uniformity}\). For example, when \(\lambda\) is greater than 0.001, we observe stable results in the second column of each dataset. Therefore, \(\lambda=0.001\) is adopted for these datasets.
Effect of calibrations By introducing a calibrator in Section 3.4, we can adopt different \(\phi_{1}\) and \(\phi_{2}\) for contrastive values to alleviate prediction bias between seen and unseen classes. We evaluate the performance on APY and FLO in Fig. 9. It can be seen in Fig. 9(a) that U rises slowly and S drops sharply when larger \(\phi_{1}\) is adopted. This shows that by directly adjusting contrastive values, the judgment ability of the model for unseen classes can be enhanced at the expense of the classification performance of seen classes. In other words, there is a trade-off between U and S. However, \(\phi_{2}\) has a different degree of impact on performance than \(\phi_{1}\). Figure 9(b) shows that \(\phi_{2}\) has little benefit from the performance improvement of S. We speculate that this is strongly related to the class isolation of APY, where the training set comes from PASCAL VOC and the test set comes from Yahoo. Thus, the S changes
slightly when \(\phi_{2}\) varies from 0 to 1. With the combination of different \(\phi_{1}\) and \(\phi_{2}\), an optimal result can be found with [0.2, 0]. Similar phenomena can also be seen in Fig. 9(c-d). It is worth noting that due to the lack of a perfect novelty detector, there are miscalculations of domains. As shown in Fig. 10, although there are significant gaps between the different domains obtained by the proposed \(I(C(\bullet))\), (which is beneficial for classification), the entropy values of different instances may still be very similar or even overlap. Therefore, the phenomenon in Fig. 9(c) that U does not increase when \(\phi_{1}\) varies from 0.3 to 0.5 can be reasonably explained. On FLO dataset, \(\phi_{1}\) and \(\phi_{2}\) are setted with [0.1, 0.1]. On AwA2 and CUB, our model achieves best results when the parameters in [0.1, 0.05] and [0.3, 0], respectively.
Notably, although CMT [36], ESZSL [37] also uses calibration strategy, they focus more on single calibration, while we calibrate the output on both seen and unseen domains. Another problem comes from that the calibration of existing methods are performed in semantic space. Since the model estimate deviates too far, conventional probability-based calibration in semantic space is likely to lose its effectiveness. Table 5 also shows that the calibration performance is worse when the classification space is semantic space. In contrast, we believe that the proposed contrastive value in this paper is more suitable for calibration than the traditional ones, because it contains not only 0,1 discriminative label information for seen classes, but also transferable continuous information for unseen classes. 5 Limitations and discussion
Although the proposed HCR achieves progressive performance in GZSL, the overall optimization still requires several hyper parameters, such as temperature for contrastive loss. We hope to model the entire optimization process through a unified end-to-end architecture in future work, thereby reducing parameter burdens.

6 Conclusion
Existing methods usually learn visual-semantic interactions or generate visual features of unseen classes to compensate for missing data. However, existing methods ignore the representation quality of visual-semantic pairs, resulting in unsatisfactory alignment. To tackle this issue, we propose a contrastive representation learning paradigm, termed HCR, which fully exploits model representation capability and discriminative information. Specifically, we first propose a contrastive embedding, which preserves not only high quality representations but also discriminative enough information from class-level and instance-level supervision. The alignment and uniformity properties are first concerned to improve the representation quality, and Hierarchical constraints then ensure that detailed information is captured. Then, we introduce a regressor by valuable prior knowledge for conducting more desirable visual-semantic alignment for unseen classes. With the help of regressor, our model can endow the visual-semantic pairs with unbiased transferable semantic knowledge. Last but not least, a pluggable novel calibrator is then aggregated to further reduce the prediction directly in contrastive embedding. Extensive experiments under ZSL and challenging GZSL settings show that the proposed method can outperform the state-of-the-art on four popular benchmarks.
