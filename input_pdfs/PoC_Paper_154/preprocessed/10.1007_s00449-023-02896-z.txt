Computer‑aided design of novel cellobiose 2‑epimerase for efficient synthesis of lactulose using lactose

Abstract
Cellobiose 2-epimerase (CE) is ideally suited to synthesize lactulose from lactose, but the poor thermostability and catalytic efficiency restrict enzymatic application. Herein, a non-characterized CE originating from Caldicellulosiruptor morganii $\scriptstyle(\mathrm{CmCE})$ was discovered in the NCBI database. Then, a smart mutation library was constructed based on FoldX $\Delta\Delta\mathbf{G}$ calculation and modeling structure analysis, from which a positive mutant D226G located within the ${\upalpha}_{8}/{\upalpha}_{9}$ loop exhibited longer half-lives at $65{-}75^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ as well as lower $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ and higher $k_{\mathrm{{cat}}}/K_{\mathrm{{m}}}$ values compared with CmCE. Molecular modeling demonstrated that the improvement of D226G was largely attributed to the rigidification of the flexible loop, the compactness of the catalysis pocket and the increment of substrate-binding capability. Finally, the yield of synthesizing lactulose catalyzed by D226G reached $45.5\%$ , higher than the $35.9\%$ achieved with $\mathrm{CmCE}$ . The disclosed effect of the flexible loop on enzymatic stability and catalysis provides insight to redesign efficient CEs to biosynthesize lactulose.

Introduction
Lactulose (4-o- $\boldsymbol{\cdot}\beta$ -galactopyranosyl-d-fructose) is a nondigestible disaccharide that can be applied in pharmaceutical and nutraceutical fields [1], due to its beneficial properties of decreasing pathogenic gastrointestinal microorganisms, enhancing colonic motility and improving mineral absorption [2]. To date, the large-scale lactulose synthesis remains dependent on the Bruyn-Alberda van Ekenstein reaction, but the low specificity and hot-alkaline condition within this method usually cause the generation of undesirable byproducts, such as galactose, tagatose, iso-saccharinic acid and colored compounds [3]. Therefore, researchers have turned efforts to develop enzyme-induced catalysis for lactulose synthesis, outpacing the inherent constraints of chemical synthesis [4].
Cellobiose 2-epimerases (CE, EC 5.1.3.11) belong to the $N$ -acyl-d-glucosamine 2-epimerase (AGE) superfamily, which can perform an epimerization function for converting lactose to epilactose and an isomerization function for biotransforming lactose to lactulose [5]. As compared to the chemistry synthesis routine, this enzyme is ideally suited to industrially produce lactulose, since its application goes cost-effectively and efficiently without generating more byproducts [6]. The reported CEs that can synthesize lactulose relate to Caldicellulosiruptor obsidiansis CE (CoCE) [7], Dictyoglomus turgidum CE (DtCE) [8], Caldicellulosiruptor saccharolyticus CE (CsCE) [9] and Dictyoglomus thermophilum CE (DitCE) [10]. The majority of CEs exhibit a representative α6/α6-barrel structure composed of six inner helices and six outer helices. The catalytic sites are formed by the residues of H188, H247, H377, E312, E250, E191, W308 and W372, and the enzymatic catalysis follows the “cis-enediol intermediate” mechanism [11]. Interestingly, strong isomerization performance is mostly observed by CEs originating from thermophilic microorganisms [4]. Among them, CsCE is regarded as the optimal candidate, since it could produce $408~\mathrm{g/L}$ lactulose using ${700}\ \mathrm{g/L}$ lactose with the yield and productivity achieving $58\%$ and $204~\mathrm{g/L/h}$ , respectively. Nonetheless, the biocatalytic toolbox of thermophilic CEs for synthesizing lactulose is still scarce, and more novel CEs should be discovered.
Protein engineering has been used as a powerful tool to modify wild CsCE for benefitting practical applications [12]. Shen et al. [13] applied a semi-rational design to obtain mutant E161D/N365P, whose half-life $(t_{1/2})$ and catalytic efficiency $(k_{\mathrm{{cat}}}/K_{\mathrm{{m}}})$ were 4 and 1.29 times those of the original enzyme. Shen et al. [5] conducted random mutagenesis to obtain the activity-improved R5M/A12S/I52V/F231L/ K328I mutant, which could push the lactulose yield up to $76\%$ without generating epilactose. These mutations were found mostly at the surface region and possessed a longrange structural effect on stability and catalysis. Feng et al. [14] found that the composition of flexible $\upalpha_{6}/\upalpha_{7}$ loop in CsCE had an impact on epimerization and isomerization activity, and higher isomerization activity was observed especially for V177D and I178D mutants. Furthermore, Wang et al. [15] replaced CsCE flexible $\upalpha_{6}/\upalpha_{7}$ loop with
Rhodothermus marinus CE loop 180–190, and the mutant RmCE showed a 2.2 and 1.3 times increase in the isomerization activity and catalytic efficiency, respectively. Thus, the consensus can be summarized from these instances, the successful mutations in these cases are located mostly away from the activity center and especially close to the enzymatic surface, and the flexible loops of CEs are associated with activity, stability, and lactulose synthesis.
Recently, a series of in silico methods have been developed to predict the hot spots for enzymatic stabilization or activity improvement. FoldX is a commercial software that can calculate the enzymatic change in unfolding free energy (ΔΔG) caused by amino acid substitution and is particularly applicable to predicting the effect of mutations on enzymatic stability [16]. Unlike directed evolution necessarily using high-throughput screening, the usage of FoldX is prone to construct a small and refined mutation library through complicated calculations and can quickly disclose the optimum candidates to be mutated [17]. For example, Buß et al. [18] designed 11 mutations for ω-transaminase using FoldX, the mutant G98M among which exhibited an increase in melting temperature $(\mathrm{T}_{m})$ by $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ over that of the wild-type enzyme. Wang et al. [19] screened four positive mutants (S142A, D217V, Q239F, and S250Y) and further combined them, and the $t_{1/2}$ of the quadruple mutation was increased by 41.7- fold compared with the original enzyme.
In this work, we first used NCBI database mining combined with structure–function analysis to obtain an unidentified CE scaffold. Then, the computer-aided rational design using FoldX was applied to ameliorate enzymatic thermostability, and some additional improvements may also take place once the mutation was within some crucial area of the candidate. Finally, the enzymatic application for lactulose synthesis was investigated and the mechanism of higher production of the mutant was deeply analyzed. This research highlights searching for an unknown CE and inferring a FoldX method to discover a mutant associated with better heat-resistant and catalysis performances. The obtained results were interesting and instructive for improving the industrial potential of more CEs.

Materials and methods

Materials and strains
Alpha-lactose, lactulose and epilactose were purchased from Aladdin Reagent Inc. (Shanghai, China). Primer design and DNA sequencing were proceeded by Tsingke Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). The Bio-Rad purifier and materials used for protein purification were obtained from Bio-Rad (Hercules, USA). Others such as isopropyl $\upbeta$ -d1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG), kanamycin, $\mathrm{pET}28\mathrm{b}(+)$
plasmid, E. coli BL21(DE3) host were preserved in our laboratory. The used reagents were of analytical grade unless otherwise stated.

Mining unidentified CE
The sequence blast in the NCBI database was conducted using CsCE (NCBI accession number YP_001179132.1) as the query sequence, and the result is shown in Table S1. Multiple sequence alignment was performed using ClustalX and displayed using ESPript software, respectively. The mined enzymes (Caldicellulosiruptor changbaiensis, CcCE, GenBank WP_127351080.1; Caldicellulosiruptor hydrothermalis, ChCE, GenBank WP_013402241.1; Caldicellulosiruptor morganii CE, CmCE, GenBank WP_045170066.1) were codon optimized and chemically synthesized by Xuguan Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). The synthesized genes were submitted into GenBank with accession no. OQ621768, OQ621769 and OP562893, respectively. The nucleotide acid sequences of synthesized genes are shown in Table S2 and their amino acids sequences are exhibited in Table S3. Then, the synthesized CE genes fusing with $6\times$ His-tag at the $3^{\circ}$ terminal were incorporated into $\mathsf{p E T}28\mathsf{b}(+)$ and transformed into $E$ . coli BL21(DE3), respectively.

Induction expression of recombinant CE
The $E$ . coli strain containing CE was cultivated in LB medium-containing $50~\upmu\mathrm{g/mL}$ kanamycin at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . After the culture $\mathrm{\DeltaOD}_{600}$ achieved 0.6–0.8, the induction expression started by adding a final concentration of $0.1\mathrm{mM}$ IPTG into the culture and shifting the cultivation temperature to $28^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . The process continued for $12\mathrm{~h~}$ , and the recombinant cells were harvested via centrifugation at $8000\times g$ for $10\mathrm{min}$ .

Activity assay
The activity assay system consisted of $200~\mathrm{mM}$ $\upalpha$ -lactose, an appropriate amount of biocatalyst (recombinant cells or purified enzymes), and $50~\mathrm{mM}$ HEPES buffer $\mathrm{(pH}7.5)$ in a total volume of $5~\mathrm{mL}$ . The reaction proceeded at $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $20\mathrm{min}$ and terminated on ice for $10~\mathrm{{min}}$ , and the obtained reacting liquid after centrifugation at $8000\times g$ for $10~\mathrm{{min}}$ was properly diluted and subjected to high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis. One unit (U) of CE isomerization activity toward lactose was defined as the amount of enzyme required to produce $1\upmu\mathrm{mol}$ lactulose per min under this condition.
Disaccharides were detected using an Agilent HPLC system (Milford, USA) and a refractive index detector (Milford, USA). The analytical column was the Asahipak Shodex VG-50-4E column $(250\times4.6~\mathrm{mm}$ , $5\mathrm{mm}$ ) (Tokyo, Japan).
The mobile phase was the mixture of acetonitrile, methanol and water according to the volume ratio of $50\%{:}25\%{:}5\%$ . The column temperature was $40~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , the injection volume was $10~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ , and the flow rate was $1\ \mathrm{mL}/\mathrm{min}$ , respectively. The retention times of lactose, epilactose and lactulose were determined to be 9.5, 8.6 and $7.6\mathrm{min}$ , respectively.

Homology modeling and molecular docking
The homology structure of $\mathbf{C}\mathbf{m}\mathbf{C}\mathbf{E}$ was generated using Alphafold 2.0 server [20, 21] and the X-ray diffraction model of CsCE (4z4l) as the template. The intermediate I of $\alpha$ -lactose with the opened glucose ring (Fig. S1) is the substrate during the limiting step of catalysis [22], whose 3D structure was generated using Chem 3D ultra 12.0 software. AutoDockTools was used to prepare the protein and ligand, water molecules were removed and polar hydrogen atoms were added. The grid box was set to contain the crucial residues of H377, H188 and H247. Docking was conducted with AutoDock Vina and the docking conformation with the lowest binding energy was confirmed for the following analysis. PyMol software was applied to present the docking pose for visualized observation.

Selection of probable mutation sites
FoldX 4.0 algorithm was applied for predicting mutations. Using the generated homology model of $\operatorname{CmCE}$ and setting the in silico temperature at $298\mathrm{~K~}$ , all the 395 amino acids of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ were subjected to computer-aided saturation mutagenesis using the FoldX-function BuildModel. Other options were set to default. The top 20 mutations with the lowest $\Delta\Delta\mathbf{G}$ scores were selected. To minimize the negative effect of mutating on enzymatic activity, the mutations that were located within a 5 Å scope from the activity center were excluded from the 20 mutations, and the remaining ones underwent site-directed mutagenesis.

Site‑directed mutagenesis
The extracted pET28b/CmCE plasmid was used as the PCR template. The designed primers are shown in Table S4 and S5. $50~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ PCR mixture consisted of $25~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ $2\times$ Phanta Max Buffer containing $\ensuremath{\mathbf{M}}\ensuremath{\mathbf{g}}^{2+}$ , $1~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ dNTPs, $2~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ forward and $2~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ reverse primer, $1~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ template, $1~\upmu\mathrm{L}$ Phanta Max Super-Fidelity DNA Polymerase and appropriate $\mathrm{ddH}_{2}\mathrm{O}$ . The PCR reaction was performed as preheating at $95~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $5~\mathrm{min}$ , reacting for 30 cycles $(95^{\circ}\mathrm{C}15\mathrm{~s~}$ , $57^{\circ}\mathrm{C}15\mathrm{~s~}$ , $72^{\circ}\mathrm{C}6\mathrm{min}$ ), and extension at $72^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $10\mathrm{min}$ . The PCR products were treated with Dpn I (Thermo Fisher Scientific, US) and transformed to $E$ . coli BL21(DE3) for expression. The recombinant cells were harvested via centrifugation at $8{,}000\times g$ for $10\mathrm{min}$ .

Protein purification
One gram of the harvested recombinant cells was resuspended in $20~\mathrm{mL}$ of $50~\mathrm{mM}$ HEPES buffer $\mathrm{(pH}\ 7.5)$ and sonicated at $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $30\mathrm{min}$ (50 W, 1 s working, 2 s resting). The soluble supernatant was collected via centrifugation at $8000\times g$ for $10\mathrm{min}$ and then loaded onto a $10\mathrm{mL}$ one-step Nickel-NTA superflow column. Following a $10{-}500\ \mathrm{mM}$ imidazole gradient elution utilizing combining binding buffer $(20\mathrm{mMNaH}_{2}\mathrm{PO}_{4}$ , 300 mM NaCl, $\mathrm{pH}7.5\rangle$ and elution buffer (20 mM ${\mathrm{NaH}}_{2}{\mathrm{PO}}_{4}$ , $300\ \mathrm{mM\NaCl}$ , $500~\mathrm{mM}$ imidazole, $\mathrm{pH}7.5\AA$ ), the target protein was collected based on the $A_{280}$ absorption and dialyzed against $20\mathrm{mM}$ HEPES buffer $\mathrm{(pH}7.5)$ . The purity and concentration of protein were analyzed using SDS-PAGE and Bradford dye-binding methods, respectively [23].

Screening stability‑improved mutation
The $T_{15}^{50}$ of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ (the temperature for retaining half of the original activity after incubation for $15~\mathrm{min}$ ) was first determined. The remaining activities of purified $\mathrm{CmCE}$ after incubating at $40{-}95^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $15\mathrm{min}$ were measured and fitted to a four-parameter Boltzmann sigmoidal function reformed with the Levenberg–Marquardt iterative algorithm $(y=a+(b-a)/1+e(x-x_{\mathrm{o}})/k)$ [24], from which the $T_{15}^{50}$ of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ was calculated to be $79.2^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ .
Then, $\mathbf{0.5~g}$ harvested recombinant cells containing mutated CEs were resuspended in $20~\mathrm{mL}$ of $50\mathrm{mM}$ HEPES buffer $\mathrm{(pH}7.5\mathrm{)}$ and sonicated as previously. Then, $100\upmu\mathrm{L}$ of each lysate was transferred into a 24-well plate, respectively, and the plate was heated at $79.2^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $15~\mathrm{min}$ . The relative remaining activity of each lysate was calculated using the original activity of each enzyme as a control [25]. The lysate with residual activity higher than that of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ was considered potentially positive.

Characterization of CmCE and D226G
On the basis of the activity assay method, the enzymatic optimal temperature was determined via measuring activities at different temperatures ranging from 40 to $90^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . The optimal $\mathrm{\pH}$ was calculated via measuring activities at different pHs between 5.0 and 9.0 with sodium citrate buffer (pH 5 to 6), PIPES buffer $\mathrm{\DeltapH}6$ to 7.5), HEPES buffer $(\mathrm{pH}7.5$ to 8) and glycine–NaOH buffer $\mathrm{\Phi_{pH}}8$ to 9). The maximum activity of each enzyme served as $100\%$ . The determination of $t_{1/2}$ values was conducted in two steps. First, each enzyme was incubated at 65, 70 and $75^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for an appointed time, and their residual activities were detected, respectively. Second, the primary research has shown that this reaction abided by the first-order kinetics, and then, the rate constant for $\mathrm{CmCE}$ and D226G was calculated using Eq. ln(instantaneous activity/initial activity) ${\bf\omega}=-{\bf\gamma}k{\bf t}$ and corresponding $t_{1/2}$ values were calculated from Eq. $t_{1/2}{=}0.693/k$ . The $\mathrm{pH}$ stability was detected by incubating the enzyme at $\mathrm{pH}5.0{-9.0}$ and $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 3 days, and then, the residual activities were measured and represented as a percentage of the initial activity.

Kinetic parameters’ analysis
The kinetic parameters of $\mathbf{C}\mathbf{m}\mathbf{C}\mathbf{E}$ and D226G were determined using the standard activity assay at $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ except for adjusting lactose concentration from 50 to $800~\mathrm{mM}$ , and the $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ and $V_{\mathrm{max}}$ values were calculated via fitting with the Michaelis–Menten plot using Origin 2018.

Biotransformation for synthesizing lactulose
The reacting system was composed of $1.5\mathrm{mg/mLCmCE}$ or D226G, $100~\mathrm{g/L}$ lactose and an appropriate HEPES buffer $\mathrm{(pH}7.5)$ in a total volume of $20~\mathrm{mL}$ . The reaction was carried out at $70~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $135~\mathrm{min}$ , and samples were taken at specific intervals and underwent ingredients analysis using the aforementioned HPLC detection.

Molecular dynamics (MD) simulation
MD simulation was performed using YASARA software with an Amber19 force field. The simulated temperature was $343\mathrm{~K~}$ . After energy minimization, all the systems were first equilibrated in the NPT ensemble and then in the NVT ensemble. Each system was subjected to 100 ps MD simulation, and the simulation time was 30,000 ps in total. All the systems were solvated with TIP3P waters in a cubic box, and the minimal distance between each protein and the edge of the box was $0.8~\mathrm{{nm}}$ . Root-Mean-Squared Distance (RMSD) and Root-Mean-Square Fluctuation (RMSF) values for backbone atoms were calculated and exhibited using Origin 2018, respectively.

Statistical analysis
Unless specifically noted, all experiments in this study were performed in triplicate. Analysis of variance was performed using the SAS program. The least significant difference was computed at $p{<}0.05$ .

Results

Mining unidentified CE acting on lactose
To supplement the scarce CE library for synthesizing lactulose, herein, the NCBI database mining was performed using the sequence of biochemically characterized CsCE as the query sequence [26], and the top ten enzymes sharing the highest identity are exhibited in Table S1. It was observed that SN. 1–9 enzymes were quite similar to CsCE, but SN. 10 Caldicellulosiruptor morganii CE dropped much in identity toward CsCE $(70.8\%)$ . Among them, Caldicellulosiruptor obsidiansis (CoCE) (SN. 9) has been reported [7]. During this narrow selection section, the screened enzymes should differ from the reported CEs as possible, and the candidates should undergo double-checking to confirm their validity of non-characterizing in the literature. Accordingly, three unidentified AGE family epimerases/isomerases originating from Caldicellulosiruptor changbaiensis (CcCE, $95.4\%$ identity), Caldicellulosiruptor hydrothermalis (ChCE, $91\%$ identity) and Caldicellulosiruptor morganii (CmCE, $70.8\%$ identity) were found (Table 1). The multiple sequence alignment (Fig. 1) indicated that the catalysis residues H188, H247, H377, E312, E191, E250, W372 and W308 [11][11] were entirely conserved in these enzymes.
The recombinant strains containing potential CE were cultivated to measure their expression. SDS-PAGE analysis (Fig. 2A) showed that recombinant CcCE, ChCE and $\mathrm{CmCE}$ strains all generated soluble bands compared to that of control, and $\mathrm{{CmCE}}$ showed a higher expression among these enzymes. Then, these potential CEs producing strains were subjected to an actual biotransformation experiment (Table 1). It was unexpected to find that $\mathrm{CmCE}$ having the lowest identity to $\mathbf{CsCE}$ obtained a lactulose yield of $34.2\%$ , higher than that of $22.7\%$ of $\mathbf{CcCE}$ and $19.4\%$ of ChCE, respectively. This interesting finding inspired us to select CmCE as a candidate for further research.

Prediction and screening of mutants with enhanced thermostability
In addition to activity, the thermostability of $\mathbf{C}\mathrm{m}\mathbf{C}\mathbf{E}$ is another considerable parameter. $\mathrm{CmCE}$ with higher thermostability can maintain the integrity of its structure and function at relatively high temperatures, which would favor improving the reaction rate and reducing the viscosity of the reaction system [28]. The free energy of unfolding $(\Delta\mathbf{G})$ of a target protein is important for protein stability [29]. FoldX can predict the change in the $\Delta\Delta\mathrm{G}$ of a protein upon mutation, and the negative $\Delta\Delta\mathbf{G}$ value of a mutation (particularly $\leq-\ 0.5\ \mathrm{kcal/mol})$ indicates that mutation yields stability [30]. Herein, using FoldX, each residue in $\mathrm{CmCE}$ (395 amino acids total), respectively, underwent virtual saturation mutagenesis and $\Delta\Delta\mathrm{G}$ calculation, and the top 20 mutations with the lowest $\Delta\Delta\mathbf{G}$ values are listed in Fig. 3A and Table S6. It was notable that D226G had the minimum $\Delta\Delta\mathbf{G}$ of $-4.08\mathrm{kcal/mol}$ , and the $\Delta\Delta\mathbf{G}$ of other mutations fell within the range from − 3.94 to $-2.14\mathrm{kcal}/$ mol. Furthermore, the $\upalpha$ -lactose intermediate I was docked into CmCE modeling structure. Since the mutations within $5\mathring{\mathrm{A}}$ of the active site should be excluded to probably avoid compromising activity, the mutations H377Y, R56M, N184I and E250Q were thus deleted from the analysis. Finally, the mutations D226G, S124M, A271M, D335R, N54M, T194M, K305Y, Q23M, L367P, T282L, Q140M, C256M, A279M, S180P, C382L, and G291C were selected for experimental verification.
The 16 mutants were constructed and expressed in $E$ . coli BL21(DE3). Owing to the $T_{15}^{50}$ value of purified $\mathrm{CmCE}$ (Fig. 2B) was measured to be $79.2~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Fig. S2), all the mutants were incubated at $79.2^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $15~\mathrm{min}$ , and their thermostabilities were detected and compared with that of CmCE (Fig. 3B). CmCE retained $48.3\%$ of its initial activity, whereas mutants G291C, S124M, L367P and $\mathrm{T}282\mathrm{L}$ exhibited $10.1\%$ , $11.4\%$ , $33.7\%$ and $13.7\%$ of relevant initial activity. Other mutants A271M, D335R, N54M, T194M, K305Y, Q23M, Q140M, C256M, A279M, S180P and C382L merely retained less than $5\%$ of relevant initial activity. To be noted, D226G was the only positive mutant, which could retain $69.8\%$ of its original activity after incubation.

Site‑directed saturation mutagenesis at 226 position
Site-directed saturation mutagenesis on site 226 was also conducted. The thermostabilities of these mutants were analyzed and the results are also exhibited in Fig. 3B. Mutants D226H, D226M, D226Y, D226C and D226Q retained similar remaining activities with CmCE, whereas other mutants exhibited different degrees of decreased residual activities.

Characterization of CmCE and D226G
After the purification of D226G (Fig. 2B), the properties of D226G and $\mathbf{C}\mathrm{m}\mathbf{C}\mathbf{E}$ were investigated in detail. They showed similar activity tendencies under a varied reaction temperature and possessed an accordant optimum temperature of $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Fig. 4A). When incubating at 65, 70 or $75^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , D226G deactivated more slowly than $\mathrm{CmCE}$ . The $t_{1/2}$ values of D226G were determined to be 149.7, 117.7 and $74.8\mathrm{min}$ , higher than 71.8, 52 and 33 min those of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ , respectively (Fig. 4B). The $T_{15}^{50}$ of D226G was also increased to $82~^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Fig. S2). Both enzymes showed the optimum activity at pH 7.5 (Fig. 4C), but D226G exhibited slightly better stability during pHs ranging from 6.5 to 8.0 after incubation at $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 3 d (Fig. 4D).

Determining kinetic parameters of CmCE and D226G
The kinetic performances of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ and D226G toward lactose were calculated at $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Table 2). Compared with the $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ of $79.9\ \mathrm{mM}$ of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ , the lower $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ of $60.2~\mathrm{mM}$ of D226G indicated that enzymatic affinity acting on lactose was enhanced upon mutation. Although the Vmax and kcat values of D226G were slightly lower than those of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ , the $1.27\ \mathrm{mM^{-1}\ m i n^{-1}}$ of $k_{\mathrm{{cat}}}/K_{\mathrm{{m}}}$ of D226G was still higher than $1.01\ \mathrm{mM^{-1}m i n^{-1}}$ that of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ .

Discussion
To supplement the scarce toolbox of CEs for synthesizing lactulose, NCBI database mining synchronously with enzymatic structure–function analysis was conducted to successfully screen $\mathrm{CmCE}$ . Characterization revealed that its optimal $\mathsf{p H}$ of 7.5 was comparable to that of $\mathbf{CsCE}$ [33] and CoCE [7], but differed not much from 7.0 that of DtCE [8] and DitCE [10]. Its optimal temperature of $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ fell within the optimum temperature range of $70–80\ ^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ of above CEs, indicating that it could be regarded as a refractory CE. However, its $t_{1/2}$ values at 65, 70, and $75^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ as well as $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ value were not as good as other CEs. In the biotransformation of $100~\mathrm{g/L}$ lactose, the yields of lactulose and epilactose merely reached $35.9\%$ and $25.4\%$ , respectively.

The low thermostability of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ has been a bottleneck for its application. Many strategies can be implemented to ameliorate its stability, such as increasing hydrogen bonds [34], filling the hydrophobic cavity core [35], and introducing proline substitution [36]. Notably, ΔΔG is a decisive feature associated with the stability of mutations, which can be calculated using FoldX and is particularly used for predicting mutation that is improved or decreased stability [19]. Herein, we identified a positive mutant D226G with improved thermostability as compared to $\mathrm{CmCE}$ . Interestingly, the residues of other CEs at the 226 position happened to be glycine (Fig. 1). Based on the consensus principle, residues appearing most frequently among homologs at a specific position probably contribute to enzymatic stability [36]. Considering this, the D226 residue was further subjected to saturation mutagenesis to ensure the integrity of the screening results [37]. Coincidentally, the result still revealed that D226G presented the highest thermostability. It seemed that the rational FoldX design can assist researchers to jump over the tedious high-throughput screening process and directly predict the optimum mutation, which could substantially speed up thermostability engineering in the practical processes [18].
The solvent-accessible position 226 was located within the flexible surface ${\upalpha_{8}/\upalpha_{9}}$ loop and far away from the activity center. To investigate the thermostability-improving factors due to mutating, MD simulations were conducted. The RMSD results (Fig.  6A) showed that D226G possessed more stable protein backbones than $\mathrm{CmCE}$ , which was consistent with the experimental results. The RMSF results (Fig. 6B) exhibited that the general fluctuation trend of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ and D226G was similar except for the flexible loop region ranging from 222 to 242. Slightly decreased RMSF values after mutating meant that the temperature resistance of this region was enhanced (probably due to the introduction of G226). Such behavior of RMSD and RMSF during analyzing thermostability proteins was also found in other enzymes, such as $(R)$ -selective transaminase [38] and lipase [39]. On the other hand, the simplest L-Gly enables rotation around $\Phi$ and $\boldsymbol{\Psi}$ due to lacking $\upbeta$ -carbon [40], which might confer the $\upalpha_{8}/\upalpha_{9}$ loop of D226G extreme flexibility. Unlike forming more hydrogen bonds with some specific loop residue after mutating [41], either D226 or G226 could form merely one hydrogen bond with neighboring Y221, respectively (Fig. 7A, B). However, this hydrogen bond in D226G rotated its direction toward the enzyme due to the small side chain of Gly, and such changes made the enzymatic structure visually compact. Based on the analysis, the substitution of D226 with G226 favored the enzyme with lower G, more flexibility and rigidification of the $\upalpha_{8}/\upalpha_{9}$ loop, which would be beneficial to enzymatic stability.
To gain insight into the impact of the mutation D226G on enzymatic activity, the size and shape of substrate-binding pockets of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ and D226G were analyzed (Fig. 7C, D and Table S7). The volume of the pocket decreased from $708\mathring{\mathrm{A}}^{3}$ of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ to $223.8\mathring{\mathrm{A}}^{3}$ of D226G, the surface area reduced from 923.2 of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ to $239.5\mathring\mathrm{A}^{2}$ of D226G, and the depth decreased from 23.6 of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ to $10.4\mathring{\mathrm{~A~}}$ of D226G. The space around the substrate in the active center of $\mathbf{C}\mathrm{m}\mathbf{C}\mathbf{E}$ seemed long and narrow, which might result in insufficient steric exclusion to limit substrate orientating [42]. In contrast, the smaller and shallower catalysis pocket was found in D226G. Furthermore, the intermolecular interactions with the substrate in $\mathrm{CmCE}$ and D226G are shown in Fig. 7E, F, and a total of ten hydrogen interactions in D226G over five of those in $\mathrm{{CmCE}}$ demonstrated that the substrate-binding affinity in D226G was stronger [43], consistent with the observed decreased $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ and increased $k_{\mathrm{{cat}}}/K_{\mathrm{{m}}}$ . Furthermore, based on the “cis-enediol intermediate” catalysis mechanism of CEs, the rate-limiting step of the entire reaction is H377 providing a proton to the O1 atom of substrate intermediate I [4]. In D226G, H377 could form two hydrogen bonds with the substrate, one of which was just with the O1 of the substrate, this behavior might be beneficial for stabilizing the protonation process. Altogether, the smaller and shallower catalysis pocket as well as the strong substrate-binding capability might contribute to the catalytic activity of D226G.

The improvements of D226G synergistically promoted enzymatic biotransformation (Fig. 5), higher $k_{\mathrm{{cat}}}/K_{\mathrm{{M}}}$ value afforded a much faster synthesis velocity of epilactose $(0{-}15\ \mathrm{min})$ and lactulose $(0{-}120\ \mathrm{min})^{\cdot}$ , and the better thermostability might confer D226G better temperature tolerance during the whole process [44]. Finally, higher yields of lactulose of $45.5\%$ and epilactose of $22.6\%$ for D226G were obtained. In addition, the ratio of lactulose to epilactose in the reacting system of D226G (2:1, mol/mol) was not the same as that in $\mathrm{CmCE}$ (1.4:1, mol/mol). This result accorded the viewpoint that the residue composition and the shape of the flexible loop influence both enzymatic epimerization and isomerization [14]. In the literature, the catalyzed residue H377 provides a proton to the C2 of substrate for epimerization or provides a proton to the O1 atom for isomerization, but the detailed mechanism about how H377 directs the catalysis before and after mutating in this work still needs to be clarified, using more crystal structures of $\mathrm{CmCE}$ containing substrate/product [4]. Based on the result of literature [45], it is also feasible to semi-rationally redesign the enzymatic substrate-binding pocket to realize the transition from epimerization to isomerization.

Conclusion
This research applied a gene mining strategy to screen novel $\mathrm{CmCE}$ , and proved the design of the protein using foldX to increase protein thermostability. Interestingly, a mutation that modified the 226 residue on the $\upalpha_{8}/\upalpha_{9}$ surface loop was discovered to promote not only thermostability but also lactose affinity, catalysis efficiency and lactulose yield. To our knowledge, this is a scarce instance regarding successfully engineering the $\upalpha_{8}/\upalpha_{9}$ loop of bifunctional CEs, which would be considered as a reference for more CEs engineering.
