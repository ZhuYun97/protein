Abstract
The detection, identification, and adaptation method based on data snooping (DIA-datasnooping) is commonly used to deal with outliers in the Gauss–Markov model. However, the application of DIA-datasnooping might be limited in case of multiple outliers. In this contribution, the Maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate is applied to the DIA framework, and a DIA method based on MAP (DIA-MAP) is proposed. Based on the prior distribution of gross errors, DIA-MAP chooses the hypothesis with the maximum posterior probability to conduct the detection and identification of outliers. To this end, a hyperparameter determination method based on supervised learning is proposed to find suitable priors for gross errors. With the priors of gross error, DIA-MAP provides a unified DIA process for both single and multiple outliers. Also, the prior can be flexibly adjusted rather than fixed to be uniform, so that the DIA method can be adapted to different application cases. Finally, a set of new evaluation indices for the DIA method with multiple outliers is defined, including True Positive Rate (TPR) which describes the detectability for outliers and True Negative Rate (TNR) which denotes the acceptance ability for inliers. Experimental results of GNSS positioning examples verified that the performance of the proposed DIA-MAP method is superior to the conventional used methods when dealing with multiple outliers.

Introduction
In GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) data processing, Least-squares (LS) estimations are commonly used to achieve unbiasedness and minimum variance. However, it is based on the prerequisite that realistic functional and stochastic models are used (Koch 1999; Teunissen 2006; Li et al. 2011). Actually, outliers occur inevitably in GNSS data collection which generally results in biased estimators for LS. There are well-established and workable methods for detection, identification, and adaptation (DIA) of outliers. The most commonly used DIA method finds its origin in the pioneering work of data snooping (Baarda 1967, 1968), see also, e.g., Alberda (1976), Kok (1984), and Teunissen (1985). Recently, a unifying framework that captures the combined estimation and testing scheme of the DIA estimator has been introduced by Teunissen (2018).
The DIA method first makes hypothesis testing between the null and a group of alternative hypothesis models. In the detection procedure, statistics that usually follow a normal distribution, $\tau$ distribution, $\chi^{2}$ distribution, $F$ distribution, or others are tested to accept or reject the null hypothesis (Pope 1976; Koch 2015; Kok 1984; Xu 1987; Lehmann 2013; Yang et al. 2013). The identification is a kind of model selection with the purpose to choose the most trustworthy alternative hypothesis which can correctly exclude the unmodeled outliers. Finally, the adaptation of parameter estimation is conducted under the identified model aiming to remove or relieve the potential bias on the unknown parameter estimates. Among the DIA methods, DIA-datasnooping (Zaminpardaz and Teunissen 2019, 2020; Yang et al. 2021) is the most classical and has been widely used in GNSS practices.
However, with the development of GNSS, there are typically large datasets that are very likely to contain multiple outliers. Although test statistics can also be generated for the case of multiple outliers (Kok 1984; Ding and Coleman 1996; Teunissen 2000), direct comparison of different individual tests statistics becomes irrational when the number of suspected outliers is not equal in all of these tests (Teunissen and Montenbruck 2017). Furthermore, it has been proved that outliers with different numbers and positions could yield the same residuals in an LS adjustment, which means that the information on outliers is not fully contained in the residuals (Baselga 2011). As a consequence, the DIA for multiple outliers based solely on residuals would be hampered by masking and swamping effects. That is if the preset maximum suspected outlier number is large, the statistical test tends to declare more outliers than there are (Beckman and Cook 1983). Besides, multiple outliers can mask each other easily, which increases the difficulty of detection and identification (Rousseeuw and Leroy 1987).
To solve this problem, DIA-datasnooping, theoretically developed for a single outlier, is usually consecutively implemented to handle multiple outliers via processing one outlier after the other, which is known as iterative DIA-datasnooping. From another aspect, the identification of multiple outliers can be implemented by model selection methods, in which the observations can be modeled as being free of outliers or accounting for various specific outlier patterns with respect to number, affected observations, and stochastic properties. In this field, the $p$ value has been proposed and elaborated to select the optimal model among multiple alternative hypotheses with different numbers of suspected outliers (Lehmann and Lösler 2016; Teunissen and Montenbruck 2017).
A different approach, Bayesian inference, can also be implemented for DIA (Box and Tiao 1968; Guttman 1973; Gui et  al. 2007, 2011). Maximum a posteriori estimate (MAP) is a classic point estimate method in Bayesian statistics (Koch 2007; Murphy 2012). In this contribution, a DIA method based on MAP (DIA-MAP) is proposed by applying the MAP estimate to the DIA method for multiple outliers. Based on the prior distribution of gross errors, DIA-MAP chooses the hypothesis with the maximum posterior probability to conduct the detection and identification of outliers. To this end, a hyperparameter determination method based on supervised learning is proposed to find suitable priors for gross errors. Furthermore, a set of evaluation metrics (Murphy 2012; Brown 2018) for DIA methods has been introduced.
The rest of this contribution is organized as follows. First, the DIA principles are reviewed and some commonly used DIA methods are enumerated. Then, the theory and application of DIA-MAP are presented. And a set of evaluation indices for DIA method are introduced. In addition, numerical examples of GNSS are used to assess the performance of the proposed DIA-MAP method. Finally, the conclusions of this study are made.

DIA-datasnooping is widely used as one of the standard algorithms for processing a single outlier. However, there are typically large datasets that are very likely to contain multiple outliers. Therefore, some multiple outliers processing methods are also developed.
Iterative DIA-datasnooping: As a conventional solution, the iterative DIA-datasnooping procedure has been widely used for processing multiple outliers. Without fixing the number of outliers beforehand, iterative detection, identification, and adaptation are conducted under the assumption of a single outlier. Once an outlying observation is identified, LS adjustment is reconducted by excluding the identified observation, and the $H_{0}$ -based inferences are corrected. However, this approach is not theoretically rigorous, since only a single outlier is assumed and tested during each iteration of the DIA-datasnooping procedure, but this assumption is overthrown immediately in the next iteration. Furthermore, as the number of outliers increases, the success rate of detection and identification will decrease gradually (Klein et al. 2021).

Determination of gross errors priors
The prerequisite for the validity and optimality of DIA-MAP is a reliable prior gross error rate $\varepsilon$ , which is also called the hyperparameter. Although the statistical characteristics of outliers would be diverse in different scenes, it is rational to assume that the occurrence of gross error for a certain type of sensor in a certain situation would have the same prior distributions. In actual applications, the prior gross error rate could be given empirically or obtained through statistical inference from a large quantity of data. For example, the prior probabilities of fault in one satellite per approach $P_{s a t}$ which is obtained through long-term statistics are introduced in the RAIM (receiver autonomous integrity monitoring) and ARIAM (advanced RIAM) algorithms (Blanch et al. 2010, 2015).
Supervised learning, commonly used in machine learning (Murphy 2012), can also be used here to estimate the gross error rate $\varepsilon$ . With a set of training data and a loss function, then the optimal parameters can be obtained by minimizing the loss function. To this end, although the real gross error rate $\varepsilon$ is unknown, using supervised learning, one can find an appropriate estimated $\hat{\varepsilon}$ for a certain case. Taking GNSS positioning as an example, if a set of training data consisting of raw observations and site coordinates is obtained in advance, the positioning RMSE can be set as the loss function, and the optimal $\hat{\varepsilon}$ is the one that can minimize that loss function through DIA-MAP. Then, the estimated $\hat{\varepsilon}$ can be applied as a prior in the same scene.

Evaluation indices for multiple outliers
When dealing with multiple outliers with unknown numbers, the conventional used evaluation method for a single outlier would become impractical. In this section, a set of evaluation indices which has been widely used in statistics is introduced to assess the performance of the DIA method for multiple outliers.
Generally, in a two-class problem, the confusion matrix, also known as the error matrix, is given in Table 1 (Murphy 2012; Brown 2018). One can see that there are a total of four reality-decision outcomes in a binary decision problem, i.e., true positive (TP), true negative (TN), false positive (FP), and false negative (FN). While the diagonal elements of the matrix represent the number of correct decisions, the nondiagonal elements denote the number of mistake decisions.
If we consider the outliers as the positive samples and the inliers as the negative samples, the confusion matrix can be also used in the multiple outliers testing problem. On the one hand, the number of correctly reject outliers is denoted as TP, and the number of correctly accept inliers is denoted as TN. On the other hand, the number of wrongly reject inliers is denoted as FP, and the number of wrongly accept outliers is denoted as FN.

Experiment
In this section, GNSS Single Point Positioning numerical examples are used to evaluate the performance of the proposed DIA-MAP and other contrast methods. Without loss of generality, the experiments were conducted with different gross error rates $\varepsilon\big(10^{-3},10^{-2},10^{-1}\big)$ . In each simulation, gross errors which obey the Bernoulli distribution with parameter $\varepsilon$ were added to the simulated inlying observations. Thus, instead of only focusing on the epochs with outliers, epochs without any outliers are also included in the total sample. In addition, different sizes of gross errors are also taken into consideration in which $\nabla_{i}$ are ranged from 1 to 20 times the standard deviation (STD) of the corresponding observation $\sigma_{i}$ . The evaluation indices values were obtained via a Monte Carlo Simulation (MCS) with simulation time $1\times10^{6}$ independent experiments. Such a simulation allows for a large number of independent realizations of the same experiment and a controlled environment, which is difficult to ensure when using real data.

Analysis under " unknown
In this subsection, for evaluating the performance of DIAMAP in actual applications, the determination of gross error rate $\varepsilon$ is considered. As well, since geometry also impacts the performance of the DIA methods, one-day data of BeiDou Navigation Satellite System (BDS) SPP examples at an International GNSS Service (IGS) station are used, in which the sample intervals are 30 seconds.
As presented before, when the true value of $\varepsilon$ is unknown, the estimated value $\hat{\varepsilon}$ can be obtained using supervised learning. Specifically, in the training phase, the RMSE of the unknown parameter can be set as the loss function, and one can adjust the parameter $\hat{\varepsilon}$ to minimize the RMSE of the DIA-MAP method so that obtaining an optimal estimation. Then, in the test phase, the optimal $\hat{\varepsilon}$ can be applied in the GNSS positioning of the same scene. For this example, the data are randomly divided into two parts, $5\%$ for training and $95\%$ for testing.
Fig. 4 shows the variation of RMSE with $\hat{\varepsilon}$ , under different gross error rate $\varepsilon$ and different gross error magnitude $\nabla_{i}$ . Table 2 lists the estimated points and intervals with a $5\%$ variation of $\hat{\varepsilon}$ which minimize the average RMSE. Generally, as $\hat{\varepsilon}$ enlarges, the average of RMSE for DIA-MAP first decreases and then increases. It is because an over small $\hat{\varepsilon}$ would reserve too many outliers, while an overlarge $\hat{\varepsilon}$ would discard too many inliers, both of which will degrade the positioning accuracy. Fortunately, one can always find an optimal $\hat{\varepsilon}$ , which can minimize the average RMSE of the DIA-MAP. However, the optimal $\hat{\varepsilon}$ could not always be equal to the truth value of $\varepsilon$ , and it usually decreases as the gross error size increases. This is because larger gross errors are easier to be detected and the optimal $\hat{\varepsilon}$ turns to be lower to alleviate the overfitting effect of the UMPI test and avoid dropping more inlying observations at this time. Fortunately, the RMSE is not sensitive to the change of $\hat{\varepsilon}$ within a certain interval, which means the estimated unknown parameter would not be affected by a small deviation of the $\hat{\varepsilon}$ . Because the performance of DIA-MAP is a result of the combination of both prior and observation information.

Furthermore, one can see from the right panels of Fig. 6 that as the gross error size enlarges these DIA methods show notable robustness for outliers. Among them, DIAdatasnooping always shows the poorest performance, since it is based on the assumption of a single outlier. Different from the case of $\nabla_{i}=30\sigma_{i}$ , the performance of DIA- $p$ value becomes worse than iterative DIA-datasnooping, since the calculation of the $p$ value relies on the precision of numerical integration. When confronted with test statistics of large magnitude caused by large gross errors, the comparison of $p$ values between different test statistics could be difficult. However, DIA-MAP always shows higher resistance to the effect of multiple outliers than those conventional used DIA methods, especially under a large gross error rate.
Fig. 6   Positioning RMSEs of five methods under different geometry (The different rows denote different gross error rates and the different lines denote different gross error sizes)
Table 3 summarizes the average RMSEs of all epochs for each method. It can be seen that the positioning accuracy of different methods shows different trends as the size and rate of gross errors change. We can find that, due to lack of robustness, the RMSE of LS always shows an increasing tendency when the gross error enlarges, regardless of the value of $\varepsilon$ . As for the DIA-datasnooping, iterative DIA-datasnooping, and DIA- $p$ value, the RMSEs remain in a stable trend with the growth of gross error size when $\varepsilon=10^{-3}$ . It means that when the gross error rate is relatively small, the conventionally used methods all show satisfactory performance on robustness. However, when $\varepsilon$ is relatively large or highdimension gross errors take up larger proportions, these methods cannot always bound the estimation errors. But for DIA-MAP, no matter how many outliers occur, the RMSE of DIA-MAP can always decline to a lower bound as the gross error enlarges. For example, when $\varepsilon=10^{-1}$ and $\nabla_{i}=50\sigma_{i}$ , DIA-MAP leads to improvements of around $71\%$ , $60\%$ , and $23\%$ over DIA-datasnooping, $\mathrm{DIA}{-}p$ value, and iterative DIA-datasnooping, respectively. In conclusion, the above results comprehensively verify the superiority of the proposed DIA-MAP over DIA-datasnooping, iterative DIAdatasnooping, and DIA- $p$ value for dealing with multiple outliers.

Conclusion
In this study, a DIA method based on MAP (DIA-MAP) is proposed. Based on the prior distribution of gross errors, DIA-MAP chooses the hypothesis with the maximum posterior probability to conduct the detection and identification of outliers. In this regard, a hyperparameter determination method based on supervised learning is proposed to find suitable priors for gross errors. Compared to the conventional used DIA methods, the proposed DIA-MAP shows the following advantages. With the priors of gross error, it provides a unified DIA process for single or multiple outliers. Also, the prior can be adjusted flexibly according to the specific applications, rather than fixed to be uniform. Besides, a set of evaluation indices for assessing the performance of the DIA method are introduced, including TPR which describes the detectability of outliers and TNR which denotes the acceptance ability for inliers.
GNSS SPP examples are used to evaluate the performance of the proposed DIA-MAP method. Besides the LS estimate, the proposed DIA-MAP method is also compared with the other three conventional used DIA methods, which are the DIA-datasnooping, iterative DIA-datasnooping, and DIA- $p$ value. Experiment results show that the robustness of these methods is all affected by the geometric strength. Because more visible satellite numbers indicate stronger geometric strength and therefore produce higher robustness. In addition, although all of these DIA methods have a certain ability of resistance to multiple outliers. As the rate and magnitude of gross errors increase, the robustness of the conventionally used methods would severely degrade. However, by taking advantage of the priors of gross errors, the DIA-MAP can always maintain satisfactory accuracy and stability, provided that reliable hyperparameters are acquired.
