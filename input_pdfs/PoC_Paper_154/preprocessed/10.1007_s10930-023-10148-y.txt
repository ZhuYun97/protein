Domain Shuffling and Site-Saturation Mutagenesis for the Enhanced Inhibitory Potential of Amaranthaceae α-Amylase Inhibitors
Ashwini S. Rane<sup>1,2</sup> · Vineetkumar S. Nair<sup>1</sup> · Rakesh S. Joshi<sup>1,2</sup> \(\circledcirc\) · Ashok P. Giri<sup>1,2</sup>
Accepted: 31 July 2023 / Published online: 19 August 2023 \(\circledcirc\) The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Science+Business Media, LLC, part of Springer Nature 2023

Abstract
Amaranthaceae \(\upalpha\)-amylase inhibitors (AAIs) are knottin-type proteins with selective inhibitory potential against coleopteran \(\upalpha\)-amylases. Their small size and remarkable stability make them exciting molecules for protein engineering to achieve superior selectivity and efficacy. In this report, we have designed a set of AAI pro- and mature peptides chimeras. Based on in silico analysis, stable AAI chimeras having a stronger affinity with target amylases were selected for characterization. In vitro studies validated that chimera of the propeptide from *Chenopodium quinoa* \(\upalpha\)-AI and mature peptide from *Beta vulgaris* \(\upalpha\)-AI possess 3, 7.6, and 4.26 fold higher inhibition potential than parental counterparts. Importantly, recombinant AAI chimera retained specificity towards target coleopteran \(\upalpha\)-amylases. In addition, to improve the inhibitory potential of AAI, we performed in silico site-saturation mutagenesis. Computational analysis followed by experimental data showed that substituting Asparagine at the 6th position with Methionine had a remarkable increase in the specific inhibition potential of *Amaranthus hypochondriacus* \(\upalpha\)-AI. These results provide structural–functional insights into the vitality of AAI propeptide and a potential hotspot for mutagenesis to enhance the AAI activity. Our investigation will be a toolkit for AAI's optimization and functional differentiation for future biotechnological applications.

1 Introduction
Amaranthaceae \(\upalpha\)-amylase inhibitors (AAIs) are knottin-type, small proteins of around 30 to 41 amino acids. They selectively interact and inhibit coleopteran \(\upalpha\)-amylases [1]. *Amaranthus hypochondriacus* \(\upalpha\)-amylase inhibitor (AhAI) is a well-studied inhibitor of the knottin type. AhAI comprises three antiparallel \(\upbeta\)-strands, five \(\upbeta\)-turns, and three disulfide bonds [2], forming hydrophobic and hydrophilic half after folding [3]. A cystine-knot fold formed by a network of disulfide bonds in AAIs makes them highly stable against temperature and peptidase or protease [4]. AAI
architecture and loop variations near the amylase substrate-binding groove provide specificity to enzyme-inhibitor interaction [5]. Structural analysis of *Tenebrio molitor* \(\upalpha\)-amylase (TMA) and AhAI complex depicted that reactive site residues of AhAI interact with active site residues of \(\upalpha\)-amylase. It involves multiple H-bonds and hydrophobic interactions resulting in the blockage of the sugar-binding pocket [3]. Polar and charged residues are mainly involved in these interactions. AAIs are competitive inhibitors of coleopteran amylases, emphasizing the importance of AAI's conserved Arg-7 in interaction [1]. AAIs structure can be divided into five segments (S1 to S5) between cysteine residues. The mutant analysis highlighted the importance of S1 in inhibition and S2 in folding [2].
AAIs share the typical architecture of signal, pro-, and mature peptides [1, 6]. Amaranthaceae and Apocynaceae \(\upalpha\)-AIs, along with spider and snail venom toxins [7] belonging to cluster-5, have a propeptide in between signal and mature peptide [1, 5]. Propeptide is the hallmark of cystine-knot proteins. Most active \(\upalpha\)-AI proteins extracted from plants consist of only mature peptides, while signal and propeptide might be lost during the processing as a part
of post-translational modifications [8, 9]. AAI propeptides are highly variable in length and sequence composition; hence, their in-detailed molecular function and biochemical relevance are enigmatic. Few speculations by researchers suggest the role of propeptide in mature peptide folding. Price-Carter demonstrated that in \(\omega\)-conotoxins, propeptide doesn't play a role in the folding [10, 11]. Furthermore, it was hypothesized that the conotoxin propeptide and protein disulfide isomerase catalyzes the oxidative folding of the conotoxin precursor [12]. It has been documented that members of cluster-5 have different origins and biological functions, but they share molecular functions. Many are often ion channel or enzyme inhibitors [13–15]. Propeptides' role is assumed to be versatile and differs depending on the protein family [16]. Still, their molecular and biological function is largely undetermined.
Here, we have used protein engineering approaches to understand propeptide roles and improve the inhibition potential of AAIs. Precursor (propeptide with mature peptide), pro-, and mature peptides were individually cloned. Purified recombinant proteins were evaluated for inhibitory potential against various amylases. Inhibition profiles of precursor and mature proteins were compared. Further, criss-cross constructs were made by exchanging pro and mature peptides of AAIs. In silico interaction studies were performed to generate structural insights into the enzyme-inhibitor complexes. AhAI's site-saturation mutagenesis was performed to understand the positional relevance of residues in specificity and activity against Coleopteran and mammalian \(\alpha\)-amylases. Potential criss-cross and mutant \(\alpha\)-AIs were recombinantly expressed and compared for inhibitory potential with parental counterparts. We anticipate that this study may shed light on structural features and their functions in AAIs. Also, this work might pave the path for engineering and directed evolution of AAIs for biotechnological applications.

2 Materials and Methods

2.1 RNA Isolation, cDNA Preparation, and PCR to Clone AAIs
The amino acid sequences of AAIs (NCBI ID: XP_021744610.1, accession number: MT627440) and a predicted AAI from Beta vulgaris (BvAI) (GenBank accession No. XP_010672947.1) were retrieved from the NCBI database. Another A. hypochondriacus inhibitor sequence (primary transcript name: AHYPO_000022-RA) was retrieved from the available whole-genome data [17]. Leaf tissues of Amaranthus hypochondriacus, Alternanthera sessilis, Chenopodium quinoa, and Beta vulgaris were used to isolate RNA using Spectrum Total Plant RNA Kit (Sigma Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). cDNA was made using M-MLV Reverse Transcriptase (Promega, Madison, WI, USA). Multiple sequence alignment of BvAI was done with reported AAIs using the Clustal omega tool [18], and phylogenetic analysis was performed by MEGA-X software [19]. As an outgroup, Actin from Dictyostelium discoidum (UniProtKB ID: P07830) was used. The EsPript3 server was used to illustrate secondary structural features [20].

2.2 Cloning, Expression, and Purification of Recombinant AAIs
ORFs of precursor \(\alpha\)-AIs from Amaranthus hypochondriacus (AhAI2_P), Chenopodium quinoa (CqAI_P), and Alternanthera sessilis (AsAI_P); mature peptides of BvAI (BvAI_M) and Amaranthus hypochondriacus (AhAI_M); propeptides of CqAI (CqAI_PP) and AhAI (AhAI_PP) were amplified by designed primer pairs (Supplementary Table S1). Amplicons were cloned in expression vector pET28a (Novagen, Madison, WI, USA), consisting of N-terminal 6X His-tag.
To clone chimaera of pro- and mature peptides, crisscross constructs AsCqAI and CqBvAI were made by overlap extension PCR using overhang primers (Supplementary fig. S4a, Supplementary table S1). Amplicons of CqAI_M, BvAI_M, AsAI_PP and CqAI_PP were obtained for further annealing and PCR reactions. The amplified products (AsAI_PP with CqAI_M and CqAI_PP with BvAI_M) were then mixed, annealed and extended by five PCR cycles. Moreover, AsCqAI and CqBvAI were amplified using a forward primer of AsAI, CqAI, and a reverse primer of CqAI, BvAI, respectively. Amplicons were cloned in the pET28a vector. All the recombinant plasmids were amplified and sequence characterized.
Recombinant plasmids and empty pET28a (vector control) were further transformed into SHuffle T7 cells of Escherichia coli (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA) [21] for protein expression (proteins produced by precursor constructs can be referred to as precursor proteins). When the optical density of cells at \(600\;\mathrm{nm}\) and at \(30\;^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) reached 0.6, cultures were chilled and induced with \(1\;\mathrm{mM}\) Isopropyl\(\upbeta\)-d-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG). The induced culture was incubated at \(16\;^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\), \(180\;\mathrm{rpm}\) for \(14\;\mathrm{h}\). Cell pellets were obtained by centrifugation, followed by the resuspension in lysis buffer (20 mM Tris, 300 mM NaCl, and 5% glycerol; pH 7). For cell lysis, the resuspended pellet was subjected to a sonication cycle of 8 s on 12 s off cycles at 45% amplitude for \(10\;\mathrm{min}\) on ice. Cell debris was separated by centrifugation. The clear cell lysate was kept for binding to previously equilibrated Ni–NTA resin for \(1\;\mathrm{h}\) at \(4\;^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\). This protein-bound slurry was then loaded on the Biorad gravity column. The column was washed with an increasing concentration of imidazole (10 mM, 25 mM, and 50 mM) in the lysis buffer. Protein was eluted with a buffer containing 250 mM
imidazole. Bradford method was used for protein quantification with BSA as standard. Proteins were visualized on \(15\%\) SDS-PAGE gel and confirmed by Western blotting, as mentioned in [1].

2.3  Determination of Molecular Weight by MALDI
The molecular weight of recombinant proteins was estimated using MALDI analysis. For this analysis, recombinant proteins of AsAI_P, CqAI_P and CqBvAI \(\left(1\ \mathrm{mg/ml}\right)\) were mixed with \(10\,\mathrm{mg}\) sinapinic acid (Sigma–Aldrich, St Louis, CA, USA) per ml of \(50\%\) ACN and \(0.1\%\) TFA in a 1:5 ratio. The dry droplet method was used to spot the protein-matrix mixture on the MALDI target plate. AB SciexMALDI-TOF/ \(\mathrm{TOF}^{\mathrm{TM}}5800\) system (Framingham, MA, USA)was used for analysis with positive linear mode from 2 to \(20\,\mathrm{kDa}\) [1].

2.4  Inhibitory Assays and Kinetics of Recombinant α‑Als
In our earlier reports, we evaluated AAIs' specificity towards coleopteran amylases. Hence, we performed the inhibitory activity of recombinant inhibitors against recombinant Tribolium castaneum \(\upalpha\) -amylase (TcAmy) [1, 22]. All inhibitory assays were carried out using DNSA assay as described earlier [1]. Further, various other amylases viz, recombinant Callosobruchus chinensis \(\upalpha\) -amylase (CcAmy), Helicoverpa armigera \(\upalpha\) -amylase (HaAmy) [23]; while other commercially available amylases from the porcine and human pancreas (PPA and HPA), human saliva (HSA), Aspergillus oryzae (AoAmy), Bacillus licheniformis (BlAmy), diastase and \(\upbeta\) -amylase from Hordeum vulgare (HvAmy) (Sigma Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) were subjected to inhibitory assays to check the specificity profile of newly identified AAIs.

2.5  Ex Vivo Callosobruchus chinensis Adult Amylase Inhibition
Callosobruchus chinensis insects were grown on mung beans under controlled conditions [22]. Adults were collected and crushed in liquid nitrogen to obtain fine tissue powder. Tissue powder was then dissolved in a \(20\;\mathrm{mM}\) citrate phosphate buffer (pH7.1:6 ratio) and incubated overnight at \(4\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}[22]\) . After centrifugation, the supernatant was used as a crude CcAmy source. The inhibition assays were carried out with mature, precursor and criss-cross \(\upalpha\) -AIs using concentrations inhibiting \(100\%\) rcCAmy [1, 22]

2.6  Inhibitory Potentials and Kinetics of Recombinant α‑Als
The \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) of new recombinant AAIs was determined using various inhibitor concentrations at fixed enzyme (TcAmy)
and substrate concentrations. The \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) values of mature, precursor and criss-cross \(\upalpha\) -AIs were compared. \(\mathrm{K_{i}}\) of precursor AAIs was determined by keeping fixed enzyme and inhibitor concentrations with varying substrate concentrations in each reaction. The same reactions were carried out with different AI concentrations [1]. All the assays were carried out using independent biological replicates.

2.7  Molecular Modeling of α‑AIs and Protein– protein Docking with Target Amylases
The library of protein chimeras was made by exchanging propeptides of AhAI, AhAI2, AsAI, CqAI, and BvAI with mature peptides of AhAI2, CqAI, BvAI, and AsAI in possible combinations. Three-dimensional structural models of precursor and chimeric AAIs were predicted using the I-TASSER server [24]. The protein sequences of TcAmy and CcAmy were retrieved from the NCBI database (GenBank accession: NM_001114376.1 and KU641476, respectively). SWISS-MODEL [25] was used to model TcAmy, CcAmy [using TMA crystal structure (PDB ID: 1CLV, chain A) as a template] and BvAI_M [using AhAI (PDB ID: 1CLV, chain I) as a template]. The inhibitor binding with target amylases was predicted using the Z-dock server [26]. The energy of docked complexes was minimized using UCSF chimera 1.10.2 software [27]. These docked complexes were visualized using the PyMol molecular visualization system (https://www.pymol.org; DeLanc Scientific LLC, USA). The interaction energy was calculated using the PDBe-PISA server [1].

2.8  Saturation Mutagenesis and Interaction Analysis of AhAI and Mutants
The amino acid sequence of AhAI was retrieved from the NCBI database (GenBank ID: KU641477). A 32 amino acid mature peptide was selected. Each of the 32 amino acids was individually mutated by replacing them with the remaining 19 amino acids by DUET server [28]. Mutants with positive free energy \(\mathrm{(ddG)}\,{\geq}\,0.5\;\mathrm{kcal/mol}\) were selected for further analysis.
Crystal structures of TMA (PDB ID: 1CLV, Chain A, TMA) and HPA (PDB Id: 1HNY) were retrieved from Protein Data Bank (https://www.rcsb.org/). The Duet server predicted the mutant structures [28]. Selected mutants with \(\mathrm{ddG}\,{\geq}\,0.5\;\mathrm{kcal/mol}\) and wild-type AhAI (PDB ID: 1CLV, Chain I) were docked with TMA, TcAmy and HPA by the Z-DOCK server [26]. Docking was performed by selecting the catalytic residues of \(\upalpha\) -amylases and corresponding inhibitor binding residues from the AhAI_TMA complex (PDB Id: 1CLV). Energy minimization and calculation were done. The energy of H-bonds (1H-bond \({\approx}0.5\,\mathrm{kcal/mol}\) ) and salt bridges (1 salt bridge \({\approx}0.3\;\mathrm{kcal/mol}\) ) added to the
energy calculated by PDBe-PISA. The mutant complexes with higher free energy than the wild-type were selected for further analysis. The mutant complex with the highest dG was also analyzed by docking the same mutant with CcAmy. Differences in the interactions of wild-type and mutant complexes were studied in silico using PyMol visualization software. The six mutants having higher affinity to TcAmy were selected. These mutants' possible combinations were made in AhAI to create double and triple mutants and then docked with TcAmy to check interaction energies.

2.9  Molecular Dynamics Simulation of TcAmy – AAI Complexes
The energy-minimized docked complexes of different amylase inhibitors with TcAmy were used for MD simulation studies (chimera, precursor, mutant and mature inhibitors in complex with TcAmy). The input files for MD simulation were generated using the CHARMM-GUI server with the Monte-Carlo method. Further, the energy minimization was performed with the steepest descent algorithm using 50,000 steps and was set to stop when the maximum force per nm decreased below \(1000\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}\) [29]. It was then equilibrated for 1250 ps at \(303.15\mathrm{~K~}\) using Nosé–Hoover thermostat. GROMACS(version 2020.1) performed a 10 ns production run with Nosé–Hoover thermostat, Parrinello-Rahman pressure coupling and default parameters. The RMSD and RMSF plots of the protein complex were created and visualized using XMGRACE (version 5.1.25). These protein complexes were uploaded to the PDBePISA server to study the interaction between the proteins in the complex [30, 31].

2.10  Site-Directed Mutagenesis and Experimental Analysis of AhAI and Mutants
Forward and reverse mutagenic primers containing the desired mutation were designed (Supplementary Table S1). Site-directed mutagenesis of AhAl was performed using Quikchange Lightning site-directed mutagenesis kit as per manufacturer protocol (Supplementary Table S4) (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, USA). The amplified products were digested by DpnI at \(37\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) for \(5\,\mathrm{min}\) to digest the parental (i.e., non-mutated) supercoiled double-stranded DNA. The mutated plasmids were transformed into \(E\) . coli TOP 10 competent cells. Colony PCR and sequence characterization were performed to check the insert with the mutation's presence.
The recombinant plasmids (wild-type, empty vector and mutants N6M, N6A) were transformed and proteins were expressed as mentioned for precursor proteins. Further, cell pellets were resuspended in a buffer ( \(20\;\mathrm{mM}\) Tris, \(300\;\mathrm{mM}\) NaCl, \(0.5\%\) Triton \(\mathrm{X}{-}100\;\mathrm{pH}\;8.7\rangle\) . The cell lysate was prepared by centrifugation after sonication. The recombinant
protein was purified from the cell lysate using the Ni–NTA chromatography protocol that was previously described. Most bacterial proteins have pI between 4 to 7; the bacterial protein contaminants were precipitated by changing the \(\mathrm{pH}\) to 4 by diluted HCl. At \(\mathrm{pH}\,4\) , the samples were gently agitated for \(120\;\mathrm{min}\) in the cold. The precipitated proteins were then removed by centrifugation at \(12000\;\mathrm{rpm}\) , \(4\,^{\circ}\mathbf{C}\) for \(30\ \mathrm{min}\) . Again the \(\mathrm{pH}\) was neutralized by NaOH [32]. Protein from the supernatant was loaded on \(18\%\) Tricine-SDS PAGE and checked for inhibitory activity against TcAmy.
\(\upalpha\) -Amylase inhibition assays and \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) studies were carried out as previously described for other recombinant inhibitors. A comparison of \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) values for wild-type and mutant proteins was made. rTcAmy, rCcAmy, HPA (Sigma Aldrich, MO, USA), and HSA (Sigma Aldrich, MO, USA) were subjected to inhibitory assays by these inhibitors.

3  Results

3.1  BvAI is A Competitive and Specific Inhibitor of Coleopteran Amylases
The putative \(\upalpha\) -AI, BvAI_M had \(78\%\) identity with earlier characterized AhAI_M [2]. The phylogenetic analysis illustrated that BvAI belongs to the clade of AAIs and closely resembles AhAI. As expected, knottin type inhibitors of Amaranthaceae and Apocynaceae form a separate clade than Poaceae family inhibitors (Supplementary fig. S1). BvAI shares similar secondary structural characteristics and disulfide bond topology with other AAIs (Fig. 1a). The BvAI_M protein (Supplementary Fig. S2 a-c)competitively inhibits TcAmy with an \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) value of \(41.06\,\upmu\mathrm{M}\) and Ki value of \(19.31\,\upmu\mathrm{M}\) . It specifically inhibits coleopteran \(\upalpha\) -amylases while activities of other amylases were found to be unaffected (Supplementary Fig. S3 a-c).
Most reactive site amino acids of AAIs are conserved, except Tyr-28 in other inhibitors is replaced with Phe-28 in BvAI (Fig. 1a). In AhAI, Arg-7 is involved in key interaction with active site residues of TMA [3]. This contact is found to be conserved amongst other AAIs [1], including BvAI. The replacement of Arg-7 by Leu abolished the \(\upalpha\) -amylase inhibitory activity of AhAI; highlighting the importance of Arg residue in AAIs [2]. In the case of AhAI, the Arg-7 directly forms a salt bridge with Asp-287 of TMA along with two hydrogen bonds with two of the catalytic residues [3]. In silico interaction, analysis showed that positively charged Arg-7 and Arg-30 of BvAI interact with negatively charged catalytic residues of TcAmy and CcAmy. In contrast, polar residue Thr-24 of BvAI interacts with one of the catalytic residues of CcAmy (Supplementary Fig. S3 d and e). 3.2  Precursor AAs Possess an α-Amylase Inhibitory Potential
3.2 Precursor AAs Possess an α-Amylase Inhibitory PotentialAAI genes are comprised of pro and mature peptides; mature peptides mainly contribute to the inhibitory activity of AAI [1]. Likewise, AI extracted as an active protein from Amaranthus hypochondriacus tissue consists of only mature peptide parts [9]. Due to a lack of evidence, the role of propeptide in the generation of mature AAI is still enigmatic. The length of AAI propeptides varies from 17 to 55 amino acids (Fig. 1a). As the propeptide sequences are highly diverse, it is more difficult to predict or annotate their function in AAI structure or activity. To elucidate propeptide functionality, we cloned and purified precursor proteins (Supplementary Fig. S2a) (Fig. 1b). Expression of these proteins was further confirmed by Western blotting (Fig. 1c). Molecular weight of AsAI_P and CqAI_P proteins was 9694 Da \((\mathrm{m/z})\) and 11761 Da \((\mathrm{m/z})\) , respectively (Fig. 1d and e) as determined by MALDI-TOF analysiswhich correlates with
their theoretical molecular mass 9853 Da and 11944 Da respectively.

3.3  Differential Interactions of Mature and Precursor α‑AIs with TcAmy
Precursor \(\upalpha\) -AIs showed specific \(\upalpha\) –amylase inhibition potential. It has been observed that \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) values for mature and precursor \(\upalpha\) -AIs differ (Table  1). \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) of AhAI2 mature (AhAI2_M) protein against TcAmy was found to be \({\sim}22~\upmu\mathrm{M}\) , while for AhAI2 precursor protein (AhAI2_P) it was \({\sim}67~\upmu\mathrm{M}\) . Conversely, CqAI_P and AsAI_P have \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) values of \(57.60\,\upmu\mathrm{M}\) and \(6.85~\upmu\mathrm{M}\) , respectively, which are much less than the \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) of CqAI_M and AsAI_M \((103.1~\upmu\mathrm{M}\) and \(89.14~\upmu\mathrm{M})\) (Table 1). Furthermore, precursor \(\upalpha\) -AIs retained the specificity toward Coleopteran amylases (TcAmy and CcAmy) like mature proteins (Fig. 2a). These recombinant precursor AAIs showed negligible or no inhibition of other amylases (Fig. 2a). Inhibition kinetics indicated that the precursor AAIs showed the competitive mode of inhibition of TcAmy. \(\mathrm{K}_{\mathrm{i}}\) values of AhAI2_P, AsAI_P, and CqAI_P are \(35.22\,\upmu\mathrm{M}\) , \(2.37\,\upmu\mathrm{M}\) , and \(21.73\,\upmu\mathrm{M}\) , respectively (Fig. 2b–d). Similar to their mature counterpart, these precursor AAIs were found to be active against crude CcAmy (Fig. 2e). Inhibition potential of AAIs with crude and recombinant CcAmy was found to be different (Fig. 2e). This difference may be due to various ex vivo factors in crude CcAmy such as multiple isoforms.
Recombinant precursor proteins, AsAI_P, CqAI_P, and AhAI2_P possess \(\upalpha\) -amylase inhibitory activity, despite highly variable propeptides. Furthermore, to assess the inhibitory activity of propeptides, propeptides of AhAI and CqAI were cloned and expressed. These recombinant proteins do not show inhibitory activity against TcAmy. This indicates that propeptide alone does not have intrinsic amylase inhibitory activity. Still, it may play a crucial role in regulating the structure–function of mature AAIs.
The molecular mechanism behind differential inhibition caused by mature and precursor AAIs was elucidated by in silico analysis. Predicted models of precursor \(\upalpha\) -AIs showed that the pro and mature peptides form two distinct domains (Fig. 3). Precursor \(\upalpha\) -AIs complexed withTcAmy showed
tight binding and packing of mature peptides in the catalytic groove of \(\upalpha\) -amylase. Binding of mature peptide in active site results in inaccessibility of the substrate, thus inhibiting its activity. In a few cases, it has been observed that propeptide also interacts at the interface of the enzyme-inhibitor complex, assisting the complex interaction (Fig. 3).This difference in interaction and interacting residues of mature and precursor \(\upalpha\) -AIs with TcAmy may cause the difference in their \(\mathrm{IC}_{50}\) values. Precursor AAI establishes multiple ancillary contacts with TcAmy and may strengthen mature peptide interaction (Table 2). As expected, polar contacts made by the mature part of AAI are predominately in the active site region. At the same time, the propeptide of precursor AAI interacts at the interface region (Table 2).

3.4  Chimeric AAIs Showed Enhanced Inhibitory Potential Without Compromising Specificity
The difference in inhibition potential and molecular interaction of mature and precursor AAIs with TcAmy hinted at generating the chimera library with multiple combinations of mature and propeptide regions. We developed criss-cross constructs to understand (i) the importance of propeptide in differential inhibition and (ii) the possibility of enhancing the inhibitory potential of natural proteins by exchanging propeptides of mature AAIs. Predicted models of criss-cross constructs showed that a change in any of the two domains changes the conformation of the adjacent domain (Fig. 4a).
Furthermore, to understand the effect of propeptide in chimera structure and its impact on the interaction with target amylase, we performed in silico study by creating the combination of mature peptide (CqAI) with varying propeptides (Supplementary fig. S5). The interaction analysis showed that along with the change in AI conformation, the total interface area and interacting residues also changed (Table 3). Interaction differences are majorly contributed by variation in the propeptide region of \(\upalpha\) -AIs with its contacts with TcAmy (Supplementary Fig. S5). Propeptides of AsCqAI and Ah1CqAI do not interact with TcAmy (Supplementary Fig. S5d and S5e). In rest complexes, both proand mature peptides interact with TcAmy (Supplementary Fig. S5a-c). Further, it has been observed that change in propeptide causes alteration in interacting residues of mature and propeptide against TcAmy (Supplementary Fig. S5 f-n). Saturation mutagenesis of AhAI by the Duet server showed many stable mutations (171 out of 608) (Supplementary Table S5). Surprisingly, C8 and C23, which form the second disulfide bond in protein, led to ddG values \(>1\) when replaced with V, L, P, I, M, and P, M, respectively. 4  Discussion
The specificity of AAIs towards coleopteran amylases attracted attention to use them as pest control agents. The gene architecture of AAIs consists of three domains, viz. signal, pro- and mature peptide. The function of propeptide
is unknown. The inhibition studies of precursor peptides were carried out to determine how propeptide's existence with mature peptide affects the inhibitory potential of AAIs. We observed a discrepancy in inhibitions by precursor and mature AAIs due to the formation of differential contacts with TcAmy. In silico interaction studies showed that the combination of pro- and mature peptides changes the conformation of the inhibitor resulting in changes in the interacting residues of \(\upalpha\)-amylase and \(\upalpha\)-AI. These changes altered the potency of the inhibition without altering specificity.
Moreover, it was mentioned that the propeptide of cystine-knot \(\upalpha\)-AIs help in the biosynthesis of mature \(\upalpha\)-AIs [8]. This is in accordance with proteins produced by CqAI_P and AsAI_P have a stronger binding affinity with TcAmy than their mature counterparts. The reverse is the case for AhAI2. Like AhAI2, the reduction in binding affinity was also observed in the precursor protein of \(\omega\)-conotoxin from the venom of Conus snails than in its mature counterpart [11]. Also, recombinant \(\upalpha\)-AIs produced from mature constructs are active [1]. Hence, propeptide may not be essential to produce active recombinant mature inhibitors in vitro. In the case of \(\omega\)-conotoxins, it is proved that they do not need propeptide for folding. The mature form of a few \(\omega\)-conotoxins has sufficient information for correct folding [10, 11]. The mutagenic study of AhAI demonstrated that segment 2 of AhAI_M is essential for folding as mutations in it affected the folding of mature peptides [2]. However, the inhibition profiles of proteins produced by mature and precursor peptides vary greatly. The attachment of propeptides to mature peptides changed the efficiency of protein. This may be because of a change in protein conformation, interaction energies due to a variation in interacting residues, enzyme and inhibitor interface area, number of H-bonds, and salt bridges formed. However, there is a possibility that in plants, the N-terminal propeptide may direct cellular routing [16] or mediate interactions with regulatory proteins [33]. factors, they may function in the storage of AIs at specific locations [16] or be involved as stress sensors [6]. However, the role of propeptide in these inhibitors is still a mystery.
Few precursor AAIs proved more effective than their mature counterparts hence domains of AAIs were shuffled to make superior inhibitors. Recombinant proteins produced by chimeric constructs CqAI-AI and AsCqAI showed tighter binding to TcAmy and hence more efficient than their parental counterparts except for CqAI_P. They also retained the specificity towards coleopteran amylases. Hence with the help of the domain shuffling approach potency of inhibitors was improved. Another protein engineering approach used was site-saturation followed by site-directed mutagenesis, which produced specific inhibitors superior to the natural ones. We observed that AhAI accommodated mutations N6M and N6A retaining activity. This is a replacement of polar residue (N) with a Y-shaped side chain by non-polar residue with a long linear side chain (M) or non-bulky, chemically inert methyl functional group as a side chain (A). This 6th position 'N' may play an important role in inhibition as it is conserved in other AAIs except for AhAI2. But its replacement with 'M' produced hyperactive mutants. At high temperatures, 'N' in proteins undergo deamidation, which may result in protein misfolding and inactivation. Therefore, this replacement of 'N' may be responsible for increasing the stability of the protein. Hence, these mutants may prove beneficial in making pest control formulations. According to previous reports, replacing N6 by V or L does not abolish \(\upalpha\) -amylase inhibitory activity, but activity is abolished when P3 or K4 is deleted. AhAI remained active after mutations W5I and N6L [2]. AhAI became inactive when R7 was replaced with L; this tells the importance of R7 as it is conserved in all AAIs. Removal of G9 impaired inhibitory activity of AhAI. Insertion of I and L between K11 and M12 abolished the inhibitory and folding ability of AhAI. Replacements of P10A/M12A/G14A/P16A do not abolish the inhibitory activity of AhAI [2]. This tells us that AAIs can accommodate many mutations. Here, we have created superior inhibitors with different protein engineering techniques. Through this, we can develop different inhibitors with altered biological activities. This study has added a few more inhibitors to the list of \(\upalpha\) -AIs. The \(\upalpha\) -AIs produced by protein engineering are hyperactive and specific toward coleopteran amylases. Here, the specificity is achieved for plant–insect interaction; hence, safe for the ecosystem. These inhibitors can be used to control coleopteran pests by making formulations for pest control or generating transgenic crops. The application of combinations of these different inhibitors could be effective in controlling the emergence of resistance. It can prove to be advantageous as an addition to pest control technology. Another added advantage of these inhibitors is non-toxicity hence imparting less evolutionary
pressure by retarding growth rather than killing insects. This study has led to the efficient development of eco-friendly pest control technology for stored grains.

5 Conclusion
This study highlights the importance of AAIs as stored grain pest control agents due to their specificity towards coleopteran amylases. Here we identified and characterized new Coleopteran specific \(\upalpha\) -AI from B. vulgaris. The difference in inhibition potentials of precursor and mature \(\upalpha\) -AIs were compared and studied. In silico analysis revealed that the combination of pro- and mature peptides changes the conformation of the inhibitor resulting in the alteration of the interacting residues of \(\upalpha\) -amylase and \(\upalpha\) -AI. These changes vary the potency of the inhibition without altering specificity. The domain shuffling and mutagenesis produced superior inhibitors than the natural ones. These protein engineering techniques can be used to explore AAIs further. This study has led to the efficient development of eco-friendly pest control technology for stored grains.
