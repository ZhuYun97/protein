Abstract
Mobile CrowdSensing(MCS) has become a promising paradigm and attracted significant attention from academia, in which mobile users can complete various sensing tasks. In most existing works, the platform asssigns tasks based on the fairness of users, i.e., user's processing ability, to minimize assignment cost, which ignores the fairness of tasks. Although some works have considered tasks’ fairness, they still suffer from either of two limitations: (i) Fairness of the user and task cannot be guaranteed simultaneously; (ii) In real-world scenarios, the distance a user can travel in a certain period is limited, which affects the assignment performance. Motivated by this, we investigate the fairness task assignment problem under distance constraint. We argue that it is necessary to not only make full use of all users’ ability to process tasks (e.g., not exceeding the maximum capacity of each user while also not letting any user idle too long), but also consider distance constraint and satisfy the assignment frequency of all corresponding tasks (e.g., how many times each task should be assigned within the whole system time) to ensure a long-term, double-fair and stable participatory sensing system. We first model two fairness constraints simultaneously by converting them to user processing queue and task virtual queue. Then we propose a Fairness Task Assignment Strategy with Distance constraint(FTAS-D), which first utilizes Lyapunov optimization technology to find a feasible assignment solution, and then we introduce simulated annealing algorithm to iteratively find the best solution. Finally, extensive simulations have been conducted over three real-life mobility traces: Changchun/taxi, Epfl/mobility, and Feeder. The simulation results prove that the proposed strategy can achieve a trade-off between the objective of minimizing the cost and fairness of tasks and users compared with other baseline approaches.

1  Introduction
With the surging of smartphone sensing, wireless networking (Yang et al. 2010), and mobile computing techniques, Mobile CrowdSensing (MCS) (Ganti et al. 2011) has become a promising paradigm and attracted significant attention from academia. In MCS the platform recruits available users with smart devices(e.g., smartphones) (Lane et al. 2010) to actually go to a specific location to perform complex urban and social sensing tasks eventually to promote large-scale data collection (Guo et al. 2016). MCS has promising application prospects in many fields (Dutta et al. 2009; Kong et al. 2020; Xia et al. 2018; Aly et al. 2017; Xia et al. 2016), such as environmental monitoring, smart city, city sensing, and social networks. In most cases, in order to complete tasks effectively, we should assign tasks to suitable users, which raises the fundamental task assignment problem in MCS (Gong et al. 2018).
Most of the existing researches of task assignment focus on the different optimization requirements (Peng et al. 2021;
Wang et al. 2018b; He et al. 2014; Xiao et al. 2017; Miao et al. 2020), including maximizing the total profit, maximizing the amount of assigned tasks or minimizing assignment cost. For better effect, some works try to make a trade-off between assignment cost and the amount of assigned tasks (Li et al. 2019b; Yan et al. 2016; Yang et al. 2019). However, these approaches concentrate on the trade-off while ignoring each task’s required assignment frequency or each user’s processing ability. In many realistic settings, in order to facilitate the long-term stability performance of the MCS system, the platform needs to consider fairness when assigning tasks, i.e., the fairness of users and tasks. In other words, the user’s processing ability is usually limited which means that too many tasks assigned to him in a certain period may cause overload and of course is unfair to users. On the other hand, the large number of assigned tasks does not indicate that each task has good assignment frequency, which means that these approaches also cannot guarantee the fairness of tasks. Therefore, how to carry out fairness task assignment in Mobile CrowdSensing has become a key issue.
Several fairness task assignment approaches have been developed in MCS (Zhao et al. 2014; Sun et al. 2021; Gao et al. 2015; Lan et al. 2020). Sun et al. (2021) proposed a Cost-Fair Task Allocation (CFA) problem for the MCS scenario, which aims to allocate sensing tasks to users so that the sensing costs undertaken by all users are as balancing as possible. Gao et al. (2015) studied a general time-dependent and location-aware participatory sensing system to guarantee users’ participation-fair. However, the above studies are based on the fairness of users such as cost-fair and participation-fair and ignore that of tasks. In a realistic scenario, to guarantee the quality of sensing tasks, each task requires good assignment frequency. Moreover, the above literature does not take the user’s travel distance into account. In realistic scenarios, the distance that a user can travel in a certain period is limited. If a assigned task’s location is too far away from the user’s location or the total distance traveled to perform all assigned tasks exceeds the user’s distance constraint, the user would not reach the task’s location, which would lead to tasks failing to complete. To this end, in this paper, we go further in this direction and take the fairness of users and tasks into consideration, while ensuring the minimization of total assignment cost.
Next, we will use a motivation example to illustrate the distance-constrained fairness task assignment problem. In Fig.  1, there are 3 users $\{u_{1},u_{2},u_{3}\}$ and 5 tasks {s1, s2, s3, s4, s5} . The platform releases some location-based tasks continuously in $T$ time slots. Each task needs to be assigned a certain number of times in the entire system time in order to collect enough data to meet the requirements of each task’s assignment frequency. We regard the distance between the user and the task as the cost of completing a task. At the same time, in a time slot, each user has a current location and a reachable travel distance, each user can be assigned a limited number of tasks, which cannot exceed user’s processing ability and travel distance. Note that a task can only be assigned to one user in each time slot and is indivisible. It is intuitive to assign tasks to nearby users to minimize the current assignment cost in each time slot. In our example, in the current time slot we could assign task $s_{1}$ to the nearest user $u_{1},s_{2}$ to the user $u_{3}$ . However, this assignment approach just tries to minimize the current cost without considering the fairness of tasks and users (i.e., some tasks may be assigned too many times, i.e., $s_{2}$ , while the other tasks have no chance to be assigned. Similarly, some users may be continuously assigned tasks and overloaded soon, i.e., $u_{2}^{\phantom{}}$ ). Fig. 1 provides an example for the fairness of users and tasks, instead of assigning $s_{2}$ , we first assign $s_{1}$ and $s_{4}$ because they have more tasks in the assignment queue (task fairness). When assigning $s_{4}$ , we prefer to assign it to $u_{1}$ rather than $u_{2}$ , even though $u_{2}$ is closer to $s_{4}$ . This is because the number of tasks assigned to $u_{2}$ almost exceeds his processing ability (user fairness). In our example, $s_{5}$ cannot be assigned because it exceeds the distance constraint for any one user. Above all, the problem to be addressed in this paper is to propose a fairness task assignment strategy with distance constraint in each time slot with the purpose of minimizing the total assignment cost while considering the fairness of users and tasks as well as the distance constraint of users.
To tackle the above task assignment problem, we need to address the following challenges. Firstly, to make the most of each user and guarantee a good sensing quality for each task, we need to consider the fairness of users and tasks. So how to deal with the fairness constraints is the first challenge. Secondly, we need to assign tasks without violating the distance constraint of users, while minimizing the total cost and maintaining the fairness of users and tasks. Therefore, our problem is actually a constrained multi-objective optimization problem. It is challenging to find a reasonable task assignment strategy based on user’s distance constrain. More importantly, it is difficult to provide the assignment performance bound to the optimal solution. Hence, how to prove the effectiveness of the proposed task assignment strategy, i.e., the bound is the third challenge.
In this paper, to address the fairness constraints, we simulate them with queues, that is, the fairness constraint of users is converted to user processing queue, and the fairness constraint of tasks is converted to task virtual queue. Then the problem of ensuring the fairness of users and tasks is transformed into the problem of maintaining the stability of user processing queue and task virtual queue. As for the second challenge, we learn that Lyapunov optimization technique can achieve system stability and objective minimization by taking control actions in dynamic scenarios. Inspired by this, we use Lyapunov optimization technique to solve the above minimizescostandmaintainsfairness constrained multi-objective optimization problem and find a trade-off between minimizing the total assignment cost and maintaining the queue stability. Specifically, we jointly consider the double queues by expanding the traditional single queue based on original optimization model. To minimize the cost, we plan a shortest path to complete the tasks for each user. Then we could find a feasible assignment solution to the problem by optimizing assignment strategy that makes the Lyapunov drift-plus-penalty function minimization. Moreover, we further apply the simulated annealing algorithm. Specifically, we take the feasible solution based on Lyapunov optimization as the initial solution of the simulated annealing algorithm, and continue to iteratively find the best solution. Regarding the third challenge, we prove the gap between our method and the optimal solution through a rigorous mathematical proof, which also shows that the method is effective in minimizing the assignment cost and maintaining the stability of queues. Then, our contributions are as follows:

2  Related work
In this section, we introduce the existing works on fairness task assignment and Lyapunov optimization methods.

2.1  Task assignment
Mobile CrowdSensing (MCS) is a new framework that has emerged in recent years, recruiting users with mobile devices to physically travel to a specific location under certain restrictions to perform sensing tasks (Lane et al. 2010). Most of the existing works focus on task assignment. The researches are devoted to finding ways to achieve a certain goal of task assignment, e.g., maximizing the number of assigned tasks, maximizing the total profit of the platform, or maximizing the overall quality of assignments. For example, He et al. (2014) considered an MCS scenario involving position-dependent tasks. They designed an algorithm to maximize platform rewards under budget constraints. In Yan et al. (2016), the author proposed TaskMe, a method for selecting participants from multitasking MCS activities. The goal of TaskMe is to maximize the total number of completed tasks while minimizing the total distance of the user’s movement. Miao et al. (2020) proposed a probabilistic quality measurement model and designed a polynomial-time online assignment algorithm. However, many studies on task assignment in MCS put their focus on assigning tasks to users without considering the fairness of them. The fairness of the user refers to the full use of each user’s ability to process the task (not exceeding the maximum processing capacity of each user, nor letting a user be idle for too long), and the fairness of the task means that each task will have a certain frequency of assignment according to its characteristics (how many times need to be assigned in the entire crowdsensing system).
In the task assignment problem of MCS, compared with the above task assignment, fairness task assignment is more complex, and there are few researches in this field. The recent study (Sun et al. 2021) designed two algorithms to balance the perceived cost borne by users and meet the requester’s data reliability requirements for the cost fair task allocation (CFA) problem in the uncertain MCS scenario. Zhao et al. (2014) proposed a fair energy efficiency distribution framework that balances task fairness and energy efficiency, and makes a control decision in each time period. Lan et al. (2020) proposed Utility-Fairness Index (UFI) to evaluate the performance of the crowdsourcing platform, based on which they address the Fairness-Aware Task Planning (FATP) problem that aims at task planning, such that the utility is maximized while fairness is well maintained. However, most previous studies on fairness task assignment only considered single user fairness or task fairness, not both of them, which led to low-quality task assignment. Different from the above researcher, we consider the fairness of users and tasks simultaneously while minimizing the total assignment cost.

2.2  Lyapunov optimization
Lyapunov stochastic optimization technique is a network optimization theory developed by Neely (2010), and in recent years, a large number of studies have been conducted on the use of Lyapunov optimization methods to solve resource allocation problems. Fang et al. (2018) introduced a new stochastic control algorithm based on the Lyapunov optimization technique combined with the technique of weight perturbation that makes online decisions on computing resource allocation, which achieves both profit optimality and system stability. Zhang and Wang (2019) proposed a top-down optimal control module (TDOC) based on Lyapunov optimization to solve the task assignment problem of quality of service (QoS) goals applied to edge computing systems. Wang et al. (2020) studied the location-aware and location diversity based offline and online crowdsensing systems to maintain system stability and achieved a time average sensing utility arbitrarily close to the optimum. They further took into account the fair allocation of worker resources, and innovatively implemented Lyapunov optimization to handle the stochastic characteristics for online control policy. Yao et al. (2012) proposed a two-time-scale control algorithm based on Lyapunov optimization. The algorithm aims to reduce power costs and optimize the trade-off between power costs and delays in distributed data centers. Liu and Chang (2018) used a scheduling algorithm based on the Lyapunov drift plus penalty framework to maximize the secondary user (SU) while meeting various constraints involved in time-varying channels and traffic conditions. In order to solve the load sharing problem of mobile users, Li et al. (2019a) proposed a Lyapunov optimization framework to maximize the offloading utility under the premise of ensuring the stability of the queue. Li et al. (2014) designed an online control algorithm based on the Lyapunov optimization framework to optimize the tradeoff between system throughput and energy consumption. Han and Zhu (2014) used Lyapunov optimization technique to propose a profit maximization algorithm for the public sense platform. Realize arbitrarily close to the optimal time average profit, and maintain strong stability. However, the above studies either only consider one cohort, or even if there are double cohorts, each cohort has its own control strategy. Different from the above researches, we consider double queues, namely user processing queue and task virtual queue. We make a control decision in each time slot to maintain the stability of two queues at the same time.

3.1  System model
We first consider a practical scenario of MCS which aims at task assignment, while taking the fairness and distance constraints into consideration. As shown in Fig. 2(upper part), the whole system time is divided into $t$ time slots, $t\in\{0,1,2,\ldots\}$ . In the system, the platform continuously publishes a set of sensing tasks throughout the system time, which are denoted by $\boldsymbol{S}=\{s_{1},s_{2},...,s_{k}\}$ , and each task $s_{i}$ corresponds to a location $s_{i}.l.$ A crowd of users with smart devices move around and participate in the MCS system to perform the sensing tasks, which are denoted by $\mathcal{U}=\{u_{1},u_{2},...,u_{n}\}$ , and each user $u_{j}$ has a location $u_{j}.l$ . Moreover, each user $u_{j}$ is associated with his reachable travel distance indicating the locations $u_{j}$ could cover in the time slot $t$ , denoted by $\mathcal{D}_{j}$ . We map all the locations of tasks and users into a 2D spatial plane. To further reduce the complexity, we assume that each task is indivisible and can only be assigned to one user in each time slot $t$ , and each user could be assigned many tasks in a time slot $t$ , but the travel distance for user $u_{j}$ to perform these assigned tasks cannot exceed his reachable travel distance $\mathcal{D}_{j}$ . Actually, this setting is reasonable for most practical purposes, since users could not travel a long distance for the MCS campaigns.
Next, we let $\boldsymbol{x}_{i j}^{t}$ indicate whether to assign task $s_{i}$ to user $u_{j}$ in time slot $t$ . If task $s_{i}$ is assigned to user $u_{j}$ successfully, then $x_{i j}^{t}=1$ , otherwise $x_{i j}^{t}=0$ . As for cost, we let $c_{j}^{t}$ denote the cost that user $u_{j}$ processes the assigned tasks, which is quantified by the distance between user $u_{j}^{:}$ ’s location and task $s_{i}$ ’s location. Notably, the cost can be measured in various ways, such as the length of time a user takes to process a task, user’s expertise for a task, etc. The use of different cost measurement methods only manifests in the different expressions of the cost function in the problem, and does not have a large impact on other parts of the problem. We only need to design the corresponding cost function to replace the original cost function according to different cost measurement methods. But our proposed fairness task assignment algorithm is still valid. To reduce the complexity, we justly use the distance between users and tasks as a measurement for simplicity to prove the universality of our theory.

3.3  Fairness model
For the task assignment, besides the distance constraint and cost, we would like to select a suitable user for each task considering the fairness. To reflect the fairness constraints of users and tasks, inspired by Lyapunov optimization technique, we can utilize queues to construct the fairness constraints for tasks and users respectively.
1) The user fairness: due to the objective of minimizing the assignment cost, a user in the user set $\mathcal{U}$ may suffer from the scenario where too many tasks are assigned to him in a certain time slot $t$ . If this scenario continues, the users could be overloaded and of course it is unfair to users. The user fairness means taking full advantage of the users’ ability under constraints, that is, not exceeding the maximum processing ability of each user while also not letting any user idle too long.
2) The task fairness: due to the objective of minimizing the assignment cost, a task in the task set $s$ may suffer from the scenario where it is not assigned in time slot $t$ because of its high cost. If this scenario continues, the task’s assignment frequency will not be satisfied and it thus causes unfairness. The task fairness means that each task has good assignment frequency, that is, how many times each task should be assigned within the whole system time.

This means that each user will not be overloaded with tasks for long periods of time. Thus, to maintain the fairness of users, we need to ensure that the corresponding user processing queue is stable under the proposed strategy.

4  Fairness task assignment under distance constraint
For mobile users with travel distance constraint, the problem of fairness task assignment becomes more intractable. In this section, we focus on the fairness task assignment problem with the objective of minimizing the total assignment cost under the fairness and distance constraints. Specifically, we introduce a Fairness Task Assignment Strategy under Distance constraint (FTAS-D) in detail. First, we set up task virtual queue and user processing queue for each task and user separately to represent the fairness of task assignment. Then we introduce and improve the Lyapunov optimization technique, and construct the double-queue Lyapunov optimization function and drift-plus-penalty function. Since the optimization problem is difficult to solve directly, we relax the constraint, that is, the objective of minimizing the upper bound of the drift plus penalty function is relaxed. After that, we solve the problem under the user distance constraint to obtain a feasible solution. Next we filter the solutions to minimize the total assignment cost and maintain queue stability. More specifically, we utilize the simulated annealing algorithm, taking the above feasible solution as the initial solution of the simulated annealing algorithm, and continue to iteratively find the best solution.

4.1  Problem hardness
Before prescribing the assignment strategy, we first prove that the distance-constraint fairness task assignment problem is NP-hard, as shown in the following theorem.
Theorem 1  The task assignment problem under the fairness and travel distance constraints is NP-hard.
Proof  Without loss of generality, we ignore distance constraint but consider the fairness of users and tasks. We also assume that all users would be willing to return to the starting location after performing the assigned tasks. Here we consider a special case, which is to let $|\mathcal{U}|=1$ (that is, only one user is involved in the assignment|). |Then we perform task assignment based on fairness and determine the sequence of task locations $L_{j}$ for the user, so as to minimize the total assignment cost, which is indeed a classic NP problem, Travelling Salesman Problem(TSP) (Freisleben and Merz 1996): a merchandiser goes to several cities to sell goods, the salesman departs from one city, passes through all the cities, returns to the place of departure, and the goal is to choose the route of travel to make the overall journey as short as possible. That is to say, the special case is NPhard. Consequently, when we further consider the number of users and distance constraint, the fairness task assignment problem is NP-hard. The theorem holds. ◻

4.3  Successor search algorithm
Since we scale the upper bound of Lyapunov drift-plus-penalty function in the above FTAS algorithm, the initial solution is merely a feasible solution. Therefore, we introduce a Fairness Task Assignment Strategy with Distance constraint (FTAS-D) based on simulated annealing algorithm to modify the initial solution and obtain the successor of the current solution to search for a better approximate solution.
As shown in Algorithm 2, the algorithm aims to optimize the initial solution by searching for successor solution until the stopping condition is met. It first initializes the best solution $\mathbf{x}_{i j^{\ast}}^{t}$ to be the initial solution $\mathbf{x}_{i j}^{t}.$ . The main loop of lines 2–17 consists of the main part of the algorithm. In each iteration of the loop, a new successor $\mathbf{x}_{i j^{\prime}}^{t}$ of the current solution is generated by removing some task from some user or swapping a task between two users (line 3). It is worth noting that algorithm applies different methods to modify the current solution randomly in this paper. The random method can help diversify the solution. Then, the algorithm determines whether the solution $\mathbf{x}_{i j^{\prime}}^{t}$ is accepted or not (lines 4–12). Specifically, if the cost of the new solution $\mathbf{x}_{i j^{\prime}}^{t}$ is lower than that of the current solution $\mathbf{x}_{i j}^{t},\mathbf{x}_{i j^{\prime}}^{t}$ is accepted. Otherwise, the probability of accepting $\mathbf{x}_{i j^{\prime}}^{t}$ i s $\exp((u(\mathbf{x}_{i j^{\prime}}^{t})-u(\mathbf{x}_{i j}^{t}))/T)$ , where $u(\mathbf{x}_{i j}^{t})$ is the cost of the solution $\mathbf{X}_{i j}^{t}$ and $T$ is the temperature. Subsequently, if the cost of the current solution is lower than that of the current best solution $\mathbf{x}_{i j^{\ast}}^{t}$ , the current best solution is set to be $\mathbf{x}_{i j}^{t}$ and $T_{r}$ is set as $T$ (lines 9–12). $T_{r}$ is used to record the temperature when the best solution is updated. In each iteration, the temperature $T$ is updated as $\gamma\times T$ where $0<\gamma<1$ (line 13). To avoid the search getting stuck in a local minimum, the temperature will increase when the temperature is lower than 0.01, which can increase the probability of escaping from the trap (Liu et al. 2019). The temperature is limited to $T_{m a x}$ to limit the probability of accepting a solution that is not good. The stopping condition is satisfied when the iteration number is equal to the predefined maximum iteration number. Finally, the searching process stops and FTAS-D algorithm returns the best solution.
This task assignment approach makes the control decision in each time slot to guide the platform to assign tasks to the most suitable users to minimize the time average assignment cost while meeting the condition of the system stability and users’ distance constraint. After the assignment of all the tasks in time slot $t$ , we update the task virtual queues and the user processing queues.

7  Conclusion
In this paper, we investigate the problem of task assignment in an MCS scenario that considers distance constraint and fairness of tasks and users, where each task should be assigned by an assignment frequency and each user’s processing ability and travel distance is limited. In order to minimize the assignment cost under the distance constraint and satisfy the fairness constraints, we first model each user’s processing ability as user processing queue and convert the constraint of task assignment frequency to the task virtual queue. The Distance-constrained Fairness Task Assignment problem (DFTA) is then formulated, aiming to minimize the assignment cost while maintaining the stability of the double-queues. To address this problem, we propose a Fairness Task Assignment Strategy with Distance constraint(FTASD), which utilizes Lyapunov optimization to generate a feasible assignment solution to the problem, and then utilizes simulated annealing to find the best solution, which can be performed in each time slot to make control actions to minimize assignment cost while maintaining system stability. To analyze the performance of the proposed assignment strategy, we find an upper bound on the total assignment cost and queue backlog. Finally, extensive evaluations on Changchun/ Taxi, Epfl/mobility and Feeder validate the effectiveness of the proposed strategy. Our simulation results show that the proposed strategy outperforms the other baselines.
