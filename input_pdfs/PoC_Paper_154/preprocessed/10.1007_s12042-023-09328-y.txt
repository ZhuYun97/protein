Transcriptome-wide Identification of CDR Family in Citrus Latifolia and its Expression During HLB Disease

Abstract
Huanglongbing (HLB) is the most devastating disease in citrus production around the world. Currently, there are no HLBresistant citrus species or genotypes, however, Persian lime (Citrus latifolia Tan.) has been reported as the most tolerant citrus species to this disease, but their molecular mechanisms of tolerance have not been characterized yet. It has been suggested that the high expression of Constitutive Disease Resistance $(C D R)$ genes is associated with HLB tolerance in Poncirus trifoliata, a close relative of citrus species, therefore, the aim of this work was to identify $C D R$ gene family members in the Persian lime transcriptome and to determine if HLB induces their expression. Transcripts with one or two catalytic domains were searched, then, phylogeny for CDR genes was carried out with the selected transcripts from C. latifolia, P. trifoliata, and $C.$ . sinensis. Similarity among proteins was estimated using alignments, identity matrix, UPGMA, and conserved motifs analyses. Once C. latifolia $C D R$ genes were identified, their expression was determined in the transcriptome data and validated by RT-PCR in asymptomatic and HBL-symptomatic leaves. A total of 146 sequences with one or two catalytic domains were found, and seven CDR clades were recovered. However, the transcriptome and RT-PCR data did not show significant differences in the expression of these ClCDR genes between HLB-symptomatic and asymptomatic plants. Therefore, although our results demonstrated the presence of $C D R$ genes in the Persian lime genome, they also suggest that HLB tolerance is not correlated with the increased expression of this gene family in this species.

Introduction
Mexican citrus (mainly lemons and limes) are exported to various countries worldwide (USDA, 2020). The Mexican state of Veracruz is the most important producer and exporter of Persian lime (Citrus latifolia Tan.) due to the high quality of the harvested fruits (Berdeja-Arbeu et al. 2019). How­ ever, Huanglongbing (HLB) disease has been reported in Persian limes orchards at Veracruz (Marquez-Pérez et al. 2018). HLB, also called citrus greening disease, represents the worse threat in the history of the citrus industry, and it is caused by three different species of Candidatus Liberi­ bacter (Thapa et al. 2020). Candidatus Liberibacter asiati­ cus (CLas) is the most widely dispersed species around the world (Ajene et al. 2020), including Mexico (López-Buenfil et al. 2017).
Due to the difficulty of isolating CLas (Merfa et al. 2019), genetic improvement is the most promising longterm management tool (Alquézar et al. 2021). Although there are no citrus genotypes or varieties resistant to CLas infection (Alquézar et al. 2021; Alves et al. 2021), some tol­ erant species such as $C.$ . aurantifolia (Arce-Leal et al. 2020) and C. australasica (Weber et al. 2022) have been reported. Therefore, to develop genetic improvement it is crucial to understand the molecular mechanisms of species with high levels of tolerance against HLB.
HLB tolerance has been well documented also in close relatives of citrus species such as Poncirus trifoliata (Ramadugu et al. 2016) and its hybrids with other citrus species (Albretch and Bowman 2012). P. trifoliata is a very important species in the citrus industry because it is used as a rootstock to other different citrus species scions, induc­ ing very desirable features to the fruits (Hu et al. 2022). Transcriptomic and genomic studies have suggested that the tolerance to HLB in $P.$ trifoliata is associated with the high expression of Constitutive Disease Resistance (CDR) family genes. These genes are highly expressed in tolerant genotypes, but are absent in susceptible species (Rawat et al. 2017). CDR genes belonging to the aspartic protease gene family, have been characterized in Arabidopsis thali­ ana (Xia et al. 2004) where they are associated with defense responses in plants against both biotic and abiotic stresses (Figuereido et al. 2021).
HLB-infected Persian lime plants show physiological alterations such as starch accumulation and decreased chlo­ rophyll contents (Flores-de la Rosa et al. 2021), and eco­ nomic losses have also been reported (Flores-Sánchez et al. 2015). However, Persian lime is still considered to be one of the least susceptible citrus species to HLB disease when compared to sweet citrus such as oranges (C. sinensis), tan­ gerines (C. clementina), and even to other limes such as Mexican limes ( $C.$ . aurantifolia). Persian lime trees are able to continue their production for many years after CLas has been detected (Evans et al. 2014). Recently, HLB-affected Persian lime trees showed better physiological performance in terms of a higher photosynthetic rates, higher stomatal conductance and transpiration than HLB-affected Mexican lime (Sivager et al. 2021), suggesting that phloem dynamics can be maintained in Persian lime during CLas infection. However, this apparent high HLB-tolerance shown by Per­ sian lime has not been studied at the molecular level.
Therefore, the aim of the present study was to evaluate the hypothesis that the HLB-tolerance of Persian lime, might be related to the ability of its $C D R$ genes family to show high expression in response to HLB in a similar manner as it occurs in $P.$ trifoliata, by identifying $C D R$ genes family in the Persian lime transcriptome and determining the effect of CLas infection on the expression of these genes.

Materials and Methods

Identification ofCDR-likeSequences in the Persian Lime Transcriptome
We obtained all CDR-like transcripts from a transcriptomic analysis made on Persian lime by our group (EstrellaMaldonado et al., in preparation). A search for sequences containing one or two Aspartic Protease Domains (DTGS or DSGT) was conducted using HMMER v3.3.2 soft­ ware (Wheeler and Eddy 2013) against the Pfam database (Accession: PF00026) (Mistry et al. 2021). All sequences containing the Aspartic Protease Domains were recovered and used in the present analysis.

Phylogeny ofCDR-likeSequences
A phylogenetic tree was constructed using CDR genes iden­ tified in our Persian lime transcriptome as well as sequences from different $C D R$ genes obtained from $P.$ trifoliata and C. sinensis genomes available from public Citrus Genome Database (https://www.citrusgenomedb.org). The sequence of $C D R$ genes from the three species were aligned by mul­ tiple alignment based on Muscle algorithm (gap open $=15$ ; gap extend $^{=3}$ ) using BioEdit Software (Hall 1999). A nonweighted Maximum Parsimony analysis was conducted with T.N.T. Software (Goloboff et al. 2008), to reconstruct the phylogenetic relationships among sequences, using a New Search Technology approach (ratchet $+$ sectorial search), and a bootstrap (1000 repeats) method was applied to resample. A strict consensus tree was constructed with all the most parsimonious trees.

Similarity Analysis Among ClCDR, CsCDR, and PtCDR Proteins
To compare the similarity between CDR proteins from the three species, all genes recovered from the phyloge­ netic tree were translated to protein using the online tool ExPASy (https://web.expasy.org/translate/) and the largest ORF was selected for further analysis. Then an alignment was constructed with a ClustalW algorithm (gap open $=15$ ; gap extend $\O=3$ ) using BioEdit Software (Hall 1999). A genetic identity matrix was calculated among all CDR protein sequences based on $p$ -distance. After an UPGMA dendrogram was constructed with the calculated distances (bootstrap $=1000$ repeats), both analyzes were performed in MEGA V.11 Software (Tamura et al. 2021). The conserved motif structures of ClCDR, CsCDR, and PtCDR proteins were analyzed using Multiple Expectation Maximization for Motif Elicitation (MEME) Software (http://meme-suite. org/tools/meme).

Expression Patterns ofClCDRGenes in Persian Lime During HLB Infection
To determine the expression of ClCDR genes, the differ­ entially expressed genes (DEG´s) database generated by Estrella-Maldonado et al. (in preparation) was consulted to obtain data of reads counts, $\mathrm{Log}_{2}$ Fold change, and $p$ -values of transcripts identified as CDR sequences.

ClCDRExpression Validation by RT-PCR and RTqPCR
Physiologically matured leaves (V5 stage according to scale proposed by Ribeiro et al. 2021) from 7-year-old Persian lime trees naturally infected by HLB, with and without symptoms were collected for RNA extraction. CLas infec­ tion was diagnosed using a nested PCR protocol developed by Lee et al. (2010). Total RNA was extracted from the same tissue using Trizol $\textsuperscript{\textregistered}$ Reagent protocol (Thermo Fischer Scientific) and treated with RQ1 RNAse-free DNAse (Pro­ mega) to avoid genomic DNA contamination. The con­ centration and purity of RNA samples were examined by NanoDrop™ 1000 Spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific NanoDrop Technologies, LLC, Wilmington, DE, USA) and the quality was evaluated by $1.5\%$ agarose gel electropho­ resis during $30~\mathrm{min}$ at $80\mathrm{V}.$ The single-strand cDNA was synthesized using M-MLV reverse transcriptase enzyme (Promega), according to the manufacturer’s instructions. A set of primers were designed using Primer Express Ver. 3.1 (Applied Biosystems USA) to evaluate the relative expres­ sion levels of ClCDR genes.
First, gene expression was evaluated by RT-PCR using the amplification product of SAND gen as a control (Mafra et al. 2012). The reaction condition was 10X PCR buffer, $\mathrm{MgCl}_{2}25\mathrm{mM}$ , dNTP´s $10\mathrm{mM}$ , $10\upmu\mathrm{M}$ of each forward and reverse primer, $1\mathrm{~U~}$ of Taq DNA polymerase (Invitrogen), and $600~\mathrm{{ng/\upmul}}$ of cDNA, at a final volume of $25~\upmu\upmu1$ . Prod­ ucts were analyzed in agarose gels $(1.5\%,60\ \mathrm{V},90\ \mathrm{min})$ , dyed with ethidium bromide $(2\%)$ , and visualized under UV light. The relative expression levels of ClCDR8 gene were validated by RT-qPCR, and UPL7 gene was used as a reference to normalize all data (Mapfra et al. 2012). The reaction specificity was confirmed using the standard melt curve method. The mixture of each tube was comprised of the following constituents: $300~\mathrm{ng}$ cDNA, 2X Sybr green master mix (Biorad), ${5\upmu\mathrm{M}}$ of each primer, and $3\upmu\mathrm{l}$ of ultra­ pure water. The reaction was performed in a thermocycler StepOne™ Real-Time PCR System and StepOne Software $\mathrm{v}2.3$ (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA). Relative Expression Levels (REL) of ClCDR8 gene in symptomatic and asymptomatic leaves, were calculated using the $2^{-\Delta\Delta\mathrm{CT}}$ method (Schmittgen and Livak 2008). RT-qPCR conditions were as follows: $95^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $3\mathrm{min}$ ; 40 cycles of $95^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $30\mathrm{s}$ 。 $59^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $30~\mathrm{s}$ and $72^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $30~\mathrm{s}$ The melting curves were analyzed at 60 to $95^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ after 40 cycles. Relative Expression Levels (REL) data were subjected to a bilateral T-Student analysis in R Software.

Results

Identification ofCDR-like genes inC. latifoliatranscriptome and phylogenetic homology
A total of 146 sequences from the Persian lime tran­ scriptome, showed the aspartyl protease domain. These sequences were used for Maximum Parsimony phyloge­ netic reconstruction along with the $C D R$ sequences from $P.$ trifoliata and $C.$ . sinensis. The search allowed the recovery of the five most parsimonious trees $(\mathrm{L}=789)$ , and a strict consensus tree was constructed resulting in seven clades where at least one $P t C D R$ or $C s C D R$ gene sequence and at least a transcript from $C.$ . latifolia were grouped. The clade containing the PtCDR1 sequence, also contains the transcript TRINITY_DN735_c2_g1_i1, and the clade was well bootstrap-supported. Other clade grouped the PtCDR8 gene with different isoforms of the transcript TRINITY_ $\mathrm{DN}3834\_\mathrm{c0\_g1}$ , and this clade showed similarity with the clade containing PtCDR3 which also has an isoform of the transcript TRINITY_DN3834_c0_g3_i1. On the other hand, the transcript TRINITY_DN9086_c0_g1_i1 showed high similarity with PtCDR6 gene, however, the transcripts TRINITY_DN12502_c0_g2, TRINITY_DN18041_c0_ g1_i2 and three isoforms of the TRINITY_DN735_c0_g1 transcript also showed similarity with PtCDR6 gene of $P.$ trifoliata.
Finally, two clades were recovered with sequences from C. sinensis, the clade with CsCDR3 which was homologous to TRINITY_20694_c0_g1_i1 transcript, and the CsCDR13, homologous to four isoforms of TRINITY_DN5173_c0_g1 transcript (Fig. 1). All ClCDR genes identified are summa­ rized in Table 1.

Molecular Similarity of ClCDR, PtCDR, and CsCDR Proteins
The amino acids sequences multiple alignment showed that there are some highly conserved regions, particularly related to functional aspartyl protease domains (Fig. 2). However, the transcript TRINITY_DN18041_c0_g1_i1 did not show any of the two catalytic domains, therefore it was dis­ carded in further analysis. All the other sequences showed a DTGS domain, but the TRINITY_DN12502_c0_g2_i1 protein had a modified DSGS domain, and also the TRIN­ ITY_DN20694_c0_g1_i1 protein had a modified DTSN domain. Interestingly, the CsCDR13 protein had a modi­ fied DTAS domain. Regarding the DSGT domain, only the TRINITY_DN20694_c0_g1_i1 protein did not have this domain, however, TRINITY_DN12502_c0_g2_i1 and TRINITY_DN735_c0_g1_i3 proteins sequences, had the modified versions of the domain as DTGT and DCGT, respectively. The protein $C s\mathrm{CDR}3$ also showed a modified DSGS domain.

Similarity matrix showed that some transcripts from Persian lime encode proteins that are very similar to their homologs of $P.$ trifoliata or $C.$ sinensis with similarity val­ ues over $90\%$ (Table 2). For instance, the transcript TRIN­ ITY_DN3834_c0_g3_i1 had a $93.6\%$ similarity value with PtCDR3 protein. Interestingly, the largest ORF encoded by the transcript TRINITY_DN9086_c0_g1_i1 was identical to the protein PtCDR1 $(100\%)$ , and also showed high similarity with PtCDR6 $(99.6\%)$ despite that there is no phylogenetic homology among PtCDR6 and $P t C D R I$ genes, suggesting that this protein might have multiple origins in $P.$ trifoliata genome, but it is unique in C. latifolia. TRINITY_DN20694_c0_g1_i1, and TRINITY_DN5173 c0_g1_i2 showed low similarity of CDR genes between $C$ . latifolia and C. sinensis (Table  2). UPGMA analysis cor­ roborated the obtained similarity values (Fig. 3).
In tyerms of conserved motifs analysis (Fig. 4), the phy­ logenetic homologous proteins, the motifs, and their organi­ zation was very similar. For instance, the proteins encoded by transcript TRINITY_DN9086_c0_g1_i1 and PtCDR1 and PtCDR6 genes, had the same motifs at the same posi­ tion and with the same length. PtCDR3 and PtCDR8 pro­ teins had very similar patterns of conserved motifs with both isoforms of transcript TRINITY_DN3834_c0, according to the phylogeny and similarity matrix (Table 2). Also, the pro­ teins encoded by the transcripts TRINITY_DN12502_c0_ g2_i1 and TRINITY_DN735_c0_g1_i3 had similar patterns to other CDR proteins, but the number and organization of them were different, suggesting that those ClCDR genes might be specific to C. latifolia. CsCDR3 and TRINITY_ $\mathrm{DN}5173\_\mathrm{c0\_g1\_i}$ proteins share four conserved motifs, including the catalytic domains, despite the fact that those genes have not phylogenetic homology. For CsCDR13 and TRINITY_DN20694_c0_g1_i1, the analysis showed that in the later, the motif containing the DTGS domain is not com­ plete at the C-terminal.

ClCDRGenes Expression in the Persian Lime Transcriptome During HLB Infection
Data obtained from the Persian lime HLB-symptomatic transcriptome, showed that the majority of the transcripts from ClCDR genes had low counts, $\mathrm{Log}_{2}$ fold change val­ ues $<2$ , and a $p$ -value $>0.05$ , indicating that there are no dif­ ferences in ClCDR genes expression in Persian lime during HLB disease (Table 3).

RT-PCR Amplification ofClCDRGenes and RTqPCR Validation ofClCDR8Gene
We obtained RNA from three replicates of Persian lime leaves with HLB-symptoms and three replicates without symptoms (corroborating that the asymptomatic samples were not infected with CLas; data not shown). Then, the cDNA was synthesized and the amplification of each ClCDR was performed and visualized in agarose gel. No amplifica­ tion was observed in almost all ClCDR genes, except in the case of ClCDR8a (TRINITY_DN3834_c0_g1 transcript). The amplification pattern observed in the electrophoresis, corresponds with the number of counts from the transcrip­ tome data, suggesting that the number of mRNA of ClCDR genes in the samples, (symptomatic or asymptomatic), was very low, therefore, it was not possible to use it for qualita­ tive observation by RT-PCR method, unlike the ClCDR8a and the control SAND gene (Fig. 5a). We compared the REL values of ClCDR8a gene found in HLB-symptomatic and asymptomatic Persian lime samples using RT-qPCR. The results of the statistical analysis showed that there are no significant differences in the expression of this gene when the CLas infection is established, result that is in line with that found with the transcriptome DEG analysis (Fig. 5b).

Discussion
HLB disease is a major concern for many farmers, tech­ nicians and researchers around the world. Due to the dif­ ficulty of CLas isolation (Merfa et al. 2019), strategies to directly attack bacteria have developed rather slowly, mak­ ing the generation of genetic resistance to the pathogen the most promising tool for the long-term management of HLB (Martinelli and Dandekar 2017; Li et al. 2021). Hence, the study of different genotypes with different levels of HLB tolerance is very important to understand the molecular mechanisms of this tolerance and then use it in breeding programs aiming to generate HLB-tolerant varieties.
$P.$ trifoliata is one of the most HLB-tolerant citrus-rel­ ative species (Albrecht and Bowman, 2012). It has been observed that $P.$ trifoliata shows higher expression levels of $C D R$ genes in response to HLB disease, than other very sus­ ceptible genotypes (Rawat et al. 2017), suggesting a role for these genes in the molecular mechanisms of HLB-tolerance.
Our results showed that in Persian lime there were at least six CDR genes present in the transcriptome, two that were phylogenetically homologous to PtCDR genes and two that were homologous to CsCDR genes (Fig.  1). The protein encoded by transcript TRINITY_DN9086_c0_g1_ i1 (ClCDR6) is very similar to two proteins, (PtCDR1 and PtCDR6) from P. trifoliata, but ClCDR6 gene is homologous to $P t C D R6$ rather than to PtCDR1. This result is consistent with reports from Rawat et al. (2017), who associated both PtCDR genes with just one gene from $C$ . sinensis; the posi­ tion of both genes in the phylogeny suggests that PtCDR1/6 proteins in $P.$ trifoliata have multiple origins with a conver­ gent evolution (Storz 2016), unlike those from C. sinensis and C. latifolia.

In contrast, ClCDR8 gene translates at least two different isoforms that are phylogenetically homologous to PtCDR8 and PtCDR3 genes (Fig. 1) and encodes proteins very simi­ lar to PtCDR8 and PtCDR3 proteins (Fig. 4). Recently, the heterologous expression of PtCDR2 and PtCDR8 genes, in the Arabidopsis cdr1 mutant line, was demonstrated to restore the hypersensitive response (HR) to Pseudomonas syringae (Ying et al. 2020), supporting the hypothesis that the expression of these genes is associated to pathogen resistance.
In the C. latifolia transcriptome, a homologous to PtCDR2 was not observed, but a homologous to PtCDR8 was found with a high basal expression (Fig. 5a). Contrary to what it was expected, the CLas infection did not induce a detectable change in the ClCDR8 expression, neither in the transcrip­ tome, nor in the RT-qPCR results, suggesting that this gene is not related to the tolerance observed in C. latifolia. More­ over, this work is the first evolutionary approximation of the complexity of the CDR family. Evidence suggests that there are many copies of genes encoding similar proteins in $P.$ trifoliata (PtCDR2, PtCDR3, and PtCDR8), with tentatively redundant functions, while in C. latifolia there is a unique gene encoding different proteins by alternative splicing, which has been reported in other citrus crops (Del Carratore et al. 2011; Wu et al. 2021). In addition, the low counts in the transcriptome and the lack of amplification by RT-PCR of all the other ClCDR genes, including the ClCDR8 iso­ form (homologous to the PtCDR3 gene), are consistent with the behavior of this family in other citrus plants, such as C. sinensis (Rawat et al. 2017). Our results give evidence that HLB does not induce changes in the expression of CDR genes in C. latifolia; that is, the tolerance reported in Persian lime is not associated with the CDR family.
Fig. 5  Amplification of ClCDR genes from leaves of Persian lime trees with HLB-symptoms $\mathrm{(HLB^{+})}$ and asymptomatic $\mathrm{(HLB^{-})}$ . (a) Expression of ClCDR genes by RT-PCR. (b) Relative expression levels of ClCDR8a gene by RT-qPCR
The results shown in the present work demonstrate that there are at least six members of the CDR family in C. lati­ folia, some of which are homologous to PtCDR and CsCDR genes. However, changes in the expression of these genes were not observed in plants with HLB, suggesting that the tolerance reported previously in C. latifolia against HLB, is not associated with this gene family. Obviously, additional experiments with knockout or overexpressed C. latifolia lines for these genes are necessary to prove this hypoth­ esis. Further studies are indeed needed to elucidate which other gene families may be expressed in response to the dis­ ease, which may explain the relatively high HLB-tolerance shown by Citrus latifolia.
