CONFIDENCE DISC AND SQUARE FOR THE CAUCHY DISTRIBUTIONS
Y. Akaoka,1 K. Okamura,2,3 and Y. Otobe4
We construct a confidence region of parameters for a sample of size $N$ from the Cauchy distributed random variables. Although the Cauchy distribution has two parameters, namely, a location parameter $\mu\in\mathbb{R}$ and a scale parameter $\sigma>0$ , we consider them simultaneously by regarding them as a single complex parameter $\gamma:=\mu+i\sigma$ . The region should be a domain in the complex plane. We give a simple and specific formula to represent the region as a disc and as a square.

1. Introduction
The Cauchy distribution is one of typical bell-shaped and stable laws that has a longer and flatter tail, which makes its mathematical handling difficult. However, for this reason, typical samples from the Cauchy distribution concentrate in its center, except for a few “outliers.” Thus, one may encounter the cases in which it is preferable to model the Cauchy distribution rather than the Gaussian distribution. From this point of view, in the preceding articles [2, 3], we have investigated estimators for the parameters of Cauchy distributions from observations. These are point estimations. However, in the present paper, we are concerned with an interval estimation, i.e., our aim is to construct confidence regions. Confidence regions are not only often used in practice but also relate to statistical hypothesis testing (see, e.g., [15, $\S7.1.2]\$ ); hence, it goes without saying its statistical importance.
On the other hand, to the best of our knowledge, no specific regions for the Cauchy distributions are known, while few studies have examined them. One of them is a paper by Haas, Bain and Antle [6], which is based on [7]. They discussed mainly the maximal likelihood estimators for the Cauchy distributions and proposed confidence intervals for the location parameter $\mu$ . However, unfortunately, the explicit formula for the maximal likelihood estimator is known only for the case of sample sizes of 3 and 4 and, furthermore, there is no algebraic closed-form formula for the case of a sample size of 5 (see [5, 13]). If the explicit formula is unknown, then the Newton– Raphson method has been often used. Hinkley [8, (2.12)] gave confidence regions for the pair of location and scale by using the maximal likelihood estimator. It is related to the likelihood-ratio test. However, it is complicated and, thus, it is hard to imagine the specific shape of the region. Vrbik also obtained confidence regions [17] based on the maximal likelihood estimator; see also [4, 9, 10] for the related inference methods for Cauchy distributions.
We also would like to point out that, although it is definitely true that computer-aided numerical simulations have become an efficient method for statistically testing or even constructing confidence intervals, it is not trivial to apply them for Cauchy distributions. To show this, we see, e.g., that a simple arithmetic mean of the Cauchy random variables still obeys the Cauchy distribution, which is independent of the number of sizes of the samples: here, we emphasize that the choice of a simple and nice estimator for the Cauchy distributions is a nontrivial problem. We also mention that few unbiased estimators were known so far except, e.g., the case of using MLEs [11] or trimmed average [14], which actually eliminates the outliers from the observations.
In this situation, we would like to propose in the present paper to take the geometric mean (see Remark 2.2 for the terminology). There are several reasons why it is nicer than the other quantities. However, since this problem belongs to the theory of point estimation, we would not like here to go in this direction any further. Here, we only point out that the geometric mean has sufficiently high integrability and is unbiased (see [3] for some additional properties of the geometric mean). Moreover, since the geometric mean is an exponential of the arithmetic mean of logarithms, it is harmless to compute the quantity. However, we note that the geometric mean $\prod_{j=1}^{N}X_{j}^{1/N}$ s i a product of the $(1/N)$ th power of (probably negative) observations. Hence, we are naturally led to treating all quantities as complex numbers. In particular, $X^{p}$ , $0<|p|<1$ , and $\log X$ are all complex random variables. Therefore, we gather some basic quantities, mainly expectations of these random variables in Section 2.
It is well known that if $X$ obeys a Cauchy distribution, then $\mathbb{E}[|X|^{p}]$ exists and, hence, $\mathbb{E}[X^{p}]$ also exists for $|p|<1$ . The explicit formula of $\mathbb{E}[|X|^{p}]$ is given by [18, (3.0.3)]. However, the derivation of [18, (3.0.3)] is very complicated. Therefore, we propose another way of evaluation of them, along with $\mathbb{E}[X^{p}]$ , in Section 2. These calculations are rather simple and direct. We also expect that, through these manipulations, it would become clear that the analysis performed in the complex plane is not a technical restriction but an essential tool for the Cauchy distributions. In particular, we hope it would be natural to regard the location parameter $\mu$ and the scale parameter $\sigma>0$ of the Cauchy distribution as a single complex parameter $\gamma=\mu+i\sigma$ but not as two distinct real parameters. Note that this idea was proposed by McCullagh [12]. Thus, our statistical inference for the parameters of the Cauchy distributions is actually a problem for a single complex parameter, i.e., we naturally consider the location parameter and the scale parameter together.
Then we show that the central limit theorem holds for the geometric mean (Lemma 3.3) and modify it to be able to determine the confidence region (Lemma 3.4) in the complex plane. This is a standard procedure. Finally, based on the central limit theorem, we obtain the confidence region for the Cauchy parameters; as a disc in the complex plane (Theorem 3.1). In Section 4, we conclude the paper by showing several numerical examples for our confidence disc.

4.4. Large $\pmb{\mu}$ and Small $\pmb{\sigma}$ . If $|\mu|$ is much larger than $\sigma$ , then all data may have the same sign. In this case, it is possible that the imaginary part of the geometric mean is vanishing and, hence, we can guess that $\sigma$ is zero. This case is illustrated in Fig. 5. One practical way to avoid this situation is to subtract a constant from each datum. It is clear that if we set $Y_{i}:=X_{i}-c$ , then the estimate using $Y_{i}$ ’s plus $c$ is equal to the estimate obtained for $X_{i}$ ’s. We also emphasize that subtracting a constant may decrease the absolute value of the geometric mean so that our estimated discs may become smaller, i.e., the estimation can be improved. However, it is impossible to get a suitable constant in order to make the data consisting of both positive and negative values. Therefore, practically speaking, it may be a candidate to subtract the median $\mathrm{med}(X_{1},\ldots,X_{N})$ from all $X_{i}$ ’s. As a result, the data would consist of the same numbers of positive and negative values.

4.5. Outlier and Gaussian Estimation. Here, we deal with one-dimensional location-scale families and consider the problem of estimation of the location when the scale is unknown. It is common to assume that the data are Gaussian distributed if the shape of frequency has the form of a bell. However, when the data have some outliers, the use of $t$ -distributions may give an inappropriate confidence interval. It should be noted that the Cauchy distribution often gives “outliers” of this kind due to its heavy density tail; hence, it is possible to expect that the inference applicable to the Cauchy distribution is more robust.
Here, we present a numerical example that consists of ten samples; each of these samples contains 100 values from a standard normal distribution. Then we replace each 100th datum with “5” to mimic an outlier. In Tables 1 and 2, we illustrate the corresponding differences. The left-hand parts of these tables display the original data, while their right-hand parts reflect the modified data containing the outliers.
In Table 1, we show how this contamination affects the confidence intervals when we use the $t$ -distribution whose degree of freedom is equal to 99, while Table 2 presents the confidence intervals obtained by using Corollary 3.2. Here, the AM and GM denote the arithmetic mean and the real part of the geometric mean, respectively. It can be seen that the confidence intervals computed by using Corollary 3.2 are not so strongly affected by the outliers.
