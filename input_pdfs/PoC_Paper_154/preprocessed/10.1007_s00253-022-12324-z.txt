The application of adenine deaminase in antibody affinity maturation
Baole \(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{u}^{1,2}\) · Yun Zhao1,2 · Lili An1 \(\bullet\) Haiying Hang1,2
Received: 17 August 2022 / Revised: 23 November 2022 / Accepted: 30 November 2022 / Published online: 16 March 2023 \(\circledcirc\) The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer-Verlag GmbH Germany, part of Springer Nature 2023

Abstract
Previously, we established a platform for antibody/protein affinity maturation based on CHO cell display. The gene of interest was mutated by activation-induced cytidine deaminase (AID), and then, a mutation library mainly containing G/C to A/T conversion was obtained by simply proliferating cells. However, the AID-induced G/C to A/T conversion limits the diversity space of the mutation library. In contrast to AID, adenine deaminase (ADA) can convert A/T to G/C. In this study, we demonstrated that ADA could efficiently induce random A/T to G/C mutations on the target gene in the CHO cell display and could be applied in affinity maturation. Our data also showed that more mutant types were obtained through the combined use of AID and ADA, thus offering an opportunity to acquire new mutants offering higher affinities than those obtained by only using AID. Examples presented in this study showed that ADA contributed to the improvement of antibody affinity either with or without AID in CHO display.

Key points
- ADA is able to induce random mutations on antibody gene in mammalian cells.- ADA induces mutations on A/T bases to compensate AID which can induce mutation on G/C.- Combination of AID and ADA can increase mutation types and maturation efficiencies.
Keywords  Adenine deaminase \(\cdot\) Activation-induced cytidine deaminase \(\cdot\) Antibody affinity maturation \(\cdot\) CHO cell display \(\cdot\) Mutation type

Introduction
Directed evolution is a useful method to improve properties of protein. Display technologies including phage, yeast, bacteria, and mammalian cell displays have been used for protein evolution (Arslan et al. 2019; Valldorf et al. 2022).
One useful feature of mammalian cell display which is different from other displays is that it can readily evolve posttranslationally modified proteins. Since the efficiency of plasmid transfection into mammalian cells is low, it is extremely difficult to transfect a large mutation library into mammalian cells. Therefore, intracellular random mutation is an appropriate alternative method. Currently, AID is used to induce intracellular mutation (de Brito et al. 2021). AID converts cytosine to uracil which pairs with adenine instead of guanine during transcription, thus generating C to T mutation in the target gene (Yoshikawa et al. 2002). Our laboratory developed a CHO cell-based display system (Chen et al. 2016). Lately, we engineered a mouse AID mutant (mAID-plus) with high mutation efficiency by site-directed mutation and removal of nuclear export signal of mouse AID (Luo et al. 2020). While the efficiency of the antibody affinity maturation was much higher when using mAID-plus than wild-type human or mouse AID, the limitation of these AIDs is obvious. The average content of A/T bases in human genome is approximately 60% (Antonarakis 2016). However, the mutations produced by AID are mainly
concentrated on the G/C bases, which limits the opportunity of mutations at most A/T bases of human antibodies. The introduction of an enzyme that can convert A/T to G/C into the CHO display system would increase the mutation diversity of the target antibody gene and further increase affinity maturation efficiency.
Liu's group developed an adenine base editor (ABE) fused with Escherichia coli adenine deaminase (TadA) and CRISPR-Cas9 for gene edition (Gaudelli et al. 2017). The deamination of adenine forms inosine, which is treated as guanine by polymerase; thus, adenine deaminase is able to convert A/T to G/C on DNA. Most of the studies on ADA focus on specific base(s) in gene editing. The conversion of A/T to G/C in a random manner on a target gene can generate new features of its protein product which cannot be designed or predicted. In a study, adenine and cytosine deaminases were fused with T7 RNA polymerase to achieve random mutation on the target gene in E. coli (Álvarez et al. 2020). However, there has been no study using ADA in the mammalian cell display and antibody/protein affinity maturation. In this study, we applied ADA to our CHO evolutionary system and obtained affinity-improved mutants that were distinctly different from those induced by AID.

Materials and methods

Plasmid construction and expression
His-GFP-TNFα was used to label the CHO cells displaying anti-TNFα antibodies and expressed in \(E\) . coli containing the PET28a \((+)\) -GFP-TNFα plasmid using the procedure of Chen et al. (2012). The GFP-TNFα was functional as a cytotoxicity agent and as a binder for labeling the scFv mutant pool displayed on CHO cells for affinity maturation (Chen et al. 2012). GFP-TAG is a GFP gene with a stop codon TAG in the middle of the gene so it cannot be expressed in cells until reverse mutations occur on the stop codon. pCDNA3.1-GFP-TAG plasmid has been described in previous study (Chen et al. 2012).
pCEP4 plasmid was used to express enzymes that mutate target genes (GFP-TAG or anti-TNFα antibody). Seven such pCEP4 plasmids were used in this study. (1, 2) Both pCEP4-mAID-plus-neo and pCEP4-mAID-plus-bsd express the AID enzyme mAID-plus. They contain neomycin (neo) and blasticidin (bsd), respectively. The details of the two plasmids were described previously (Luo et al. 2020). (3, 4) pCEP4-TadA-7.10 and pCEP4-TadA-8e express the ADAs TadA-7.10 and TadA-8e, respectively. TadA-7.10 and TadA-8e were created by Gaudelli et al. (2017) and Richter et al. (2020). TadA-7.10 and TadA-8e sequences were codon-optimized and synthesized by Genscript biotech company (China). SV40 NLS were added to the N- and
C-termini of TadA-7.10 and TadA-8e, forming NLS-ADANLS. SV40 NLS were added to the N-terminus of mAIDplus and TadA-7.10, forming NLS-AID and NLS-ADA. (5, 6) pCEP4-TadA-7.10-mAID-plus and pCEP4-mAID-plusTadA-7.10 express the fusion enzymes TadA-7.10-mAIDplus and mAID-plus-TadA-7.10, respectively. The EAAAK linker was inserted between TadA-7.10 and mAID-plus. SV40 NLS were added to the N- and C-termini of TadA7.10-linker-mAID-plus. For no. 3–6 plasmids, enzyme DNA sequences were inserted between HindIII and XhoI of the pCEP4 plasmid. (7) pCEP4-promotor-mAID-plus-promotorTadA-7.10 express mAID-plus and TadA-7.10 from their own independent promotors. This plasmid was reconstructed from pCEP4-mAID-plus-neo. Another promoter was added in the pCEP4-mAID-plus-neo plasmid. The promoter-TadA7.10-stop codon fragment was amplified with primer pairs containing the SalI restriction endonuclease site; the PCR product was cut by SalI and the fragment was inserted into the pCEP4-mAID-plus-neo plasmid, so that the two deaminases can be expressed separately in one plasmid.
We used pCDNA3.1 to express the wild type and mutations of anti-TNFα scFv to assess their affinities. The antibody gene with a his-tag on the C-terminus was inserted between EcoRI and XhoI of the pCDNA3.1 plasmid.
All the above-described plasmids were sequenced and confirmed that no errors were introduced into them.

Cell culture
CHO-puro cells and CHO cells expressing anti-TNFα scFv were established previously (Chen et al. 2016; Luo et al. 2020). CHO cells were propagated in IMDM medium (HyClone, SH30228.01) containing \(10\%\) fetal bovine serum (FBS, HyClone, SH30084.03), \(0.1\ \mathrm{mM}\) hypoxanthine, and \(0.016\;\mathrm{mM}\) thymidine (Gibco, 11,067–030), at \(37\ ^{\circ}\mathbf{C}\) in a \(5\%\) \(\mathrm{CO}_{2}\) incubator. Subcultures were carried out every 2–3 days. The 293F cells were propagated in SMM293TII (SB, M293TII) medium at \(37\ ^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\) and \(125\ \mathrm{rpm}\) in a \(5\%\) \(\mathrm{CO}_{2}\) incubator.

Transfection and antibody affinity maturation.
To test the reverse mutation of GFP-TAG, CHO-puro cells were seeded into wells of a six-well plate. The cells were transfected with a mixture of \(1~{\upmu\mathrm{g}}\) pCDNA3.1-GFP-TAG, \(1~\upmu\mathrm{g}\) pCEP4 expression plasmids expressing different deaminases and/or AID, and \(5\,\upmu\mathrm{L}\) PEI MAX (Polysciences, 24,765) for \(4{-}6\,\mathrm{h}\) . The cells were collected and analyzed for reverse mutation rate using FACSAriaIIIu after \(48\,\mathrm{h}\) .
To assess the antigen-binding abilities of mutant scFvs, CHO-puro cells were seeded into wells of a six-well plate. The cells were transfected with a mixture of \(2~\upmu\mathrm{g}\) pCDNA3.1-anti-TNFα-antibody-TM plasmid and \(5\,\upmu\mathrm{L}\) PEI
MAX for \(4{-}6\,\mathrm{h}\). After \(48\,\mathrm{h}\), each cell sample were incubated with APC-conjugated anti-HA antibody (Cayman, 13,406) and GFP-TNFα for \(30\,\mathrm{min}\) at \(4\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\). The antibody displaying level and antigen binding ability were analyzed using FACSAriaIIIu.
In the affinity maturation experiment, the CHO cells that express anti-TNFα-scFv were seeded into a six-well plate. The cells were transfected with \(2\,\upmu\mathrm{g}\) of pCEP4 expression plasmids expressing different deaminases and/or AID and \(5\,\upmu\mathrm{L}\) PEI MAX for \(4{-}6\,\mathrm{h}\) and maintained in IMDM containing \(10\%\) FBS and HT for one night. Then the cells were expanded in IMDM with \(10\%\) FBS, HT, and \(1\,\mathrm{mg/mL}\) G418 disulfate (Amresco, E859-5G) for 11–13 days and incubated with APC-conjugated anti-HA antibody and GFP-TNFα for \(30\,\mathrm{min}\) at \(4\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\), and enriched 20,000 cells that expressed high-affinity scFvs using FACSAriaIIIu. When the enriched cells grew to \(10^{8}\) (named S1 cells), 2000 cells were enriched by fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) and named S2 cells. The genomic DNA of S2 cells was purified with a genomic DNA purification system (Promega, A2360). The scFv genes with a HA tag on the C-terminus were amplified from the genomic DNA with primer pairs that contained NheI and XhoI restriction endonuclease sites; the PCR products containing mixed genes of the S2 cell population enriched by FACS were cut by NheI and XhoI, and the fragments were inserted into a vector with a transmembrane domain at the C-terminus (pCDNA3.1-TM) for sequencing. Each of the 70 clones was randomly selected for DNA sequencing by the Sanger sequencing technology. Primers used for scFv gene PCR were as follows:
scFv-F: ATACGCGCTAGCATGACCCGGCTGAC scFv -R: ATACGCCTCGAGAGCATAATCTGGAA
The PCDNA3.1-anti TNFα-his plasmid was transfected into 293F cells, and the cells were cultured for 5 days. The supernatant was then collected, and anti TNFα-scFv-his was purified with a Ni column (BBI C600791).

Antibody affinity measurement
The affinities of scFvs were measured by the Octet biomolecular interaction technology (Octet Red96). GFP-TNFα protein was conjugated with biotin and concentrated to \(10\,\upmu\mathrm{g/mL}\). The GFP-TNFα-biotin was added and fixed to streptavidin (SA) biosensors. The detection conditions used were (1) baseline \(60\,\mathrm{s}\), (2) loading \(240\,\mathrm{s}\), (3) baseline \(60\,\mathrm{s}\), (4) association \(240\,\mathrm{s}\) with a series of concentrations of antiTNFα scFv, and (5) dissociation \(240\,\mathrm{s}\). The \(K_{\mathrm{on}}\) and \(K_{\mathrm{off}}\) rates were measured by Octet software, and the KD value was calculated for each antibody mutation by the \(K_{\mathrm{off}}/K_{\mathrm{on}}\) ratio by Octet software.

Results

ADA-induced mutation in GFP
In previous studies, we transfected an engineered AID (mAID-plus) gene into CHO cells to induce mutation on an antibody gene, and successfully matured affinities of the antibodies (Chen et al. 2016). The majority of mutants are the converters of G/C to A/T. In this study, we intend to test if ADAs can induce random conversion of A/T to G/C on genes of interest in CHO cells. Gene editing studies showed that recombinant ADAs can convert A/T to G/C of the designated bases of the gene of interest (Kang et al. 2018; Ryu et al. 2018; Yan et al. 2018). We wondered if recombinant ADAs can randomly induce the conversion of A/T to G/C on an interested target gene in CHO cells. In this study, we used the ADA (TadA-7.10) which was originally created to precisely edit specific designated bases (Gaudelli et al. 2017). Since we intended to introduce random conversion, we did not use the Cas9. We constructed two TadA-7.10 plasmids containing either the N-terminus nuclear localization sequence (NLS) or N-terminus as well as C-terminus NLS. The fusion of NLS to either the N-terminus of TadA-7.10 or both of its termini increased its enzyme activity (Koblan et al. 2018). We also constructed NLS-fused mAID-plus enzymes as controls of TadA-7.10 s. We first used a GFP gene to test whether the ADAs can perform reverse mutation. There is a TAG stop codon in the middle of the GFP gene, and GFP can be normally expressed only after reverse mutation occurs on the stop codon, so the GFP fluorescence can be detected in cells.
In this study, we cloned both AID and ADA into an expression plasmid, and transfected into CHO cells together with GFP-TAG plasmid. The fluorescence ratios of the cells bearing AID with N-terminus NLS and two-termini NLS were \(1.33\%\) and \(0.964\%\), respectively. The fluorescence ratios of the cells bearing ADA with N-terminus NLS and two-termini NLS were \(4.28\%\) and \(4.73\%\), respectively. The results showed that there was little difference in the mutation efficiency of a deaminase with N-terminus NLS and two-termini NLS. For AID, the mutation efficiency of the N-terminus NLS enzyme was better, while the efficiency of two-termini is better for TadA-7.10. Therefore, NLS-AID and NLS-ADA-NLS are selected for the later experiment (Fig. 1).

Affinity maturation by simultaneous use of mAID-plus and TadA-7.10
The size of library diversity is an important factor for obtaining affinity-improved mutants. In the previous section, we showed that ADA can produce A/T to G/C mutations. Then we wondered about the effect of the combination of AID and ADA. There are 3 different ways of combining AID and ADA. First, we fused the two deaminases with only one driving promoter in one expression plasmid (fusion enzymes: AID-ADA and ADA-AID). Second, we constructed an expression plasmid containing promoter-AID-stop codon followed by promoter-ADA-stop codon (double promoters). Third, we inserted AID and ADA in two different plasmids and transfected cells with the two plasmids together (double plasmids). We transfected the above-described 4 sets of plasmids into cells separately, and enriched cells twice, and sequenced 70 scFv clones from each of the enriched cell samples as described in the last
section (Fig. 4). The frequencies of mutations and numbers of mutation types were calculated (Fig. 5). The mutation frequencies and numbers of mutation types induced by AIDADA, double promotors, and double plasmids are close to each other. The mutation frequency induced by ADA-AID is obviously lower than those of the other three samples, and its number of mutation types is slightly smaller than those of the other three samples.
In all the 4 samples, mutations occurred on both A/T and G/C, and the majority of the mutations fits into the model of G/C to A/T conversion or the model of A/T to G/C conversion (Fig. 6 and Table 2). There are exceptions. For instance, the base G543 changed into A543, T543, and C543, and these mutations changed Met to Ile. The change of this amino acid residue happened in all the 4 cell samples.

We chose those mutant scFv clones which have either a large \(\mathrm{GM}_{\mathrm{GFP}}\) and/or a large OF for their affinity measurement. The KD value (Table S1) of the wild type is \(2.48\mathrm{E}-06\) , and all the KD values from the tested mutant clones which are able to bind to GFP-TNFα are lower than the wild-type KD value, suggesting that the affinities of all these mutants surpass that of the wild type. The KD values of the top three clones \((\mathrm{A}495\mathrm{G}+\mathrm{A}719\mathrm{T}\) , A719T, and A718T) are \(4.65\mathrm{E}-08\) , \(8.55\mathrm{E}-08\) , and \(8.22\mathrm{E}-08\) . Of note, the point mutations A719T and A718T do not fit into the model of A/T to \(\mathrm{G}/\mathrm{C}\) conversion. Furthermore, we performed rank correlation analysis on the MFI and KD value of selected scFvs. Spearman's rank coefficient of correlation is − 0.802, \(P\!<\!0.001\) . Therefore, MFI is positively correlated with the affinity of scFv. The data presented here indicate that the combined use of AID and ADA can efficiently induce more diverse point mutations and improve affinity.

Discussion
Previously, our laboratory created an AID enzyme (mAIDplus) which can induce mutations with great efficiency on the target gene to improve affinity maturation (Luo et al. 2020). AID-induced random mutations are mainly concentrated on C/G bases, and most of the A/T bases cannot be mutated by AID, which limits the diversity of mutation libraries. In this study, we applied ADA to the artificial evolutionary system based on our CHO cell display, increased the diversity of random mutations, and quickly obtained single-chain antibodies with higher affinities (Tables 1, 2, and S1).
The ADAs TadA-7.10 and TadA-8e were originally created in Liu's laboratory for gene edition (Antonarakis 2016; Yan et al. 2018). These enzymes are able to induce mutations on multiple A/T bases in the anti-TNFα scFv gene (Table 1) in our CHO cell system. None of the mutations induced by ADAs occurred on C/G bases. All the ADA-induced mutations except one (A719T) are A/T to G/C (Table 1). These features largely reflect the mechanism of action of ADA, and compensate for the inability of AID to efficiently induce mutations on A/T bases, thus increasing opportunities to create more point mutations at more sites in genes of interest for maturation. AID has preference for sequences surrounding the mutation sites, the hotspots of mutation are RGYW and WRC (R, purine A/G; Y, pyrimidine C/T; W, A/T) sequence, and the cold spot is SYC \((\mathbf{S}\!=\!\mathbf{G}/\mathbf{C})\) sequence (Pham et al. 2003; Rogozin and Kolchanov 1992). The data presented
here are limited to informing if TadA-7.10 and TadA-8e have preference for the base sequences surrounding the A/T bases that are mutated by the enzymes. Significantly more data will be needed to give such information. In the process of mutation induced by AID and ADA, we do not know whether there is a competition between the two enzymes. We cannot tell whether there is a possibility that a base will be mutated by one enzyme and then mutated back by another. In our data, we did identify some clones that contain both types of mutations. Therefore, it is feasible to use the two enzymes for co-mutagenesis for affinity maturation.
The adenine deamination activity of TadA-8e is 590 times that of TadA-7.10 (Richter et al. 2020). TadA-8e was developed from its parent deaminase TadA-7.10 by screening for mutations which give high efficiencies of gene edition to the deaminase. However, the frequency and the number of mutation types in anti-TNFα scFv induced by TadA-8e are not significantly higher than those of TadA-7.10. This result suggests that the gene editing rate is not equivalent to the rate of random mutations, and ADAs with a higher mutation rate might be developed by screening for high random mutation efficiency.
In this study, we were able to induce diverse mutations with four different combinations of mAID-plus and TadA-7.10, and improved the antibody affinity in the majority of the tested scFv clones. The amino acid positions of all point mutations were analyzed (Table S1). All mutation sites except G389A and G428A are in the variable region. T328G and C344T are located in the CDR3 region of the heavy chain, and the A715G, A718T, A719T, and G721A are located in the CDR3 region of the light chain. The results of affinity showed that these mutations located in the CDR3 region, except for T328G, contributed largely to the affinity. This platform simultaneously using AID and ADA is an easy and efficient method to mature antibody affinity. We also selected A/T point mutations and G/C point mutations to combine them into one antibody, and this antibody had a higher affinity than the antibodies containing single point mutation (Table S2). This is another strategy that can be applied to achieve higher affinity in the future.
