Abstract
Hippo signaling pathway is an evolutionarily conserved network that regulates organ size growth and tissue regeneration. Hippo signaling dysfunction results in uncontrolled cell proliferation and influences cell differentiation. Aberrant Hippo pathway signaling is implicated in cancer progression, by promoting cell proliferation, cancer stem cell properties, chemoresistance and metastatic capacity. EpithelialвҖ“mesenchymal transition (EMT) is also well known to be implicated in carcinogenesis. Loss of cell polarity, disruption of cellвҖ“cell junctions and cytoskeletal remodeling are essential during EMT. At the same time, signals related to intercellular contact, cell-extracellular matrix contact, polarity and mechanotransduction are included in the list of regulatory inputs into Hippo pathway. Therefore, the emerging association between Hippo pathway and EMT in cancer is not surprising. Recent studies have begun to unravel the mechanisms of interaction between Hippo signaling pathway and EMT. In this review, we describe the existing evidence of cross talk between Hippo signaling pathway key molecules and the process of EMT, with emphasis on the role of HippoвҖ”EMT interplay in cancer.

Introduction
Hippo signaling pathway is a highly conserved pathway that controls organ growth and tissue homeostasis. Initially described in Drosophila melanogaster, Hippo pathway was later found to be conserved across higher order vertebrates and is currently known to play a fundamental role in developmental biology [1]. The core key components of Hippo pathway in mammals are two kinase complexes, the mammalian sterile 20-like kinases (MSTs) and the large tumor suppressor 1/2 kinases (LATS1/2). These kinase complexes interact mainly with two adaptor proteins. MST1/2 kinases form a complex with the adaptor protein Salvador homolog 1 (SAV1) resulting in phosphorylation and activation of LATS1/2 [1]. Activated LATS1/2 binds to MOB kinase activator 1A/B (MOB1A/B) and inhibits the transcriptional activator YAP (Yes-associated protein) and TAZ (transcriptional co-activator with PDZ-binding motif). YAP and TAZ are the major targets of the core Hippo pathway kinases. Tumor suppressor neurofibromin 2 (NF2), also known as Merlin, is part of Hippo pathway upstream of these kinases and promotes the activation of the pathway in order to inhibit YAP/TAZ activation. Another upstream regulator of Hippo pathway is the family of WWC (WW-and-C2-domain-containing) proteins, consisting of the cytosolic scaffolding proteins
WWC1 (also known as Kibra), WWC2 and WWC3, that activate LATS kinases resulting in inhibition of YAP/TAZ nuclear import. Once phosphorylated by LATS1/2, YAP/ TAZ proteins bind to 14-3-3 proteins resulting in cytoplasmic sequestration (see Figs.В 1, 2).
When Hippo signaling pathway is inhibited, YAP/TAZ are activated and able to translocate into the nucleus [2]. However, YAP and TAZ lack a DNA-binding domain. Thus, the access of YAP/TAZ to DNA depends on the association with transcription factors. The main family of transcription factors that YAP/TAZ predominantly use, are the TEA domain (TEAD) family members TEAD1-4, thus regulating the expression of specific targets such as the essential for stem cell reniewal transcription factor Sox2, CTGF (connective tissue growth factor), BIRC5 (baculoviral inhibitor of apoptosis repeat-containing 5), or Cyr61 (cysteine-rich angiogenic inducer 61) [3, 4]. The target genes whose expression is regulated by nuclear YAP/TAZ collectively control several cancer related processes, such as cell proliferation, differentiation and stemness, mechanotransduction, resistance to therapy, metabolic reprogramming, immunosuppression and angiogenesis. Other YAP/TAZ transcriptional partners such as Smad have been identified. Therefore, YAP/TAZ can be considered as oncogenes and the core kinases as tumor suppressor genes. Hippo signaling dysfunction has been reported in several cancers, such as breast, brain, liver, lung, prostate, gastric, pancreatic and colorectal cancer (CRC) [2].

Emerging evidence suggests that in cancer there is an interplay between Hippo signaling pathway and EpithelialвҖ“mesenchymal transition (EMT). EMT is a transdifferentiation program whereby epithelial cells acquire the migratory, spindle-shaped phenotype of mesenchymal cells. EMT occurs normally during embryonic development and wound healing, and pathologically in cancer and fibrosis [5]. Loss of cell polarity, disruption of cellвҖ“cell junctions and cytoskeletal remodeling are essential during EMT. Since the regulatory inputs to the Hippo signaling pathway include signals related to cellвҖ“cell contact, cell-extracellular matrix contact, cell polarity and mechanotransduction [6], it is not surprising that a link between Hippo signaling and EMT has emerged. It is often unclear whether Hippo signaling pathway is an inducer of EMT, or downstream of EMT, or both. It is possible that there is a self sustaining loop, that promotes cancer, between epithelial-mesenchymal transdifferentiation and Hippo pathway regulation [6]. A review focusing on the interactions between Hippo pathway and EMT in cancer is so far lacking in the scientific literature. The purpose of this review is to describe the recent evidence on the association of Hippo pathway key components with EMT, and to highlight its importance in cancer initiation, progression and metastasis.

YAP/TAZ expression correlates withВ EMT inВ several types ofВ cancer
The activation of Hippo effectors YAP/TAZ and the induction of EMT, have both separately been associated with several hallmaks of cancer. Nevertheless, numerous studies suggested that there is a connection between YAP/TAZ and EMT. In 2006, YAP expression in mammary epithelial cells was shown to be able to induce EMT, anchorage-independent growth, aberrant cell proliferation and inhibition of apoptosis [7]. TAZ has also been shown to stimulate cell proliferation, inhibit anoikis and promote EMT in mammary epithelial cells [8]. Since these first studies in mammary epithelial cells, accumulating evidence suggests that Hippo pathway is an important inducer of cancer related EMT. YAP was correlated with EMT in colorectal [9, 10], breast [11], lung [12, 13], pancreratic [14, 15], head and neck [16], esophageal [17], endometrial [18], ovarian [19], gastric [20], brain [21] cancer, as well as in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) [22]. TAZ has also been linked with the process of EMT in breast cancer [23], especially hormone-receptor negative, oral and esophageal squamous cell cancers [17], gastric [24], thyroid cancer, especially papillary type [25, 26] and gliomas [27]. Thus, reports in various types of cancer confirmed the link between YAP/TAZ and EMT [28]. In these reports the expression of mesenchymal markers such as vimentin and N-cadherin correlated negatively with the expression of epithelial markers such as E-cadherin, and this was associated with the upregulation of YAP and TAZ, as well as with downregulation of LATS1 and MST [25, 28].
Aberrant Hippo pathway signaling has also been correlated with preneoplastic lesions, such as in Helicobacter pylori induced EMT and intestinal metaplasia in gastric mucosa, where LATS2 plays an important role in maintaining the epithelial phenotype [29], whereas YAP has been linked to carcinogenesis [30]. Another such example is basal and squamous cell carcinoma where YAP/TAZ where found to correlate with EMT and to be key determinants in cancer initiation [31].
Remarkably, YAP/TAZ upregulation and EMT have been correlated with tumor aggressiveness and worse clinical characteristics and outcome in several types of cancer, such as colorectal cancer [10, 32], breast cancer [11, 33], gastric cancer [34] and pancreatic cancer [35]. Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), characterized by aggressive behavior and poorer prognosis compared with other types of breast cancer, is one such example. In a recent study examining the role of HMGA2 (High Mobility Group AT-Hook 2) in TNBC pathogenesis both the downregulation of EMT related markers and the reduction in YAP expression were concurrently associated with HMGA2 inhibition [36].
Recently, we started to gain new insights into the underlying mechanisms of interaction between YAP/TAZ and core Hippo kinases with EMT (see Fig.В 3).

Direct interactions betweenВ YAP/TAZ andВ theВ major EMT related transcription factors
The first mechanism of cross-talk between Hippo pathway and EMT is the ability of YAP/TAZ to directly interact with EMT transcription factors (see Fig.В 2). Tang etВ al. demonstrated that the EMT master regulators Snail and Slug (also known as Snail1 and Snail2) form complexes with the Hippo pathway transcriptional co-activators YAP and TAZ, and via these complexes cooperatively regulate mesenchymal stem cell function [37, 38]. Recently, Snail-YAP interactions were documented to promote gastric cancer related EMT, as a result of the interaction between LATS1/2 and the tight junction family member claudin6, resulting in YAP translocation into the nucleus [20]. Interestingly, another EMT master regulator, ZEB1 was also shown to directly interact with Hippo transcriptional co-activator YAP. Lehmann etВ al. showed that ZEB1 directly binds to YAP, but not to TAZ, and this cooperation regulates the expression of a вҖҳcommon ZEB1/YAP target gene set'. This ZEB1/YAP gene set is correlated with poor survival, resistance to therapy and metastasis in hormone receptor-negative breast cancer [33]. Evidence suggests that in aggressive breast cancers YAP, ZEB1 and JUN (AP-1) transcription factors cooperate to form a transcriptional activator complex [11]. In this way, ZEB1 is capable of acting as a transcriptional activator of the expression of tumor promoting protein targets. Conversely, when ZEB1 binds on DNA with its canonical E-box motif, without the functional cooperation with YAP and AP-1, it acts as a transcriptional suppressor of epithelial genes. Except from aggressive breast cancer, it has also been shown that in pancreatic cancer cells YAP forms a complex with the zinc-dependent EMT transcription factor ZEB1, and this is regulated by zinc transporter ZIP4 [14].

CellвҖ“cell contact andВ cytoskeleton related molecules mediate Hippo pathwayвҖ”EMT interactions
The second mechanism of interaction between YAP/TAZ and EMT is mediated by molecules associated with intercellular junctions and contact inhibition. CellвҖ“cell contact plays an important role in both EMT and Hippo pathway regulation. E-cadherin is the major component in adhjerens junctions between epithelial cells. The loss of E-cadherin and the disruption of cellвҖ“cell junctions is essential EMT. The cytosolic tail of E-cadherin via members of the family of catenins is bound to the cytoskeleton and mediates signals towards downstream pathways, including Hippo signaling pathway [39]. Accordingly, a-catenin as well as tight junction-polarity complexes, such as Crumbs, have been found to activate Hippo pathway and mediate contact inhibition (see Fig.В 1)В [39]. This is evident in cell cultures as a decrease in nuclear YAP expression, when the cells grow at higher density. Thus, Hippo pathway is not only an inducer of EMT, but it is also regulated downstream of EMT related molecules. Recently, it has been shown that in lung cancer cells E-cadherin cooperates with a large GTPase activating protein (p190A) to activate LATS kinases, promote the phosphorylation of the Hippo pathway transducer YAP and induce contact inhibition of proliferation (see Fig.В 1)В [40]. P190A knockdown was also shown to promote EMT at least in part through activation of Hippo-YAP signaling transcriptional outputs in endometrial cancer [18]. Furthermore, overexpression of YAP and depletion of the Hippo pathway components Merlin, LATS and Kibra blocks the E-cadherinвҖ“mediated contact inhibition of proliferation [41]. TAZ has also been shown to reduce cell contact inhibition as wells as stimulate cell proliferation and promote EMT in mammary epithelial cells [8]. Furhermore, the member of the tight junction family Claudin6 was shown to interact with LATS1/2 facilitating the translocation of YAP into the nucleus [20]. The cadherin family member FAT4, was shown to regulate YAP in ovarian cancer cells and the loss FAT4 was promote EMT via YAP regulation [19]. Thus, transmembrane proteins and their adaptors play an important role in mediating HippoвҖ”EMT interactions.
Beyond these membrane-related molecules, mechanical cues produced by the EMT-associated cytoskeletal remodeling also regulate the activation of Hippo pathway. The cytoskeletal actin remodeling during the process EMT in HPV infection associated cancers, has been correlated with the induction of Hippo pathway by promoting the cytoplasmic retention of inactive P-YAP [42]. Specifically, the actin binding protein gelsolin is implicated in EMT-related cytoskeleton remodeling partly by regulating YAP activity. Accordingly, in another study on bladder cancer, a gelsolin-family actin-modulating protein, CAPG (gelsolin-like actin-capping protein) has been shown to promote nuclear aggregation of YAP inВ vitro and inВ vivo by regulating the Hippo pathway, which in turn promotes EMT and tumor aggressiveness [43].

CellвҖ‘polarity related molecules andВ Hippo pathwayвҖ”EMT crossВ talk
The third mechanism underlying Hippo pathway and EMT correlations is related to cell polarity and intriguingly, cell polarity-associated cancer stemness. Overexpression of TAZ in breast cancer cells has been shown to promote not only EMT and invasion, but also self-renewal of cancer stem cells [44]. It has also been shown that the EMT-related acquisition and maintenance of stem cell traits in breast cancer cells is dependent on TAZ [23]. Interestingly, a new mechanism underlying the regulation of cancer stemness by cell polarity has been revealed. Scribble is a major gatekeeper of epithelial polarity, normally localized at the basolateral membrane. Scribble, is also an upstream regulator of Hippo pathway, leading to inhibition of TAZ, by participating in the recruitment of MST2 to the LATS/TAZ complex. The loss of apicobasal polarity during EMT, results in the translocation of Scribble from the membrane into the cytoplasm. Thus, inactivated Scribble disrupts the inhibitory association between TAZ and the Hippo pathway core kinases. Furthermore, Scribble via LATS regulates a member of the Ski family named SnoN. The transcriptional cofactor SnoN binds to Hippo pathway molecules, including LATS and MST, and inhibits TAZ phosphorylation, enhancing the protumorigenic activity of TAZ (see Figs.В 1, 2)В [44, 45]. Certainly, the mechanisms underlying the self-renewal capacity of cancer stem cells of various cancer types probably differ. For example, in an aggressive oral squamous cell carcinoma cell line that forms spheres the CSC self-renewal is dependent on a LATS2-TAZ-Snail axis in a Scribble-independent manner [46].

Indirect interactions: EMT related molecules are included inВ theВ list ofВ YAP/TAZ target genes
Mechanistically, YAP/TAZ can also regulate EMT transcription factors, and vice versa, by indirect interactions, without immediate physical contact.
Firstly, EMT-related molecules are included in the list of YAP/TAZ target genes [47]. Notably, the expression of the EMT-master regulators ZEB, Snail, Slug and Twist has been found by several investigators to be upregulated as a result of YAP/TAZ overexpression. In clear cell renal cell cancer YAP has been shown to control Twist expression at the transcriptional level by direct binding of TEAD1 to the gene promoter region of Twist [48]. In non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) cells YAP has been shown to bind to TEAD and transcriptionally activate the EMT related transcription factor Slug. Furthermore, silencing YAP resulted in EMT inhibition and downregulation of Slug expression inВ vivo [49]. YAP was also shown to promote EMT by directly transcriptionally activating Slug expression in colorectal cancer cells [50]. In another recent study examining gastric carcinogenesis, TAZ has been found to induce EMT and cancer stem cell properties via ZEB1 upregulation in response to $H$ . pylori infection [24]. In gastric carcinoma cells the induction of EMT has also been correlated with YAP signaling activity regulation by SOX9, a member of SOX family of transcription factors that control cell fate and differentiation [51]. In oral squamous cell carcinoma the EMT transcription factors Twist, Snail, Slug and ZEB were upregulated upon TAZ overexpression [52].
Secondly, apart from the EMT-master regulators, other molecules known to play a crucial role in EMT, including AKT signaling pathway, $\mathrm{Wnt/\upbeta}$ -catenin signaling, hypoxia-inducible factor- $1\upalpha$ (HIF1A) and transcription factor FOS, have been reported to be regulated by YAP/ TAZ signaling. In pancreatic cancer cells YAP has been found to induce EMT and chemoresistance, through several mechanisms, such as hyperactivation of AKT signaling pathway, known to regulate EMT [35]. In another study in pancreatic cancer cells YAP was found to interact with HIF1A and to activate Snail, thus inducing EMT [53]. A positive HIF1A/YAP loop in pancreatic cancer has also been confirmed by another recent study [54]. In colon cancer and pancreatic cancer YAP has been found to correlate with worse clinical features and outcome [32, 35]. Interestingly, in colon and pancreatic cancer YAP has been found to promote survival of cancer cells after KRAS suppression. YAP1 signaling is able to functionally replace KRAS in KRAS-dependent cancer cells. Mechanistically, it was shown that YAP1 and FOS regulate EMT in order to rescue the viability of cancer cells upon KRAS suppression [32]. In laryngeal cancer cells YAP inhibition suppresses tumor growth by inhibiting EMT and $\mathrm{{Wnt/\upbeta}}$ - catenin signaling [55]. $\mathrm{Wnt/\upbeta}$ -catenin signaling-induced EMT was also correlated with TAZ overexpression in a recent study in pancreatic cancer cells [56].

Indirect interactions: EMT related transcription factors regulate molecules thatВ control YAP/TAZ andВ core kinases
EMT transcription factors can regulate YAP/TAZ activity indirectly by influencing other molecules that control YAP/TAZ or the core kinases. One such example is the thrombin receptor-mediated regulation of Hippo pathway by Twist. The inhibition of Hippo pathway has been found to be required for the invasive phenotype and the stemness in Twist-mediated EMT [57, 58]. Twist a basic helix-loophelix transcription factor is among the EMT master regulators. Recently, it has been documented that Twist can downregulate Hippo pathway by transcriptional induction of the expression of the thrombin receptor (also known as protease-activated receptor 1, PAR1) [58]. PAR1 promotes EMT and cancer stem cell properties through LATS inhibition, resulting in YAP/TAZ activation [57, 58]. Another example of the regulation of Hippo pathway by the EMTrelated transcription factors is the recent finding of the regulation of YAP expression by ZEB1 in hepatocellular carcinoma cells [59].

Hippo pathway andВ partial EMT inВ collective cell migration
Another mechanism of regulation of EMT by Hippo pathway is implicated in collective cell migration. Recently, YAP has been shown to regulate the acquisition of EMT properties in collective cell migration. The process of EMT is not an ON/OFF phenomenon. Epithelial cells undergoing EMT can acquire some mesenchymal features, without full transdifferentiation into the mesenchymal phenotype. This is called partial EMT and these cells are epithelial/mesenchymal hybrids [60]. In expanding epithelial sheets it has been shown that cells at the edges undergo full-EMT, whereas submarginal cells undergo partial-EMT. Interestingly, YAP has been shown to control this process during a switch-like enhancement of EMT, by receiving mechanical cues and interacting with the E-cadherin expression suppressor Wilms Tumor protein (WT1) [61].

Epigenetic regulatory mechanisms linking Hippo pathway andВ EMT
An emerging mechanism that controls HippoвҖ”EMT interactions is through epigenetic modifications, including microRNAs, long non-coding RNAs and DNA methylation.
Micro RNAs (miRNAs) play an important role in posttranscriptional regulation of gene expression, by pairing with complementary sequences within mRNA molecules. YAP was found to be a direct target of miR-375 in prostate cancer. This microRNA is inversely correlated with EMT and has been shown to be directly repressed by the EMT related transcription factor ZEB1 [62]. Thus, a regulatory ZEB1/miR-375/YAP1 circuit regulates EMT in prostate cancer. Furthermore, miR-375 is highly expressed in Merkel cell carcinoma, where it has also been documented that its target genes are involved in Hippo and EMT-associated pathways [63]. In an inВ vitro study in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) cells it was reported that miR-497 promoter methylation and the subsequent inhibition of miR-497 transcription and its target YAP, results in NOTCH signaling pathway activation, which promotes EMT [64]. Another miRNA has been found to induce EMT and stemness in breast cancer through YAP/Hippo pathway. Specifically, miR-520b promotes stemness in breast cancer cells by enhancing Hippo/ YAP signaling pathway through LATS2. This is in accordance with the fact that miR-520b is upregulated in cancer tissues of breast cancer patients and it predicts poor prognosis [65]. Recently, in esophageal squamous cell carcinoma miR-34a-5p has been reported to inhibit EMT migration and invasion by decreasing the expression of YAP and TAZ in a LEF1-dependent way (targeting lymphoid enhancer-binding factor 1) [17]. Another regulatory mRNA in esophageal squamous cell carcinoma that has been identified is miR-31, whose overexpression results in LATS2 inhibition as well as in EMT activation [66]. In gastric cancer cells miR-664a-3p expression is significantly upregulated and it promotes YAP/ TAZ nuclear accumulation by downregulating MOB1A, and it also promotes EMT related tumor progression [34]. In HCC carcinoma cells, miR-1254 expression resulted in decreased phosphorylated LATS1 (p-LATS1) and phosphorylated YAP (p-YAP), as well as in increased vimentin and Slug and decreased E-cadherin [22]. Thus, miR-1254 in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) cells promotes EMT and this is related to Hippo signaling downregulation. In HCC cells another microRNA, miR-103 promotes metastasis and EMT by directly inhibiting LATS2 [67]. In colorectal cancer cells miR-125a-5p functions as an important tumor suppressor and inhibits EMT by targeting TAZ [68]. The list of miRNAs that regulate the Hippo-EMT interplay is constantly growing [56, 69вҖ“72].
DNA methylation is another major epigenetic mechanism of regulation of gene expression. The histone methyltransferase EZH2 (Enhancer of zeste homolog 2) promotes carcinogenesis by regulating several cancer related processes such as EMT [73]. Interestingly, EZH2 is a key molecule in silencing the expression of WWC1 (Kibra), an upstream Hippo pathway effector that has been found to suppress EMT in breast cancer [74].
Apart from miRNAs, long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) are also emerging as important regulators in gene expression. YAP and the EMT transcription factors Snail1 and Twist have been found to be dephosphorylated by a lncRNAdirected PTEN enzymatic switch [75]. Therefore, YAP/ Snail/Twist are collectively activated and induce EMT in a lncRNA dependent regulatory network.

TEAD family ofВ transcription factors andВ EMT
YAP and TAZ lack a DNA-binding domain and rely mainly on TEAD family of transcription factors to exert their effects on gene expression. In mammary epithelial cells, TEAD binding is important for TAZ to induce mesenchymal-like morphological changes [76]. Silencing of TEAD expression was found to inhibit TAZ from promoting cell proliferation and inducing EMT [76]. A recent study has shown that increased nuclear TEAD4 expression in tissue samples from CRC patients predicts poor prognosis, whereas inВ vitro data from the same authors suggest that TEAD4 directly upregulates the expression of the EMT-related transcription factor SIX1 in CRC cells [77]. Accordingly, in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, TEAD4 overexpression promotes EMT and correlates with aggressiveness and worse prognosis [78]. In another recent report, poor prognosis of patients with HCC was correlated with high expression of TEAD2. High mRNA expression of TEAD2 was also strongly associated with genes related to EMT and angiogenesis [79]. TAZ-TEADs interaction and transcriptional activation were found to be essential for EMT and CSCs in oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC). Specifically, the EMT transcription factors (Twist, Snail, Slug) and CSCs regulators (c-myc, Sox2) were significantly decreased upon either TAZ or TEADs knockdown. Several factors important for pluripotency and selfвҖҗrenewal such as Sox2 were found to contain putative TEAD-binding sites and were bound by TEADs complex during stem cell renewal [52]. Sox2 is also known to regulate EMT in esophageal squamous cell carcinoma [80]. Recently, in a study examining clear cell renal cancer progression and metastasis, TEAD1 was found to control Twist expression at the transcriptional level by directly binding to its promoter region [48].
Interestingly, TEAD transcription factors integrate signals not only from Hippo pathway, but also from various other signal transduction pathways including Wnt, $\mathrm{TGF}\upbeta$ , and
EGFR signaling [81]. The majority of studies are focused on the Hippo pathway core kinases and YAP/TAZ. However, the Hippo-dependent and the Hippo-independent regulatory network governing TEAD functions only emerged recently.

WWC proteins andВ EMT
Evidence suggests that the levels of the cytosolic scaffolding proteins WWC1, WWC2 and WWC3 that negatively control YAP activity through LATS 1/2 activation, play an important role in several types of cancer partly through regulating the process of EMT [82]. In breast cancer cells the loss of function of WWC1 (also known as Kibra) induces EMT by EGFR signaling activation, through AREG induction in a YAP1 dependent manner [83]. In HCC patients reduced WWC2 expression correlated with adverse clinicopathological features and overall survival, whereas in HCC cells it suppresses EMT and invasion by downregulating Hippo signaling [84]. In pancreatic cancer stem cells the inhibition of miR-10a resulted in reduced EMT and stemness maintenance via enhancing the expression of the scaffolding protein WWC2 [85]. In CRCs the downregulation of WWP2 by the long noncoding RNA LINC00460 binding to the promoter of WWC2 results in increased expression of the downstream target genes of Snail, Twist and N-cadherin, thus facilitating the process of EMT [86]. In tissue specimens from lung cancer patients the expression of WWC3 negatively correlates with the expression of the EMT related transcription factors Snail and Slug. In human lung cancer cell cultures WWC3 knockdown induces EMT, whereas WWC3 expression downregulates Snail and Slug by activating Hippo signaling via LATS1 phosphorylation of YAP [12].

Therapeutic perspectives
Hippo signaling pathway components represent promising therapeutic targets in cancer [87]. One strategy could be the enhancement of the activity of the core tumor-suppressive kinases. NEDD8-activating enzyme (NAE) mediates the degradation of LATS1/2, consequently increasing the activity of YAP. NAE inhibitor pevonedistat was used in patients with haematologic malignancies and advanced-stage solid tumors with encouraging results. Accordingly, in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma cells the transfection with LATS1 expressing plasmids inhibited metastasis and EMT [88].
Another Hippo signaling-related anticancer strategy could be the inhibition of the oncogenic role of YAP, TAZ and their association with TEAD. Therefore, YAP inhibitors, TAZ inhibitors and blockers of YAP/TAZ-TEAD could be promising new agents in oncologic treatment. YAP1 inhibitor CA3 was effective in mouse models of oesophageal and colorectal cancer [9, 89]. In a colorectal cancer cell line study CA3 resulted in induction of the epithelial marker E-cadherin and downregulation of the mesenchymal marker vimentin, thus YAP inhibition promoted the downregulation of the process of EMT [9]. Interestingly, in a murine CRC model developed by the same authors, the primary tumor was almost abolished and the survival rate was improved by the use of chemotherapy combined with the YAP inhibitor [9]. Super-TDU, a peptide that antagonists YAP1 binding to TEAD inhibited tumor growth in mouse models of gastric and colorectal cancer [89]. TAZ inhibitor AR-42 suppressed proliferation, induced apoptosis and cell cycle arrest and inhibited the expression of EMT and cancer stem cell related proteins in oral cancer cells [90]. Furthermore, in cisplatin resistant nasopharyngeal cancer cells depletion of TAZ reverses EMT phenotypes to MET characteristics and restores chemosensitivity [91]. Accordingly, in cisplatin-resistant gastric cancer cells overexpression of TAZ correlates with EMT [92]. In glioma cells, inhibition of YAP-TEAD association represses EMT [21]. Furthermore, in glioma cells where TAZ has also been found to play an important role [27], blocking TAZ-TEAD binding resulted in tumor suppression by repressing EMT [93]. Therefore, YAP/TAZ-TEAD complex inhibitors represent promising therapeutic targets in malignant gliomas.
Natural compounds can also exert anticancer properties related to the inhibition of Hippo pathway and EMT in neoplastic cells. Luteolin a flavonoid found in fruits and vegetables was found to suppress EMT in triple-negative breast cancer by inhibiting YAP/TAZ activity [94]. In pancreatic cancer, an inВ vitro study on the anticancer effects of catechol, a compound found in some fruits and vegetables, showed that catechol can downregulate the expression of EMTrelated markers and enhance the chemo and radiosensitivity of cancer cells by inhibiting YAP expression via induction of phosphorylation of AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) [15].

Conclusions
There is emerging evidence that a deadly cross-talk between Hippo signaling pathway and EMT promotes tumor initiation and progression. Recently, we started to gain new insights into the underlying mechanisms of interaction between Hippo pathway core molecules and EMT-related proteinsВ (see Fig.В 3). Firstly, there are direct interactions between YAP/TAZ and Snail/Slug, as well as direct interactions between TAZ and ZEB. Secondly, molecules associated with intercellular junctions, cytoskeleton and cell polarity, such as a-catenin, claudin-6, cadherin FAT4, Crumbs polarity complex and Scribble, have been found to regulate YAP/TAZ activity and their interplay with the process of EMT, which is not surprising considering that cellвҖ“cell contact plays an important role in both
EMT and Hippo pathway. Thirdly, epigenetic modification targets regulate concomitantly Hippo pathway and EMT and the list of the related microRNAs and other epigenetic regulatory mechanisms is constantly growing. Finally, Hippo pathway molecules and EMT-related markers can influence each other reciprocally without direct physical interaction. Accordingly, the EMT-related transcription factors can be overexpressed upon YAP/TAZ upregulation and at the same time they can regulate the activity of molecules that influence Hippo pathway members. Thus, in cancer cells Hippo pathway and EMT can interact and sometimes it is unclear whether Hippo pathway induces EMT, or is downstream of EMT or there is a self-sustaining loop. Indisputably, Hippo and EMT have been correlated with worse clinical outcome in several types of cancer. Therefore, the cross-talk of Hippo pathway with EMT is an emerging reason why inhibition of YAP/TAZ activity is a promising therapeutic strategy in cancer treatment.
