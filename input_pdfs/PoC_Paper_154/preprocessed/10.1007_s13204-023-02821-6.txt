Abstract
Dehydrogenation studies are performed on $\mathbf{Mg}(\mathrm{H}_{2})$ upon the addition of 3 atomic wt $\%$ Ni and 10 wt $\%$ of electrochemically exfoliated reduced graphene oxide (rGO) via high-energy ball milling. During non-isothermal dehydrogenation, the onset temperature of dehydrogenation from $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ is seen at ${\sim}275^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ when heated at $5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ per min. This is significantly lower than the onset temperature of uncatalyzed $\mathrm{\Delta\mathrm{MgH}}_{2}$ i.e., ${\sim}400^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . Ni and $\operatorname{rGO}$ together alleviate the incubation period and increase the gravimetric capacity of dehydrogenation compared to their individual presence. High-energy ball milling of Mg, Ni, and rGO establishes $\mathrm{{\bf{M}}g{-}}\mathrm{{C}}$ and $_{\mathrm{Ni-O-C}}$ interactions. After subjecting to isothermal hydrogenation at $\sim15$ bar and $\sim320~\mathrm{^{\circ}C}$ , $\mathrm{Ni-C}$ and $\mathrm{C-H}$ interactions form. Also, the formation of an intermediate $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ phase incorporates lattice contraction in $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell causing structural catalysis. These multiple interactions and structural catalysis in $\mathbf{Mg}$ -Ni-rGO cause the formation of various locally catalyzed phases, which help in dehydrogenation at ${\sim}275^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . These combined catalytic effect and structural catalysis are retained even after subjecting these nanocomposites to ten hydrogenation/dehydrogenation cycles.

Introduction
Magnesium is propitious in terms of hydrogen storage in the solid host for fuel cell application. However, its dehydrogenation temperature is very high $\left(>400^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\right)$ (Schlapbach and Z√ľttel 2001; Jain et¬†al. 2010). The kinetics of $\bf{M g}\mathrm{-H}$ reaction can be improved using various techniques such as decreasing the particle size of reactant $\mathrm{(Mg/MgH_{2})}$ ) by employing a ball mill, adding catalysts or incorporating both techniques simultaneously (Sakintuna et¬†al. 2007; Abe et¬†al. 2019).
Generally, the dehydrogenation kinetics of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ was improved using transition metal (TM)-based catalysts such as Ni (Zaluska et¬†al. 1999; Shahi et¬†al. 2013), Pd (Konda and Chen 2016), Ti, Fe (Shahi et¬†al. 2013; Prachi R. et¬†al. 2016), etc. Among these transition metals, Ni was found to be a better catalyst. However, such an improvement comes at the cost of gravimetric capacity due to the heavy weight of the transition metals (Xie et¬†al. 2009). According to a report in literature, the gravimetric capacity decreased from
$6.5\%$ to $1.5\%$ by increasing the Ni content from $10\mathrm{\wt\\%}$ to 80 wt $\%$ (Xie et¬†al. 2009). To overcome this limitation, lightweight C-based materials such as graphite (Jord√°-Beneyto et¬†al. 2007), CNT (Rather et¬†al. 2016), carbon nanofiber (Shahi et¬†al. 2012) and activated carbon (Jia et¬†al. 2012) were also used as catalysts for dehydrogenation from $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ . The decomposition temperature of nano $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ is reduced to $\sim334~\mathrm{^\circC}$ when 2 wt $\%$ of PCNF is admixed with $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ (Shahi et¬†al. 2012). Lototskyy et¬†al. reported that the decomposition of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ occurs at $331^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ when $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ ball milled with 2 wt $\%$ of thermally exfoliated graphite (Lototskyy et¬†al. 2013). In a work, addition of 5 wt $\%$ of CNT resulted in $5.5\mathrm{wt}\%$ of hydrogenation at $4.6\:\mathrm{MPa}$ and $400^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ in $\mathbf{Mg}$ (Rather et¬†al. 2016). Reduced graphene oxide (rGO) is also a suitable catalyst, as it has a lighter weight and it prevents the agglomeration of active phase due its higher surface area (Pantha et¬†al. 2015; Cho et¬†al. 2016). In the recent past, 10 wt $\%$ rGO is added to $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ which attenuated the delay in the dehydrogenation (incubation) by $60~\mathrm{{min}}$ at $320^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and improved the kinetics (Shriniwasan et¬†al. 2018). In our recent work, simultaneous catalytic effect of rGO and boron was studied. Use of optimum boron to carbon $(B/C)$ weight ratio reduced the incubation period during hydrogenation to $2.25\mathrm{h}$ (Satya Aditya et¬†al. 2021). Graphene (rGO) was chosen over other carbon based catalysts for its superior physical and chemical properties. Besides, rGO actively participates in the hydrogen sorption reaction by exhibiting C-H interactions (Tozzini and Pellegrini 2013).
Many studies were carried out to understand the individual catalytic effect of TM and C-based catalysts. Several efforts were also carried out by various research groups in combining heavy weight transition metals and light weight C-based catalysts for enhancing the kinetics of hydrogen sorption in $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}$ . In a study, the decomposition kinetics of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ at $300^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ with C-supported Ni catalyst was studied (Lillo-R√≥denas et¬†al. 2008). In another study, the temperature of dehydrogenation of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ was observed at $300^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , when material was heated at rate of $5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ per min in the presence of Ni and 5 wt $\%$ of CNT (Wu et¬†al. 2010). Recently, it was demonstrated that the hydrogen release from $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ could occur at $225^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ when graphene sheet supported Ni (Ni6GS4) was used as catalyst (Zhang et¬†al. 2017). However, the higher concentration $(60.8\mathrm{\wt\\%})$ ) of Ni led to the gravimetric penalty. Huang and his group used Ni nano particles wrapped with carbon and studied their catalytic effect on the hydrogen release from $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ . They reported that the hydrogen was released at $275.7\ \mathrm{^circC}$ in the presence of Ni and C (Huang et¬†al. 2017). Most of these studies demonstrate promising hydrogen sorption in $\mathrm{{MgH}}_{2}$ upon using combined transition metal & C-based catalysts. However, the reasons of such combined catalytic effects are yet to be explored at the structural and atomic level.
In this study, the combined effect of lower fraction $(\sim3$ atomic wt $\%$ with respect to $\mathbf{Mg}^{\prime}$ ) of heavier catalyst (Ni) and higher fraction (10 wt $\%$ ) of lighter catalyst (electrochemically exfoliated reduced graphene oxide $\left(\mathrm{rGO}\right))$ on the (de) hydrogenation behavior of $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}$ is studied. These catalysts are added to $\mathbf{Mg}$ and ball milled and eventually hydrogenated. Besides, the mechanism of dehydrogenation was also analyzed experimentally. During non-isothermal dehydrogenation, the onset temperature of dehydrogenation from $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ is occurred at $\sim275\ \mathrm{^\circC}$ when heated at a rate of $5\ \mathrm{{^\circ}C}$ per min. This is significantly lower than the onset temperature of uncatalyzed $\mathrm{{MgH}}_{2}$ that occurs at ${\sim}400^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ under similar conditions (Gangrade et¬†al. 2017). Ni and rGO together alleviate the incubation period and increase the gravimetric capacity of dehydrogenation compared to their individual presence. A high-energy ball milling of $\mathbf{Mg}$ , Ni, and rGO establishes $\mathrm{{Mg-C}}$ and $_{\mathrm{Ni-O-C}}$ interactions. After subjecting to isothermal hydrogenation at $\sim15$ bar and $\sim320\mathrm{~}^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , $\mathrm{\DeltaNi-C}$ and $\mathrm{C-H}$ interactions form. In addition to these interactions, formation of an intermediate $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ phase compresses the $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell causing structural catalysis. The presence of multiple interactions and lattice contraction leads to the formation of various locally catalyzed phases. These phases help in dehydrogenation from $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ at a much lower temperature of $\sim275^{\circ}\mathrm{{C}}$ compared to uncatalyzed $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ .

Experimental

Synthesis of¬†Mg/Ni/rGO‚ÄĎbased nanocomposites
The rGO was synthesized using electrochemical technique, which was used in one of our earlier works (Muthu and Tatiparti 2020). The rGO was exfoliated from graphite rod using it as a working electrode. $\mathrm{\sfAg/AgCl}$ and Pt mesh were used as reference and counter electrodes, respectively. The electrochemical exfoliation was performed in an aqueous solution of ammonium sulfate at $3\mathrm{v}$ potential for $3\mathrm{h}$ duration using CHI660E workstation.
In $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ nanocomposite, rGO and Ni $(25~{\upmu\mathrm{m}}$ , $99\%$ pure) quantities were 10 wt $\%$ and 3 atomic wt $\%$ , respectively, and the remaining fraction was $\mathbf{Mg}$ $44\upmu\mathrm{m},>99\%$ pure). In addition to this, two nanocomposites $(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}\mathbf{-}\mathbf{r}\mathbf{G}\mathbf{O}$ and $\mathrm{\mathbf{Mg-Ni}},$ ) were synthesized. These compositions were synthesized using Fritsch Pulverisette 7, Premium Line planetary ball mill. The ball milling was performed for $20\mathrm{h}$ using tungsten carbide balls (Shriniwasan et¬†al. 2018). The handling of materials was carried out in MBraun UNIlab Plus glove box under argon atmosphere.

Hydrogen storage in¬†nanocomposites
The (de)hydrogenation experiments were performed using Sievert type apparatus. Hydrogenation was carried out at $\sim15$ bar hydrogen pressure and $\sim320\mathrm{~}^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . The reaction was aborted after saturation when no further hydrogen was absorbed. To understand the onset temperature and capacity of hydrogen release from the nanocomposites, non-isothermal dehydrogenation was performed. The nanocomposites were subjected to a thermal scan from ambient to high temperature $(450^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ at a rate of $5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ per min in an evacuated condition. Hydrogen pressure starts building up when the material reaches the onset temperature. These pressure values were recorded and plotted against temperature.

Material characterization
To understand the presence of various phases after the ball milling and hydrogenation, X-ray diffraction (XRD) was performed. Cu $\mathrm{K}\alpha$ radiation of wavelength $(\lambda)=1.5406\mathrm{~\overset{\circ}{A}~}$ at $30~\mathrm{mA}$ , $40~\mathrm{kV}$ was used in PANalytical EMPYREAN goniometer to record the XRD spectra. Rietveld refinements were carried out to estimate the phase fractions and unit cell parameters of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ in the hydrogenated nanocomposites. Fullprof suite software (Rodriguez-Carvajal 1990) was employed for Rietveld refinement.
Microscopic characterization was performed to understand the distribution of various materials in the nanocomposites. JEOL JSM 7600F Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) was used to capture images in secondary electron imaging (SEI) mode. To distinguish between the heavier and lighter elements present in the nanocomposites, back scattered imaging was done in low angle back scattered electron imaging (LABE) mode.
X-ray photo-electron spectroscopy (XPS) with an Al KőĪ source was employed to obtain high resolution spectra in Axis Supra photo-electron spectrometer (Kratos Analytical). The XPS spectra was obtained from both ball milled and hydrogenated samples. The presence of atomic interactions and other functional groups were analyzed from deconvoluting the spectra using Gaussian function.
Differential scanning calorimetry curves were obtained from simultaneous thermal analyzer, PerkinElmer (STA 8000). The DSC curves for all the nanocomposites are obtained at a heating rate of $5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ per¬†min.

Results and¬†discussion
The hydrogenation curves obtained at $\sim15$ bar and 320 $\mathrm{{^\circC}}$ from all three ball milled nanocomposites are shown in Fig.¬†1a. An initial delay is observed during isothermal hydrogenation in all the nanocomposites and is termed here as ‚Äėincubation period‚Äô. The nanocomposites in the presence of rGO result in significantly lower incubation period of $2\mathrm{h}$ . whereas, $\mathbf{Mg-Ni}$ has the highest incubation time $(5\:\mathrm{h})$ . The dehydrogenation curves for all the nanocomposites plotted against temperature $(T)$ are shown in Fig.¬†1b. Both the gravimetric capacities and onset temperatures for dehydrogenation from the nanocomposites can be observed from Fig.¬†1b. The dehydrogenation capacity of the $\mathbf{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ nanocomposite is the highest at $\sim5\mathrm{\wt\\%}$ followed by that of Mg-rGO and Mg-Ni. The gravimetric hydrogen release in all the nanocomposites are observed to be lower than that after their hydrogenation. This could be due to the fact that the sample reactor was not evacuated during dehydrogenation experiments. Hence, the hydrogen accumulating during dehydrogenation at earlier (i.e. lower) temperatures could prevent further hydrogen release from the nanocomposites.
The onset temperatures for dehydrogenation from Ni containing nanocomposites (i.e. Mg-Ni, $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}$ -Ni-rGO) are lower. In $\mathbf{Mg-Ni}$ , the onset temperature is observed at $\sim330\mathrm{~}^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , which is lower than the onset temperature of $\scriptstyle\mathbf{Mg-rGO}$ $(\sim350^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ . When Ni and rGO together added, the onset temperature is further decreased to ${\sim}275^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . Thus, the addition of 3 atomic wt $\%$ of $\mathrm{Ni}$ to $\scriptstyle\mathbf{Mg-rGO}$ has reduced the onset temperature of dehydrogenation by $\sim75^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . DSC curves for the dehydrogenation are shown in Fig. S1. The peak in the endothermic direction (negative Y-axis) is due to the dehydrogenation of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ . An observation of these DSC curves also shows the presence of exothermic peaks (positive Y-axis) in all the nanocomposites. This could be due to the oxidation of $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}$ upon its formation following the dehydrogenation of $\mathrm{MgH}_{2}$ . The exothermic peak in the $\mathbf{Mg}$ -Ni-rGO nanocomposite (mostly) resides within the net negative heat flow values. This suggests that the $\mathbf{Mg}$ forming upon dehydrogenation of this nanocomposite is oxidizing to the least extent. On the contrary, the $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}$ forming upon dehydrogenation of $\mathbf{Mg}$ -rGO and $\mathbf{Mg-Ni}$ are oxidizing to larger extents. This could be due to the fact that $\mathbf{Mg}$ in Mg-Ni-rGO experiences more (and possibly stronger) interactions with C (from rGO) and Ni, in opposition to that in Mg-Ni and Mg-rGO. Since the peaks of oxidation (exothermic peak) and dehydrogenation (endothermic peak) are mutually very close in any given nanocomposite, it is difficult to identify the onset temperatures for dehydrogenation from these nanocomposites and compare the same with those from the respective TPDs. However, the onset temperatures of dehydrogenation for Mg-Ni-rGO from DSC is ${\sim}275^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and is close to that from TPD (Fig.¬†1b). From Fig.¬†1a, b it is evident that rGO acts as superior catalyst during hydrogenation in $\mathbf{Mg}$ ; whereas $\mathrm{\DeltaNi}$ is a suitable catalyst for dehydrogenation of $\mathrm{MgH}_{2}$ . When both Ni and $\operatorname{rGO}$ are added to $\mathbf{Mg}$ (Mg-NirGO), superior hydrogenation and dehydrogenation kinetics are observed suggesting a combined catalytic effect.

To understand the reason for combined catalytic effect, the phases present in the synthesized nanocomposites are investigated. XRD was carried out in all nanocomposites after ball milling (BM) and hydrogenation (Hyd) and the patterns are shown in Fig.¬†2. Due to the marginal crystallinity of rGO, it is not ascertained in XRD patterns (Shriniwasan et¬†al. 2018). In the sample containing $\mathrm{Ni}$ , ball milling results in the formation of $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{Ni}$ . However, $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}$ is the major phase in all the nanocomposites after ball milling (Fig.¬†2a). Upon hydrogenation (Hyd), most of the $\mathbf{Mg}$ is converted to $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ (Fig.¬†2b). However, the $\mathbf{M}\mathrm{g}_{2}\mathbf{Ni}$ phase in the $\mathrm{\DeltaNi}$ containing nanocomposites was not fully hydrogenated. It $(\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{Ni})$ absorbed a small amount of hydrogen and formed a non-stochiometric compound $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ (Hayashi et¬†al. 1984). $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ is an intermediate solid solution of $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{Ni}$ phase (Posx et¬†al. 1984; Humphries et¬†al. 2018). Therefore, as the crystal structure of $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ is same as $\mathbf{Mg}_{2}\mathbf{Ni}$ , their XRD peaks mutually overlap. Further confirmation of $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ is done by performing Rietveld refinement (Fig. S2). While fitting, the space group for the $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ was considered as $P6_{2}22$ (180) which is same as $\mathbf{Mg}_{2}\mathbf{Ni}$ with the additional atomic position of hydrogen as 6f and $6\textup{h}$ (Wyckoff). The background, FWHM and shape parameters were also refined along with the atomic positions. Other crystallographic phases $(\mathrm{MgH}_{2}$ , $\mathbf{Mg}$ and $\mathbf{MgO}_{\mathit{i}}^{\mathrm{{\Phi}}}$ ) were fitted subsequently using their standard crystallographic information from ICSD database (https://‚Äčicsd.‚Äčfiz-‚Äčkarls‚Äčruhe.‚Äčde/‚ÄčAcces sed 25 Dec 2020). The unit cell parameters of body centered tetragonal (BCT) $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ obtained from refinement are listed in Table¬†1. It is evident from Table¬†1 that the volume of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell is decreased (compression) in the nanocomposites containing Ni. This compression is likely because of the presence of an intermediate phase $\begin{array}{r}{{\bf M g}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}}\end{array}$ . This shows the structural catalysis of $\mathrm{\Delta\mathrm{MgH}}_{2}$ i.e., compression of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell improves the dehydrogenation kinetics as shown in Table¬†1 and Fig.¬†1b. The unit cell dimensions for $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ are the lowest in $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ and moreover, its onset temperature is seen to be the lowest.

As $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ is the major phase in all the hydrogenated nanocomposites (Fig.¬†2b), the hydrogen released during dehydrogenation (Fig.¬†1b) is predominantly from $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ . This implies that there could be a possibility of the formation of locally catalyzed regions in the presence of Ni causing a decrease in the dehydrogenation onset temperature of $\mathrm{{\bfMg-}}$ $_{\mathrm{Ni-rGO}}$ . The SEM images of the $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ nanocomposites after ball milling and hydrogenation are shown in the Fig.¬†3a and b, respectively. SEM was performed in the backscattered mode (LABE) to identify the presence of Ni/ $\mathbf{Mg}_{2}\mathbf{Ni}/\mathbf{Mg}_{2}\mathbf{Ni}\mathbf{H}_{0.3}$ phases at different regions. The brighter region (within yellow circles) at various locations is mostly due to the presence of $\mathbf{Mg}_{2}\mathbf{Ni}$ (Fig.¬† 3a) and $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ (Fig.¬†3b). These regions could be the possible sites where local catalytic effect is seen. $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}$ and C could not be identified separately as there is no significant contrast difference between them due to the low difference in their atomic numbers. These SEM images further support that the improvement in the dehydrogenation kinetics in $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ could be due to the co-catalytic effect of Ni and carbon. These locally catalyzed phases can form as a result of chemical interactions among the elements $\mathbf{Mg}$ , Ni and C in rGO.

To estimate the chemical interactions, the high resolution XPS spectra of nanocomposites containing rGO were recorded. The O1s spectra were recorded after ball milling (BM) and C1s spectra were recorded after hydrogenation and shown in Fig.¬†4a and b, respectively. The oxygen (O1s) spectra (Fig.¬†4a) of BM $\scriptstyle\mathbf{Mg-rGO}$ and $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ were deconvoluted by considering peaks at $\sim534.3\ \mathrm{eV}$ (C‚ÄďOH) (Yang et¬†al. 2009; Garg et¬†al. 2018), $\sim533.5\ \mathrm{eV}$ $\mathbf{\Gamma}(\mathbf{MgO})$ (Sugiyama et¬†al. 1996), ${\sim}532\ \mathrm{eV}$ $\scriptstyle\mathbf{C}=\mathbf{O}_{,}$ (Wu et¬†al. 2014; Lee et¬†al. 2019) respectively. Here, an additional peak is observed at ${\sim}531.01\ \mathrm{eV}$ for $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ nanocomposite. This peak resembles that of $_{\mathrm{Ni-O-C}}$ interaction (Wu et¬†al. 2014). The carbon (C1s) spectra of hydrogenated nanocomposites were deconvoluted at $\sim283\ \mathrm{eV}$ $(\mathrm{Mg-C)}$ (Cho et¬†al. 2016), $284.6\mathrm{eV}$ $(\mathrm{C}{\mathrm{-}}\mathrm{C}\ \mathrm{sp}^{2})$ (Carolan et¬†al. 2017; Kolay et¬†al. 2021), ${\sim}285.1\ \mathrm{eV}$ $(\mathrm{C-H})$ (Nikitin et¬†al. 2008), ${\sim}286.5\ \mathrm{eV}$ $({\mathrm{C}}{\mathrm{-OH}})$ (Parmar et¬† al. 2020), $\sim288\ \mathrm{eV}$ $\mathrm{\tilde{C}=O\tilde{\it_{\cdot}}}$ (Ngom et¬†al. 2020), $\sim290~\mathrm{eV}$ $(\pi\to\pi^{*}),$ (Shriniwasan et¬†al. 2018). The cause for the formation of $\mathrm{{\bfMg{-}}}\mathrm{{C}}$ and $\mathrm{C-H}$ interactions was already discussed in our previous work (Shriniwasan et¬†al. 2018). These interactions show the catalytic ability of rGO during (de)hydrogenation behavior in $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{g}$ . Also, the presence of high intense $\mathrm{C-C}\ \mathrm{sp}^{2}$ peak depicts that the structure of rGO is retained after ball milling and hydrogenation. Similar interactions are also observed in $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ system. However, an additional $\mathrm{Ni-C}$ interaction is observed at 283.5‚Äď284.5¬†eV (Zhao et¬†al. 2011; Adamska et¬†al. 2012;
Furlan et¬†al. 2014). The $_{\mathrm{Ni-O-C}}$ and $\mathrm{\DeltaNi-C}$ interactions after ball milling and hydrogenation respectively could be responsible for the low onset temperature during dehydrogenation from $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ nanocomposite (Fig.¬†1b). $\mathbf{Mg}_{2}\mathbf{Ni}/$ $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ are the sources of $\mathrm{Ni}$ , which is participating in these $_{\mathrm{Ni-O-C}}$ and $\mathrm{\DeltaNi-C}$ interactions. This implies that there are some regions in the $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ nanocomposite, (Fig.¬†3) that involve multiple atomic interactions $(\mathrm{Mg-C}$ , Ni‚ÄíC, C‚ÄíH). This provides a combined catalytic effect of multiple catalysts.
The present analysis demonstrates the behavior and mechanism of dehydrogenation when Ni and rGO are simultaneously used as catalysts. The overall dehydrogenation from $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ nanocomposite is analyzed by comparing it with other two nanocomposites where only one catalyst is present. In $\mathbf{Mg-rGO}$ , the catalytic effect of $\mathrm{rGO}$ is through $\mathrm{{\bf{M}}g{-}}\mathrm{{C}}$ and $\mathrm{C-H}$ interactions. In the $\mathbf{Mg-Ni}$ system the reduction in the onset temperature of dehydrogenation is due to structural catalysis as a result of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell compression. Based on these catalytic effects, the H capacity (wt $\%$ )- $T$ curve in Fig.¬†1b for $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ nanocomposite is classified into various regions as shown in Fig.¬†5. Firstly, the gray region (Fig.¬†5) above $350^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ where the hydrogen is released predominantly is due to $\mathrm{{\bfMg{-}}}\mathrm{{C}}$ interaction. This is named as graphene catalyzed $\mathrm{{MgH}}_{2}$ . Hydrogen released at the temperature range of $330{-}350~\mathrm{^circC}$ (red region) in Fig.¬†5, can be ascribed to structural catalysis (unit cell compression) of $\mathrm{{MgH}}_{2}$ . This is seen in $\mathbf{Mg-Ni}$ nanocomposite, where $\mathrm{\mathrm{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell compression occurred (Table¬†1). This region is named as structurally catalyzed $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ . As seen from Fig.¬†5 and Fig.¬†1b, the decrease in the dehydrogenation temperature in $\mathrm{Mg-Ni-rGO}$ system is $275^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . This decrease in the temperature is due to the combined catalytic effect of unit cell compression and $\mathrm{Ni-C}$ interaction. Hence, the temperature region from $275^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ to $330^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (blue region) in Fig.¬†5 represents co-catalyzed $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ region that is responsible for significant improvement in the dehydrogenation kinetics.

The isothermal hydrogenation and dehydrogenation cycles are performed at $320^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ on all the nanocomposites. This hydrogenation and dehydrogenation performance is superior in comparison to other $\mathbf{Mg}$ based composites (Liu et¬†al. 2014; Shao et¬†al. 2021). In Mg-Ni-rGO nanocomposite rGO and Ni catalyze $\mathbf{Mg}$ during hydrogenation and dehydrogenation, respectively (Fig.¬†1 and 6). From Table¬†1, Ni catalyzes the dehydrogenation process by structural catalysis through a shrinkage of the $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell after hydrogenation. To see the sustenance of this structural catalysis by Ni in Mg-Ni-rGO nanocomposite, the variation of $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell parameters upon cycles are estimated by employing Rietveld refinement (see Fig. S2). The unit cell parameters of body centered tetragonal $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ ( $\dot{\mathbf{\Omega}}_{a}$ and $\boldsymbol{c}$ ) and volume $(V)$ are plotted versus cycle number in Fig.¬†7. Conclusions
To understand the individual and simultaneous catalytic effect of Ni and rGO on the hydrogen absorption and release behavior of magnesium, Mg‚ÄíNi‚ÄírGO-based nanocomposites were synthesized and studied. Reduced graphene oxide is synthesized using electrochemical technique. During the hydrogenation process of these nanocomposites, rGO acts as superior catalyst compared with Ni. Whereas, Ni containing nanocomposites possess better dehydrogenation kinetics. Interestingly, the nanocomposite containing both rGO and Ni shows better gravimetric capacity during hydrogenation and lower onset temperature of dehydrogenation.
The onset temperature of dehydrogenation in $\scriptstyle\mathbf{Mg-rGO}$ nanocomposite is observed at $\sim350^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Fig.¬†1b) that occurs as a result of $\mathrm{{\bf{M}}g{-}}\mathrm{{C}}$ interactions as seen from C1s XPS spectra (Fig.¬†4a). In $\mathbf{Mg-Ni}$ nanocomposite where 3 atomic wt $\%$ of Ni is added to $\mathbf{Mg}$ , $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{Ni}$ phase formed that further produced an intermediate hydride phase of $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ upon hydrogenation. The presence of $\mathrm{Mg}_{2}\mathrm{NiH}_{0.3}$ phase resulted in $\mathrm{\mathbf{MgH}}_{2}$ unit cell contraction that led to a decrease in the dehydrogenation onset temperature to $\sim330\mathrm{~}^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ in $\mathbf{Mg-Ni}$ .
