Novel IGFALS mutations with predicted pathogenetic effects by the analysis of AlphaFold structure

Abstract
Purpose According to the American College of Medical Genetics (ACMG) classification, variants of uncertain significance (VUS) are gene variations whose impact on the disease risk is not yet known. VUS, therefore, represent an unmet need for genetic counselling. Aim of the study is the use the AlphaFold artificial intelligence algorithm to predict the impact of novel mutations of the IGFALS gene, detected in a subject with short stature and initially classified as VUS according to the ACMG classification.
Methods A short-stature girl and her parents have been investigated. IGFALS mutations have been detected through clinical exome and confirmed by Sanger sequencing. The potential presence of co-occurring gene alterations was investigated in the proband by whole exome and CGH array. Structure of the ALS protein (encoded by the IGFALS gene) was evaluated through the AlphaFold artificial intelligence algorithm.
Results Two IGFALS variants were found in the proband: $\mathrm{c.1349T>C}$ $(\mathrm{p.Leu450Pro})$ and c.1363_1365delCTC (p.Leu455del), both classified as VUS, according to ACMG. Parents’ analysis highlighted the in trans position of the two variants. AlphaFold showed that the mutated positions were found the concave side a horseshoe structure of the ALS protein, likely interfering with protein-protein interactions. According to a loss of function (LoF) effect of the two variants, reduced levels of the IGF1 and IGFBP-3 proteins, as well as a growth hormone (GH) excess were detected in the proband’s serum.
Conclusions By using the AlphaFold structure we were able to predict two IGFALS gene mutations initially classified as VUS, as potentially pathogenetic. Our proof-of-concept showed a potential application of AlphaFold as tool to a better inform VUS interpretation of genetic tests.

Introduction
IGFALS is a 2 exons-containing gene, located on chromosome 16p13.3 and encoding the Insulin-like growth factorbinding protein acid-labile subunit (ALS) having a mass of 84 to $86~\mathrm{kD}$ . ALS is produced in the liver and is present almost exclusively in serum, bound in a ternary complex with IGF1 or IGF2 and IGF binding protein (IGFBP) 3 or 5 [1, 2]. Most of the ALS protein contains 21 leucine repeats motifs forming a horseshoe-shape structure that participate in protein-protein interaction primarily through the negatively charged concave surface [3]. The IGF-IGFBP-ALS ternary complex increases half-life of IGF:IGFBP binary complex [4]. IGFALS loss of function (LoF) mutations in homozygous or compound heterozygous state cause height deficiency in a range of $^{-2}$ to $^{-4}$ standard deviations (SDS)
during late prenatal age and childhood, some of these subjects reaching a normal range height in adult state [5, 6]. In ALS-deficient subjects, IGF1 and IGFBP3 levels are reduced because of rapid clearance, though normal production rate [7]. In heterozygous state, pathogenetic IGFALS variants are associated with modest hypostaturalism [8], in many cases heterozygous carriers have a normal stature range, but lower than relatives without IGFALS variants [9]. A more recent survey of more than 60 patients also suggested that one functional IGFALS allele is insufficient in stabilising the multiprotein complex [10]. The role of IGFALS LoF in causing growth impairment has been confirmed by knockout mice: at 10 weeks age, a $13\mathrm{-}20\%$ of growth reduction has been detected as well as decreased IGF1 and IGFBP3 levels [11].
Global analytic procedures of genome such as Clinical Exome Sequencing, Whole Exome Sequencing and Comparative Genomic Hybridisation (CGH)/single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) array have enormously increased the possibility to find causative variants. However, these approaches are limited in sensitivity and specificity due to a lack of data covering large numbers of variant in coding genes, resulting in Variant of Uncertain Significance (VUS) (i.e. a genetic variation whose impact on the disease risk is not yet known according to the American College of Medical Genetics (ACMG) classification) [12], which represents an unmet clinical need for genetic counselling. The AlphaFold artificial intelligence algorithm is able to efficiently predict the 3D structure of proteins of the human proteome according to its amino acid sequence [13]. The use of AlphaFold could help in VUS reclassification and, therefore, improve efficacy of genetic counselling after genetic testing.

Patient and methods

Patient
A 7.2-year-old girl was referred to the paediatric endocrinology clinic due to premature pubarche. At presentation, her height was $111.3\mathrm{cm}$ ( $_{-2.14}$ SDS, with a target height of $162\mathrm{cm},$ and her weight was $19.3\mathrm{kg}$ $(-1.3\ \mathrm{SDS})$ ; with a BMI of 15.6 $(\mathrm{kg}/\mathrm{m}2)$ . There were no dysmorphic features and no body disproportions. The Tanner stage was B1 PH2, without axillarc. The bone age was 8 years and 10 months according to Greulich-Pyle (Table 1).
She was born from unrelated healthy parents with normal stature, albeit a negative SDS (father, $-1.1$ ; mother, $-0.67)$ . Intra uterine growth restriction (IUGR), was diagnosed at 34 weeks of gestation, during prenatal screening. She was born small for gestational age (SGA) at 36 weeks of gestation with a 1.758 gr weight $(<3^{\circ})$ , a $44\mathrm{cm}$ length $(5-10^{\circ})$ and a head circumference of 31 cm $(5-10^{\circ})$ . Postnatal follow-up showed slow but regular growth, without catch-up growth. Neuromotor development was normal.
Preliminary studies for the precocious pubarche showed normal basal serum concentrations of androgens, normal ACTH test and a prepubertal GnRH test. A diagnosis of isolated precocious pubarche was formulated.
Because of the short stature, a stimulation test for GH secretion was performed and showed an exaggerated GH response. Low levels of IGF1 $(47\mathrm{ng/ml}$ ; $-2.2\thinspace\mathrm{SD})$ and IGFBP3 $\mathrm{341~ng/ml}$ ; $-2.6\mathrm{SD}$ ) were detected (Table 1).

DNA sequencing
The Proband’s and parents’ blood samples were collected after written informed consent. Genomic DNA was isolated from peripheral blood using the QIAamp DNA Blood Midi Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Whole Exome Sequencing was performed using the Whole Exome Solutions kit by Sophia Genetics $(>19,000$ RefSeq genes), following the manufacturer’s recommendations. The resulting libraries were subjected to paired-end sequencing on an Illumina NextSeq 550. Raw data were converted to Fastq files for computational analysis which included read alignment to the human genome reference sequence (GRCh37/hg19), duplicate marking, base quality score recalibration, indel alignment and variant calling using the SOPHIA DDM Platform (Sophia Genetics, SA). Clinical exome sequencing data quality acceptance parameters included a mean depth of coverage $50\times$ , with ${>}98\%$ regions at $25\times$ . Sanger sequencing has confirmed the presence of the IGFALS mutations. Briefly, sequencing reactions were analysed by a 3500 DX DNA Sequencer (Life Technologies) analyser using the BigDye Terminator kit v3.1 (Applied Biosystems,
Foster City, CA, USA); the software Sequencing Analysis v5.4 (Life Technologies) was used for mutation detection.

CGH array
CGH array analysis was performed using the Agilent Human Genome CGH oligonucleotide array $180\mathrm{K}$ , following the manufacturer’s instructions (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA). The image was analysed with Agilent Feature Extraction and Genomic Workbench 6.5.018 Lite Edition Software.

Protein modelling
The sequence of Insulin-like growth factor-binding protein complex acid-labile subunit (Uniprot code: ALS_HUMAN) was first aligned with the Protein Data Bank sequences, finding many homologs with identity percentages up to $38\%$ . In the absence of more similar proteins, we decided to use the model deposited in the AlphaFold human proteins database [13]. The model refers to the isolated protein whereas homologs in the PDB show interactions with other proteins. Due to the limited sequence identity, we did not attempt modelling of the interactions.

Results and discussion
The proband was subjected to whole exome sequencing to find gene variation with a short stature pathogenetic impact. Two variants, both in a heterozygous status, have been found, both in exon 2 of the IGFALS gene (NM_001146006.1): $\mathrm{c.1349T>C}$ (p.Leu450Pro) and c.1363_1365delCTC (p.Leu455del). The two mutations were confirmed by Sanger sequencing; segregation analysis demonstrated the paternal and maternal origin of the $\mathrm{c.1349T>C}$ and c.1363_1365delCTC variants, respectively (Fig. 1, panel A). Neither of the mutations were present in the gnomAD population database (https://gnomad.broa dinstitute.org) and both were classified as VUS according to the ACMG guidelines. To exclude the presence of other genetic alterations, the proband was also profiled through CGH array with negative results.
The potential role of the two identified IGFALS gene variants was assessed by analysing the of ALS protein structure generated by the AlphaFold artificial intelligence algorithm. The mutations occured on two leucine forming in the same Leucine repeat motif that shapes the horseshoe conformation of the protein (Fig. 1, panel B). Each mutation disrupts the leucine repeat motif, resulting in conformation distortions affecting protein interactions. The AlphaFold structure, therefore, suggested that both mutation had a LoF effect. As a matter of fact, SIFT (https://sift.bii.a-star.edu.sg)

shows that the only tolerated amino acid at position 450 is leucine and few other residues could be tolerated at position 455. The potential pathogenetic effect of the compound heterozygous state for the short stature of the proband is also supported by laboratory results, in particular by IGF1 and IGFBP3 low plasmatic levels (Table 1). Moreover, our patient was born SGA as well as with low head circumference; these features appear to be common in ALSdeficient patients [9, 14, 15].
Based on AlphaFold data, we suggest that both mutations could be considered as potentially pathogenic. Thus, the use of AlphaFold should be encouraged for the experimental validation of VUS, using cell culture approaches [16, 17].
In conclusion, by using the AlphaFold structure we were able to predict two IGFALS gene mutations as probably pathogenetic, although being previously classified as VUS according to ACMG. Our proof-of-concept showed a potential application of AlphaFold as tool to a better inform VUS interpretation of genetic tests.
