Abstract
The mechanism of slope stability prediction is formulated based on its material, geometrical and environmental situation, and slope stability prediction has been accepted as a tool for analyzing and predicting future structure stability based on geotechnical properties and failure mechanisms. However, the study of slope instability is complex and usually difficult to explain by mathematical methods. The number of slope cases limits the accuracy of slope stability prediction, and the variability of soil or rock parameters of slopes poses new challenges for prediction using conventional algorithms. To improve the accuracy of slope stability state prediction, this paper proposes an efficient slope stability state prediction method with a highly robust convolutional neural network named the multiscale, multichannel, one-dimensional convolutional neural network (MSC-1DCNN) and substantial empirical data collected worldwide. The collected dataset is amplified. Additionally, the probability of failure is calculated considering the variability of soil or rock parameters. Compared with some state-of-the-art prediction methods, the MSC-1DCNN presents high prediction accuracy. The proposed method is applied to a slope, and the results indicate that this paper provides a reliable slope stability state prediction method for homogeneous slopes worldwide.

1  Introduction
Slope stability analysis is a worldwide challenge, mainly reflected in the field of engineering construction. Slope instability causes landslides, which bring about losses to national public property and endanger the lives and safety of citizens. Every year, slope instability leads to death, structural collapse, traffic paralysis, and other phenomena (Verma et al. 2013; Meng et al. 2010). With rapid economic development, the range of human activity areas continues to expand, and many traffic facilities and buildings are built in mountainous areas. Thus, the safety of slopes is a great concern. Slope engineering is an uncertain, nonlinear, and highly complex system (Xue et  al. 2008) whose stability is affected by a variety of random, fuzzy, and complex factors. Certain analysis methods, such as the limit equilibrium method (for example, the Bishop method and Spence method) (Bishop 1955; Spencer 1967) and numerical simulation, can be used to accurately evaluate the stability state of the slope. However, these methods are limited by the availability and accuracy of slope engineering geological data and the knowledge and experience of evaluators. Moreover, some analysis methods need to establish a slope ontology, which is costly to analyze.
In recent years, many scholars have tried to use uncertainty analysis methods for slope stability analysis and have achieved a good effect. These methods mainly include logistic regression (Chen and Chen 2021), the fuzzy analytic hierarchy process (Qin and Qin 2010), rough set theory (Wang et al. 2009), Bayes (Jiang et al. 2020), and machine learning (Xue et  al. 2008; Wei et  al. 2021). These methods have been well applied in slope stability prediction, but different classification methods have different classification results. Machine learning is rapidly developing for better efficiency, rapid autonomous learning, and accurate prediction capabilities. Scholars worldwide have introduced support vector machines (SVMs) (Balogun et al. 2020; Samui 2013), artificial neural networks (ANNs) (Pham et al. 2020; Ngo et al. 2021), extreme learning machines (ELMs) (Liu et al. 2014; Peethambaran et al. 2020), and other machine learning methods to slope stability analysis and achieved a series of results. As a robust prediction method, SVMs have been utilized in slope stability analysis to predict the stability state of a slope (Zhao and Feng 2003; Samui and Kothari 2011). However, the penalty parameters and kernel functions of SVMs have a great influence on slope stability prediction, and there is a lack of reference standards for the selection of penalty parameters. To solve the problem of parameter selection, some optimization algorithms were introduced and combined with SVMs to deduce a penalty parameter and kernel function with better prediction effectiveness, which improves the prediction accuracy (Ma and Kong 2009; Xue 2017). However, the application of parameter optimization introduces new model parameters and increases the complexity of the method. ANNs are excellent classification perceptrons that only use two hidden layers to achieve good nonlinear effects (Wang 2003) and can excavate potential damage mechanisms based on existing knowledge. Thus, numerous studies on slope stability based on ANNs have been conducted and have successful applications (Cho 2009; Lin et al. 2009). Although ANNs are successful in many ways, a major drawback is that it is difficult to give a reasonable interpretation of the overall structure of the network (Park and Rilett 1999; Tang et al. 2013), and this method relies on a large amount of data. A small amount of data will limit the prediction performance of ANNs. In addition, ANNs have several inherent drawbacks, including overfitting, slow convergence, poor generalizing performance, and arriving at a local minimum. ELMs are single hidden layer feedforward neural networks. Its thresholds and weights do not need to be repeatedly adjusted, which can increase the computational speed while ensuring accuracy (Huang et al. 2006).
After a series of literature reviews, it is determined that the prediction accuracy of slope stability prediction methods (such as SVMs, ELMs, and ANNs) has several drawbacks, such as low accuracy, complex structure, and overfitting. Adopting a new method to improve prediction accuracy and reduce the complexity of the algorithm is the key technical problem. Moreover, in the selection of prediction methods, most scholars select traditional machine learning algorithms to predict stability slopes without considering the state-of-the-art techniques of convolutional neural networks (CNNs). CNNs employ deep learning to extract features from data while learning the relationships between them.
On the other hand, the variability of the physical and mechanical properties of soils or rocks has been widely recognized as one of the main sources of uncertainty (Griffiths et al. 2002; Cho 2007; Goh et al. 2019) regarding the factor of a slope calculated based on poorly known soil or rock parameters (Zhang et al. 2010; Juang et al. 2013). Moreover, the determination of the soil or rock parameters is complex and highly time-consuming. To avoid such misleading results, the slope stability evaluation method based on probability analysis was proposed to calculate the slope failure probability utilizing the probability distributions for $c$ and $\varphi$ in the literature (Vanmarcke 1997; Oka and Wu 1990; Liang et al. 1990; El-Ramly et al. 2002; Cho 2007; Griffiths et al. 2009; Cho 2010; Ji and Low 2012; Li et al. 2014; Dyson and Tolooiyan 2019).
Given the above problems, this paper carries out a study of slope stability prediction based on a CNN, introduces a one-dimensional convolutional layer (1DCNL) to carry out convolution learning of input data, and conducts in-depth extended research on its network structure (Zhang et  al. 2019). CNNs with a 1DCNL are referred to as one-dimensional convolutional neural networks (1DCNNs). A multiscale parallel module (MSM) and multichannel parallel module (MCM) are introduced to propose the multiscale, multichannel, one-dimensional convolutional neural network (MSC-1DCNN). Moreover, the rule between slope influencing factors and slope safety is utilized to complete the amplification of the dataset. Furthermore, the probability of failure is calculated by sampling from the distribution of soil or rock parameters. The superiority of the proposed method is verified by a comparative analysis of experiments, and the prediction accuracy is further improved. A slope example is presented to illustrate the proposed methodology.

2  Methods

2.1  Scheme framework
In this study, we offer an MSC-1DCNN-based homogeneous slope stability state prediction method integrated with empirical data to calculate the stability state of a slope with historical empirical data. An efficient scheme framework considering the variability of soil or rock parameters is presented in the flowchart of Fig. 1. The framework is divided into three steps as follows:
Step 1: Creating the dataset. Historical empirical data on the slope are obtained from previous literature, which will be amplified to include previous studies and divided into a training set and a validation set. Step 2: Instantiating an empirical model is a vital part of the current study. As shown in Fig. 3, an empirical model named MSC-1DCNN is developed for soil or rock slopes considering the parameters affecting the slope stability and the stability state of the slope. After building the MSC-1DCNN, it will be instantiated based on a training set to establish the relations among the parameters affecting slope stability and the stability state of the slope. In this case, the stability state of the slope is determined by the input data.

Step 3: The slope stability considering the variability of soil or rock parameters is evaluated. First, the joint probability density function (PDF) of $c$ and $\varphi$ is constructed, and random sampling is carried out. 2.2  Model structure

2.2.1  One‑dimensional convolutional neural networks
CNNs are utilized in the image recognition field, and the two-dimensional convolutional layer (2DCNL) is commonly adopted. Replacing 2DCNLs with 1DCNLs can realize the convolution of one-dimensional data and extract the potential relationship features among data. Structurally, 1DCNNs are almost identical to CNNs in that they also include a series of convolutional and pooling layers and output the results via a fully connected layer. In terms of usage, considering that few parameters are involved, the pooling layer is dropped. A basic 1DCNN is constructed by a simple structure of double convolution layers and one full connection layer of binary classification. The basic network structure of the 1DCNN is shown in Fig. 2.
In Fig. 2, 1Dconv (32, 3) is a 1DCNL with convolution kernel sizes of 3 and 32 channels, 1Dconv (64, 3) is a 1DCNL with convolution kernel sizes of 3 and 64 channels, Flatten is used to transform the extracted multidimensional features into one-dimensional vectors, and Dense (2) is a fully connected layer with 2 neurons.
Fig. 2   Structure of the 1DCNN

2.2.2  MSC‑1DCNN
1DCNNs can reach the basic requirements of prediction, but further improvement is needed to improve the prediction ability. To improve the structure of the 1DCNN, it is necessary to seek a balance between the hierarchical depth and the width. A deeper or wider network architecture commonly exhibits a stronger ability to extract data features and a higher classification accuracy (Montufar et  al. 2014; Bengio et  al. 2013; Nguyen et  al. 2018; Szegedy et  al. 2015, 2016a, b). However, the main challenge faced by deep architectures is the diminishing gradient, which leads to a high computational cost, an increase in training parameters, memory requirements, and a high demand for performance equipment (Khan et al. 2020). Therefore, it is essential to establish a suitable network structure according to the data characteristics. To improve the prediction performance of the model and reduce the computational burden, the structure of the 1DCNN is optimized, and two modules, MCM and MSM, are introduced. The overall network structure is shown in Fig. 3.
Different scale convolution layers extract different knowledge for the same feature matrices, and the learning efficiency of the 1DCNN can be improved by setting multiscale convolution layers (Cai et al. 2016; Deng et al. 2019). Note that the scale means the size of a kernel. Therefore, we propose the MSM network structure, which is a block with multiscale parallel convolutional layers. The calculation process of the MSM is presented as follows:
(1) The inputs are simultaneously convolved by convolution layers of different scale convolution kernels to extract feature matrices;   
(2) The feature matrices are summed by the add layer, and this sum will be activated by the activation function;   
(3) The dropout layer randomly sets a part of the output parameters to zero and passes the output to the next convolutional layer.
MCM is based on the idea of multichannel parallel convolution for inception. Note that the channel indicates the number of kernels. The different channel convolution layers convolve and activate the input feature matrices, and then the output is concatenated in the channel dimension to increase the channel dimension and achieve lighter training parameters compared with the commonly utilized method of amplifying the convolution dimension (Szegedy et al. 2015, 2016a, b). The dropout layer randomly sets parts of the parameters in the activated output to zero and then transforms the output to the next convolutional layer.
Fig. 3   Overall architecture of the MSC-1DCNN
As shown in Fig. 3, the MSC-1DCNN uses a convolutional layer with a kernel size of 1 to limit the number of computational parameters. By mapping the high-dimensional feature matrices to the low-dimensional feature matrices, the training parameters are reduced by using the 1-scale convolution layer (a convolutional layer with a kernel size of 1) (Lin et al. 2013). Therefore, by controlling the number of training parameters, the MSC-1DCNN not only increases the model depth but also expands the model width.

2.2.3  One‑dimensional convolution layer
The convolutional layer is the key and the core to extracting features. The convolution kernel is an important parameter of a convolutional layer that is responsible for extracting the corresponding data features. Different convolution kernels are used to extract distinct features from the input data. The more convolution kernels there are, the more abstract the extracted features (Zhu et al. 2019b). There are three types of convolution layers: 1DCNL, 2DCNL, and a three-dimensional convolution layer (3DCNL). The 1DCNL is usually used to extract linear data, the 2DCNL is usually used to extract image features, and the 3DCNL is usually employed to extract geometric features. In this paper, the 1DCNL is utilized to obtain the relationship between the slope characteristic parameters and slope stability and generates one-dimensional feature maps by carrying out the convolution operation on the local part of the input data. Each convolution kernel detects specific features at all locations on the input one-dimensional feature map and fulfils the weight sharing on the same input one-dimensional feature map. The 1DCNL has the characteristics of local connectivity and weight sharing, which effectively reduce the complexity of the network and the number of training parameters. 2.2.7  Dropout layer
Dropout means that during the training of the CNN, neural network units are temporarily discarded from the network according to a certain probability. Since the discarding process is random, each batch iteration trains different CNN parameters. Dropout is proposed in response to the time-consuming and easy overfitting of the CNN. The method proposed in this paper introduces dropout, whose corresponding parameter is set to 0.5 (Hinton et al. 2012), which reduces the complex coadaptations of neurons since a neuron cannot depend on the presence of other specific neurons. Therefore, it is necessary to learn more robust properties that are useful in conjunction with many different random subsets of the other neurons.

3  Advancement analysis of the empirical model

3.1  Data amplification and data analysis
In this study, the cohesion $(c)$ , internal friction angle $(\varphi)$ , unit weight (γ), pore pressure ratio $(R_{\mathrm{u}})$ , and geometry feature parameters, such as slope angle $(\beta)$ and slope height $(H)$ , are used as input data. Overall, the empirical data of 108 slope cases were obtained from the literature (Lu and Rosenbaum 2003; Feng et al. 2018; Sah et al. 1994; Zhou and Chen 2009; Li and Wang 2010; Yan and Li 2011), and parts of the slope cases in the collected data are shown in Table 1. Because the number of slope cases is not sufficient to support slope stability prediction, a data amplification method was proposed to improve the accuracy of slope stability prediction.
Previous studies showed that the larger the material parameters $c$ and $\varphi$ are, the safer the slope will be, and the material parameters $\overset{\cdot}{c}$ or $\varphi$ ) are positively correlated with the safety factor (Li et al. 2012; Gökceoglu and Aksoy 1996). Hence, the collected data were amplified based on the inclusion of previous studies. The data amplification rule is defined as follows: (1) The collected original data were divided into two categories: failure and safety; (2) $c$ and $\varphi$ in the slope failure data were reduced, and the generated new data were categorized as the slope failure category; (3) data on slope safety increased the values of $c$ and $\varphi$ , and the generated new data were categorized as the slope safety category; and (4) on the scale of data augmentation, $c$ and $\varphi$ were simultaneously and continuously increased or decreased by $10\%$ , $20\%$ , $30\%$ , $40\%$ , and $50\%$ on the basis of the initial value. The data were amplified by 5 times the original. The amplified data consist of 540 groups, including 185 groups of slope failure data and 355 groups of slope safety data. The amplified data are shown in Table 2 and box graph Fig. 4. The distribution characteristics of the influencing factors are shown in Fig. 5.
According to Fig.  5, the distribution characteristics of influencing factors can be obtained:
(1) Figure 5a shows that slope instability is more likely to occur when γ is smaller than 22 $\operatorname{kN}/\mathrm{m}^{3}$ and that slope failure is less likely when γ is greater than $22\mathrm{\kN/m}^{3}$ .   
(2) Figure 5b shows that slope failure mostly occurs in slopes with a low value of $c$ , mainly concentrated in slopes with $c$ values less than $50\mathrm{{kPa}}$ .   
(3) According to Fig. 5c, slope failure mostly occurs in slopes with lower internal friction angles, mainly concentrated in areas below $30^{\circ}$ .   
(4) As shown in Fig. 5d, slope failure mostly occurs in the medium- and small-scale slopes, while the failure occurs less at slope heights over $100\mathrm{m}$ .

3.3  Effectiveness of the MSC for slope stability prediction
In this section, the impact of the MSC on the 1DCNN is discussed. The MSC is composed of the MSM and MCM. The performance was compared for four network structures, including the MSC-1DCNN, MS-1DCNN (only the MSM is included), MC-1DCNN (including the MCM), and 1DCNN (not including the MSM and MCM). The evaluation results of the four networks are shown in Table 4.
Compared with the 1DCNN, the MS-1DCNN had more than $1.1\%$ , $0\%$ , $1.59\%$ , and $0.8\%$ improvements in the $A,P,R$ , and $F_{1}$ of the slope stability prediction, respectively, which shows that the multiscale parallel convolution is quite important for the evaluation index $R$ of slope stability prediction and can effectively assist the network in classification. In addition, compared with the 1DCNN, the MC-1DCNN improved the $A,P,R$ , and $F_{1}$ of the slope stability prediction by $1.1\%$ , $1.64\%$ , $0.05\%$ , and $0.82\%$ , respectively, which also proves that the multicore parallel convolution can promote the performance of the classification, especially the evaluation index $P$ . Compared with the MC-1DCNN, MS-1DCNN, and 1DCNN, the MSC-1DCNN had a considerable improvement in $A,R,P$ , and $F_{1}$ . This finding means that multiscale parallel convolution and multicore parallel convolution can complement each other and improve the classification performance of the network. The experimental results prove that the MSC effectively improves the slope stability prediction performance of the network.

4  Case analysis with the MSC‑1DCNN

4.1  Overview
In this study, a slope located in Shao Jiazhuang village of Guizhou Province in China is used to analyze the stability of the slope with the proposed method, as shown in Fig. 9a. The case slope has a height of $14~\mathrm{m}$ and a slope angle of $39.47^{\circ}$ . A field test was performed on the slope to investigate its structure and composition. According to the field survey results, the slope is mainly composed of stone soil, and the specific physical and mechanical parameters employed in the slope model are shown in Table 8. During the survey, shallow surface collapse and new cracks occurred locally on the left side of the slope, as shown in Fig. 9c–e.

4.2  Generating samples of probability based on the joint probability distribution
It is well known that the physical and mechanical properties of natural soil or rock present variability, which indicates that the soil or rock parameters will be changed so that the prediction results of slope stability will be invalidated. For this question, the probability distribution of soil or rock parameters is utilized to address the uncertainty caused by the variability of soil or rock parameters.
Previous studies have shown that there is a strong statistically negative correlation between c and $\varphi$ and that the correlation structure of $c$ and $\varphi$ is symmetric. Notably, c or $\varphi$ were proven to be lognormal distributions (Rethati 1988; Cherubini 2000). Thus, c and $\varphi$ are dependent, and it is unreasonable to analyze slope stability without considering the relationship between $c$ and $\varphi$ . According to the drilling test, the marginal distributions of $c$ and $\varphi$ were assumed to follow lognormal distributions (mean and standard deviation of 22.4 and 2, respectively, for $c$ ; mean and standard deviation of 28 and 1, respectively, for $\varphi$ ), and the correlation coefficient of $c$ and $\varphi$ was set to − 0.7. 5  Discussion

5.1  Advanced nature of the MSC‑1DCNN
(1) The MSC-1DCNN is a powerful learning tool for establishing a correlation model between the input variable and the output variable. The MSC-1DCNN is employed to predict the state of slope stability with the material parameters and geometry parameters of slopes. The prediction accuracy of a slope stability state is better than some prediction methods (such as SVMs, ELMs, and ANNs), as shown in Table  7, because the important feature is extracted utilizing the MSM and MCM, which extract the shared features by simultaneously processing the slope prediction indices $C,\varphi,E,\gamma,\beta,H,$ and $R_{\mathrm{u}}$ . In conclusion, the proposed method is a state-of-the-art technique. The idea of data amplification originates from the field of image identification (Zhu et al. 2019a), which helps improve the ability of image identification. Considering the difficulty of obtaining historical data, the few data obtained were employed for data amplification based on the slope stability mechanism summarized according to previous research results: When other parameters remain unchanged, the increase in internal friction angle or cohesion will increase the stability of the slope (Li et  al. 2012; Gökceoglu and Aksoy 1996). According to the comparison results shown in Table 7, the evaluation indices of the prediction ability with the amplified data are higher than those without the amplified data.

5.2  Features of the present scheme
Compared to previous studies, the proposed method of slope stability state evaluation based on the MSC-1DCNN has the following features:
The empirical model is used to predict the state of slope stability, which has a lower time cost. In previous studies, the established prediction model was complex with a high time cost (Juang et al. 2013; Peng et al. 2014). In general, the finite element model is designed to create a dataset for establishing a substitution model, but the process is time-consuming. The stability of the case slope described in Sect. 4.1 was calculated in 5 s using GEOstudio based on the limit equilibrium method. The stability of the case slope was calculated in 40 s using ABAQUS based on the strength reduction method. However, the prediction time of the MSC-1DCNN for the slope stability state was only 0.06 s. The prediction times may be $625\mathrm{~s~}$ and 5000 s when the dataset containing 125 samples is created using GEOstudio and ABAQUS, respectively. Moreover, the dataset produced using the simulation tool is not universal, which means that each slope should repeatedly make its dataset. In conclusion, the empirical model with historical empirical data is a low-cost and highly universal model for single-layer slopes.
Fig. 10   Joint probability distributions of $c$ and $\varphi$
(2) In past studies, am empirical model was employed to calculate the state of a slope, but the variability of soil or rock parameters was never considered. The variability of the physical and mechanical properties of soils or rocks has been widely recognized as one of the main sources of uncertainty (Griffiths et al. 2002; Cho 2007; Goh et al. 2019), resulting in the uncertainty of the slope factor of safety being calculated based on poorly known soil or rock parameters (Zhang et al. 2010; Juang et al. 2013). Thus, the variability should be considered in evaluating the stability state of a slope. As shown in Fig. 11, the statistics of the prediction results and the distribution of the stability state of the slope with different $c$ and $\varphi$ are presented.

6  Conclusion
This article proposes the MSC-1DCNN for the stability state of slope prediction based on historical empirical data. The conclusions are summarized as follows:
(a) The MSC-1DCNN has good prediction performance for slope stability prediction. Compared with the ANN, SVM, and ELM, the MSC-1DCNN significantly improves the prediction accuracy. The proposed MSM and MCM have demonstrated their effectiveness for understanding and learning discriminant features of slope prediction indices.   
(b) The empirical model is a universal tool that can predict the single-layer slope. The established empirical model is a low-cost model.   
(c) Data amplification is an effective method for improving the prediction ability of slope stability.   
(d) The variability in soil or rock parameters can be taken into account in the slope state evaluation in the empirical model, which makes the empirical model more meaningful.   
(e) Only homogeneous slopes are analyzed in this paper due to the limitation of case slope data. Slopes with complex multilayer geological conditions should be considered in the future to make the slope safety prediction method more valuable.
