Relationship between HuR and tumor drug resistance

Abstract
Human resistance protein R (HuR), also known as embryonic lethal abnormal visual-like protein (ELAVL1), is an RNAbinding protein widely expressed in vivo that affects the mRNA stability of targeted and is involved in post-transcriptional regulation. Recent studies have shown that HuR is aberrantly expressed in different human cancers and is an essential factor in poor clinical prognosis. The role of HuR in numerous tumors suggests that it could be a new target for tumor therapy and as a marker for efficacy and prognostic assessment. This review focuses on the relationship between HuR and drug resistance in different tumors and briefly describes the structure, function, and inhibitors of HuR. We summarize the mechanisms by which HuR causes tumor resistance and the molecular targets affected.

Introduction
Cancer is the leading cause of death and a significant barrier to increasing life expectancy in all countries. Global Cancer Statistics 2020 finds that the burden of cancer incidence and mortality is rapidly growing worldwide [1]. Drug therapy plays an irreplaceable role in tumor treatment, especially in patients with advanced disease or patients who cannot undergo surgery. However, the emergence of drug resistance has become a vital obstacle that affects the efficacy of treatment [2, 3].
RNA-binding proteins (RBPs), which can bind to specific mRNAs, play physiological or pathological roles, are a family of proteins composed of thousands of proteins. With the development of proteomics and immune technology, many new RBPs and their partners have been discovered [4]. More than 40 RBPs are responsible for nearly all aspects of RNA metabolism, from synthesis to degradation [5]. RBPs and ribonucleoproteins co-regulate RNA processing and posttranscriptional modifications, including RNA maturation, trafficking, stabilization, and translation [6]. The current study shows that RBP functions through a “ripple effect” mainly by binding to cancer-associated RNAs, affecting key events at downstream targets [7]. RBP is involved in multiple processes of tumor development, and changes in its activity mediate tumor proliferation, migration, invasion, and immune escape [8]. Among many RBPs, human antigen protein R (HuR) is one of the RBPs with the most in-depth research and potential in tumor-related therapy [9].
HuR, also known as embryonic lethal abnormal vision like 1 (ELAVL1), encodes a gene located on chromosome 19p13.2 [10]. Although HuR is primarily localized to the nucleus of cells, they can obtain ectopic entry into the cytoplasm when stimulated, where they perform their primary function (stabilizing mRNAs) [11, 12]. The ELAVL family is composed of HuR, HuB, HuC, and HuD. HuR is the only gene ubiquitously expressed in all human tissues, while other members of the ELAVL family are expressed mainly in nerve cells [13]. HuR contains three RNA recognition motifs (RRMs) that facilitate binding to mRNA [14]. HuR is widely expressed in vivo and can target the stability of mRNAs and participate in post-transcriptional modification [15]. HuR is closely related to the occurrence and development of inflammatory diseases, metabolic-related diseases, and cancer [16]. In recent years, studies have found that post-transcriptional regulatory mechanisms play a critical regulatory role in the drug resistance of various tumors. HuR is associated with drug resistance of multiple tumors, such as pancreatic cancer [17], colorectal cancer [18], breast cancer [19], bladder cancer [20], prostate cancer [21], lung cancer, oral cancer [22], ovarian cancer [23], glioma [24], et al. The correlation of HuR drug resistance in many tumors suggests that it has become a new target for tumor therapy, and can be used as a marker for efficacy and prognosis evaluation.

Structural biology of HuR
The specific binding of RBP to conserved sequences in mRNA is mediated by the RNA-binding domain, which is reflected mainly in the three RNA recognition motif (RRM) domains in the molecular structure of HuR, including RRM1, RRM2, and RRM3 (Fig. 1). RBP can bind to adenine and uracil-rich elements (AU-rich elements, ARES) in 3-UTR (untranslated regions, UTR) of mRNA, and mainly binds to the poly A mRNA tail through RRM3, directly or indirectly regulating the stability, translation, subcellular localization, and degradation of the mRNA [25]. ARES is present in various mRNAs, especially transcription factors, cytokines, and lymphokines, which are common determinants of RNA stability. Normally, ARES mainly destabilizes mRNA by accelerating deadenylation, while HuR can increase the stability of mRNA by competing for the binding sites of these ARES. ARES plays a crucial role in cell growth, development, and immune response [26]. Numerous studies have shown that the binding of ARES to mRNA is not random, and HuR can stabilize ARES structures with c-fos, but not C-myc or GM-CSF [27]. The study also showed that HuR translocation is regulated by the nucleocytoplasmic shuttle sequence (HNS), which is located between the second and third RRM domains, and is one of the critical factors that controls HuR cytoplasmic localization [13]. Although HuR is expressed primarily in the nucleus, HNS can regulate the dynamic subcellular localization of HuR. Under the regulation of NHS, HuR can be translocated from the nucleus to the cytoplasm. This nucleocytoplasmic shuttle involves two pathways, and one is the chromosome region maintenance (CRM1)-dependent pathway that involves the protein ligands pp32/PHAPI and APRIL, and another CRM1-independent pathway that requires the participation of the endogenous shuttle signal HNS [28].

Function of HuR
The HuR protein is widely expressed in the body and has a variety of functions. Changes in its level of expression or cellular localization are closely related to the occurrence of some diseases, such as pathological inflammation [29], atherosclerosis [30], tissue insufficiency [31], tumor [32, 33]. The expression of the HuR protein is required for survival in mice, and postnatal deletion of ELAVL1 results in extensive loss of intestinal villi, causing obstructive enteritis, and resulting in mouse death. Additionally, loss of HuR resulted in apoptosis of progenitor cells in the bone marrow, thymus, and gut, resulting in the death of mice [34]. The loss of HuR has also been shown to lead to a lethal phenotype in mice in the second trimester. Studies have shown that HuR also plays an essential role in muscle development [8, 35]. HuR can also regulate inflammatory responses, and activation and cytoplasmic localization of HuR can lead to a robust pro-inflammatory response. HuR can stabilize proinflammatory factors such as IL-6, IL-17, TNF- $\upalpha$ , et  al., thus promoting the inflammatory response [36–38]. HuR can also mediate the occurrence of inflammatory bowel disease by mediating the relative stability of IRF-1 and pIgR mRNA [39]. LPS-induced systemic inflammatory response led to intimal hyperplasia and increased HuR expression in animal models [40]. In addition, $\operatorname{HuR}$ is also involved in the regulation of various renal diseases, such as glomerulonephropathy, renal tubular ischemia–reperfusion injury, renal fibrosis, renal tumors, and chronic renal inflammation [41]. HuR also plays a corresponding role in various liver inflammation and metabolism, including viral hepatitis, alcoholic liver disease, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, liver fibrosis, and liver cancer [42]. Deletion of HuR attenuates left ventricular hypertrophy, dilation, and fibrosis in a mouse model of pressure-load-induced vasculature [43].

In addition, HuR is also involved in the regulation of tumor cell processes, such as the regulation of the cell cycle, apoptosis, cell signaling, stress response, and inflammation [44–46]. The HuR protein can bind to the 3'UTR of MAT2A mRNA, together with MAT1A, regulates the synthesis of SAMe, a critical molecule associated with hepatocyte proliferation, and promotes the dedifferentiation of hepatocytes [47]. Furthermore, HuR silencing also activates caspase3 and reduces the number of cells in the S phase in HepG2 cell lines, suggesting that HuR silencing promotes apoptosis and cell cycle arrest [48]. HuR also interacts with the $3^{\prime}$ -UTR of Fas mRNA, preventing its translation. In contrast, silencing of $\operatorname{HuR}$ increased the expression of Fas and increased the sensitivity of HCC cells to FasL. The $\mathrm{HuR}$ protein could effectively prevent Fas-mediated HCC apoptosis, suggesting that the targeting of HuR can induce apoptosis [49]. HuR also appears to regulate the activation of liver stellate cells by PDGF and $\mathrm{TGF}\upbeta$ [50]. Not only can HuR bind to mRNA but also HuR can bind to LncRNA, increase mRNA stability, and promote its expression. $\mathrm{HuR}$ has been reported to combine with LINC00324, and stabilize FAM83B expression to promote gastric cancer cell proliferation [51]. HuR also promotes the progression of gallbladder cancer by stabilizing LncRNA-HGBC, which regulates the miR-502-3p/SET/AKT axis [52].

HuR and tumor drug resistance
Drugs play an irreplaceable role in cancer treatment. However, the emergence of drug resistance has become the biggest obstacle to treatment. In recent years, HuR has been extensively studied in drug resistance. Related studies have shown that knockdown of the tumor suppressor ARID1A sensitizes tumor cells to cisplatin [53] and that HuR can bind to the distal part of ARID1A 3'-UTR, and this interaction allows HuR to prolong the half-life of ARID1A mRNA and enhances ARID1A expression [54]. HuR can also regulate the level of the chemotherapeutic target TOP2A, thereby regulating the efficacy of doxorubicin [55]. The heterogeneity of glioma represents the greatest challenge in glioma treatment, which increases with tumor grade and resistance to treatment. HuR overexpression stimulates the pro-inflammatory cellular composition of the glioma microenvironment, which favors the development of glioma heterogeneity and resistance to treatment [56]. The $\mathrm{HuR}/$ lincRNA- $\cdot\mathsf{p}^{\k_{21/\upbeta}}$ -catenin pathway was also found to attenuate the stemness and radioresistance of glioma stem cells [57]. Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) exhibits resistance to chemotherapy and is not responsive to apoptotic stimuli, and $\mathrm{HuR}$ blocks the translation of Fas mRNA and effectively prevents Fas-mediated apoptosis in HCC cells [49]. Additionally, HuR knockdown reduced DNA repair proteins and inhibited intracellular thioredoxin reductase, thus improving the radiosensitivity of TNBC cells in vitro [58]. Long-term treatment with abemaciclib (a CDK4/6 inhibitor) confers resistance to gemcitabine and 5-FU, but inhibition of HuR increases its sensitivity [59]. In addition, a novel tumor angiogenesis inhibitor, cryptotanshinone, can destabilize TNF- $\upalpha$ mRNA and reduce drug resistance of tumor cells by reducing HuR nucleocytoplasmic transfer [60]. Numerous studies have clearly shown that the HuR differential expression is associated with tumor chemoresistance (Table 1, Fig. 2).
HuR-mediated drug resistance pathways generally occur in three ways: HuR regulates the expression of ABC transporters in tumors; HuR regulates the expression of apoptosis proteins; HuR regulates the expression of cyclins and DNA damage repair proteins (Fig. 3). Of course, tumor drug resistance mechanisms are complex and diverse, and HuR can also regulate drug resistance through other signaling pathways (Table 1).

HuR inhibitors

Inhibit HuR expression
Many studies have been conducted to reduce drug resistance in tumors by finding ways to regulate the expression of HuR. The most effective approach at present is the development and application of HuR siRNAs (Fig. 4) [32, 99]. Muralidharan et al. developed nanoparticle siRNA targeting HuR and tested its efficacy in H1299 and CCD16 cells. Observed differences in proliferation viability between H1299 and CCD16 cells treated with HuR siRNA and control cells, and HuR expression was significantly decreased in cells treated with siRNA. The indicated HuR siRNA could regulate HuR expression in tumor cells and inhibit tumor cell growth [100]. Recently, the nanoparticle HuR siRNA has also been treated in melanoma cells and has achieved specific curative effects [101]. siHuR significantly enhanced epirubicinmediated apoptosis by increasing reactive oxygen species, thereby promoting the cytotoxic effect of epirubicin [74].

Inhibit cytoplasmic relocalization
The post-transcriptional regulation mechanism mediated by HuR is closely related to the drug resistance of tumors. Some small molecules can directly target HuR and inhibit cytoplasmic relocation of HuR [102, 103]. Studies have shown that MS-444 inhibits HuR cytoplasmic translocation in human glioblastoma cells and colorectal cancer cells (Fig. 4) [103–105]. The primary mechanism is that MS-444 disrupts HuR cytoplasmic trafficking and releases bound mRNA. Overexpression of HuR increases TRAIL resistance in pancreatic cancer cells, and pretreatment of pancreatic cancer cells with the HuR small molecule inhibitor MS-444 (Novartis) significantly improved TRAIL sensitivity [92].
It has also been shown that HuR-mediated PIM1 regulates phosphorylation and inactivation of BAD and protects cells from hypoxic stress through activation of MEK1/2. At the same time, MS-444 inhibits HuR cytoplasmic translocation, abolishes hypoxia-induced PIM1 overexpression, and significantly improves the sensitivity of pancreatic cancer cells to oxaliplatin and 5-FU under hypoxic conditions [93]. Another study showed that treatment of Hep3B cells with N-benzylcantharidinamide reduced MMP-9 mRNA stability, which was also achieved by inhibiting HuR cytoplasmic relocation [106]. Similarly, cytoskeletal inhibitors latrunculin A and blebbistatin dose and time dependently reduce the cytoplasmic expression of HuR in HepG2 and Huh7 cell lines [107, 108]. FDA-approved pyrvinium pamoate, an anthelmintic drug that inhibits HuR cytoplasmic accumulation, is used in combination with chemotherapy drugs such as cisplatin, doxorubicin, vincristine, and oxaliplatin to enhance chemotherapeutic effects on tumor cell cytotoxicity [20].

Inhibits the ability to stabilize targeted mRNAs
mRNA HuR can stabilize mRNA, thereby promoting the function of the corresponding mRNA, which is also a director of HuR treatment. CMLD-2, a novel compound that competes with HuR for binding, disrupts its specific binding, and stabilizes mRNA (Fig. 4) [109]. In the study by Lorenzo et al., MAD2 was one of the targets of $\operatorname{HuR}$ and MAD2 protein levels decreased significantly after treatment with CMLD-2 [110]. Treatment of non-small cell lung cancer cells with CMLD-2 produced dose-dependent cytotoxicity, which reduced HuR mRNA and HuR regulatory protein (Bcl2 and p27) mRNA in tumor cells, causing cell cycle arrest in the G1 phase and induced apoptosis [111]. KH-3, a small-molecule compound, can lead to breast cancer invasion by inhibiting the interaction of HuR with FOXQ1 mRNA [112]. Another study showed that quercetin, as a monomer of traditional Chinese medicine, can destroy the stability of the binding of the HuR protein to mRNA [36, 42, 113]. Such methods of competitively inhibiting the binding of HuR to target proteins also provide new ideas for developing new anticancer drugs.

Conclusion
$\mathrm{HuR}$ is a crucial regulator of tumor progression. In this review, we focus mainly on the impact of $\mathrm{HuR}$ in the development of drug resistance in cancer. We found that HuR regulates the stability of numerous cancer-related mRNAs and mediates drug resistance in multiple tumors through post-transcriptional regulatory mechanisms. By analyzing the resistance mechanism of $\operatorname{HuR}$ to different drugs and the regulation mechanism of $\mathrm{HuR}$ , it can provide new ideas for the drug resistance treatment of tumors. Although there have been no FDA-approved HuR inhibitors so far, the combination of HuR inhibitors with drugs may be more beneficial to tumor treatment.
